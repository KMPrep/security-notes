AI-BOM, Model Cards, and Data Cards

Domain: AI Supply-Chain Security / AI Governance
Frameworks: OWASP LLM03 (Supply Chain), NIST AI RMF (Map / Govern), SPDX 3.0 AI Profile, CycloneDX ML-BOM, EU AI Act Art. 11/13

1. Overview

An AI Bill of Materials (AI-BOM) is a machine-readable inventory of every component that makes up an AI system: base and fine-tuned models, training and evaluation datasets, embeddings, framework and library dependencies, hardware/accelerator targets, and their provenance and licenses. It sits across the whole ML lifecycle — data, training, model artifact, and inference/serving — and is the supply-chain artifact that lets you answer "what is actually inside this system and where did it come from." Model cards and data cards are the human-readable governance companions: a model card documents a model's intended use, evaluation results, limitations, and safety characteristics, while a data card documents a dataset's collection, composition, consent/licensing, and known biases. This matters because a modern AI system pulls thousands of transitive dependencies plus third-party weights from hubs like Hugging Face, and without an inventory you cannot do vulnerability response, license compliance, or provenance verification. The topic maps to OWASP LLM03: Supply Chain, is a core input to the Map and Govern functions of NIST AI RMF, and aligns with Google SAIF provenance/inventory controls and emerging SPDX AI Profile and CycloneDX ML-BOM standards.

2. How It Works

2.1 Core Mechanics

An AI-BOM extends the classic SBOM concept. A traditional SBOM (SPDX or CycloneDX) enumerates software packages, versions, hashes, and licenses so you can map a new CVE to affected systems. An AI-BOM adds the ML-specific components a code SBOM never captures:

  • Model artifacts — base model, fine-tunes, adapters (LoRA), quantized variants, their format (safetensors vs pickle vs GGUF), weights hash, and the hub/registry they came from.
  • Datasets — training, fine-tuning, RLHF, and evaluation sets, with source, license, consent basis, and version/snapshot.
  • Model metadata — architecture, parameter count, training compute, hyperparameters, and the framework version used to train.
  • Pipeline dependencies — the usual software packages (transformers, torch, CUDA), plus data-processing and serving code.
  • Provenance and signatures — who produced each artifact, build attestations, and signing material.

The trust boundaries are the seams where a third party's artifact enters your system: pulling weights from a public hub, ingesting a scraped dataset, or installing a pip package. What crosses those boundaries — weights, data, code — is exactly what the AI-BOM must record so that when one of those upstream components is later found malicious or vulnerable, you can trace impact. CycloneDX 1.5+ adds an ML-BOM extension with modelCard and data component types; SPDX 3.0 defines an AI and Dataset profile. Model and data cards attach the risk transparency layer on top of the inventory: they turn a raw component list into something a risk reviewer, auditor, or incident responder can reason about.

Top 3 Critical Mitigations

1. Generate a Signed AI-BOM in CI and Gate On It

The foundational control is to produce the AI-BOM automatically at build time using a standard format (CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX 3.0 AI Profile) so it always reflects the deployed artifact, then sign it and store it in a central, queryable store. The BOM records each component's hash, version, license, format, and provenance. When a new CVE or a malicious-model advisory lands, you query the store by package or weights hash and instantly get the list of affected systems — turning incident response from days of manual archaeology into a single query. The trade-off is that a BOM is only as good as its coverage and freshness: a hand-maintained or stale BOM gives false assurance, so generation must be automated and the document itself must be tamper-evident (signed, immutable storage).

2. Provenance and Signature Verification on Ingestion

Every artifact that crosses a trust boundary — third-party weights, datasets, packages — should be pinned by cryptographic hash and verified against a signature or build attestation before it enters the pipeline. Tools like sigstore/cosign for model signing, in-toto/SLSA attestations, and hash-pinned pulls prevent typosquatting and swapped-artifact attacks and let you prove a model is the one the model card describes. This directly closes the biggest AI-supply-chain gap: unverified weights from public hubs. The limitation is that signing proves origin and integrity, not safety — a signed model can still be backdoored — so provenance must be paired with model scanning and evaluation, and it depends on an ecosystem where publishers actually sign.

3. Model Cards and Data Cards Bound to the Artifact

Model and data cards provide the risk-transparency layer: intended use, eval metrics, known limitations, safety testing, dataset composition, consent basis, and licenses. Their security value comes from binding the card to the exact signed artifact (same version, same hash) so risk reviewers and auditors reason about what is actually deployed, not an aspirational description. This is what makes governance and compliance (EU AI Act technical documentation, NIST AI RMF Govern) auditable, and it feeds incident response by documenting a model's data lineage. The trade-off is that cards are self-reported and can be incomplete, optimistic, or stale — they document but do not enforce, so they must be reviewed and version-locked, never trusted blindly (which itself is an LLM09 Overreliance risk).

5. Practice Questions

Tier 1 — Fundamentals

  1. What is an AI-BOM and how does it differ from a classic SBOM? An AI-BOM is a machine-readable inventory of an AI system's components — models, datasets, embeddings, and software dependencies — with provenance and licenses. A classic SBOM only covers software packages; an AI-BOM adds model artifacts and datasets, which are the ML-specific supply-chain risks. This is asked to confirm you understand the AI-specific expansion of supply-chain hygiene.
  2. What is a model card? A human-readable document describing a model's intended use, evaluation results, limitations, training data summary, and safety characteristics. This is asked to test governance literacy.
  3. What is a data card and why does it matter for security? It documents a dataset's source, composition, consent/licensing, and known biases, which supports provenance, privacy compliance, and poisoning-risk assessment. This is asked to check that you connect data lineage to risk.
  4. Which OWASP LLM risk covers this area? LLM03: Supply Chain, covering vulnerable dependencies, malicious models, and poisoned data. This is asked to anchor you to the standard framework.
  5. Name two SBOM standards and one AI extension. SPDX and CycloneDX are the SBOM standards; CycloneDX ML-BOM and the SPDX 3.0 AI Profile extend them for ML. This is asked to confirm standards awareness.

Tier 2 — System Design and Trade-offs

  1. Design a pipeline that produces and consumes an AI-BOM for a fleet of models. Generate the BOM automatically in CI in CycloneDX ML-BOM format, recording each component's hash, version, license, format, and provenance; sign the BOM; push it to a central queryable store keyed by hash; and wire security response tooling to query that store on any new advisory. This is asked to see if you can operationalize inventory rather than just define it.
  2. A new CVE drops in a common ML library. Walk me through response using the AI-BOM. Query the BOM store for the affected package and version across all systems, produce the impacted-system list, assess reachability and exploitability, patch or rotate, then regenerate and re-sign the BOM to confirm remediation. This is asked to test whether the BOM actually shortens the response loop.
  3. How would you catch a malicious pickled model before it reaches production? Record artifact format in the BOM, block pickle-based formats in favor of safetensors, scan and sandbox any model load, and verify provenance/signature on ingestion. This is asked to test the concrete top AI-supply-chain RCE vector.
  4. How do you keep an AI-BOM from becoming stale and giving false assurance? Generate it automatically on every build so it tracks the deployed artifact, tie it to the immutable artifact hash, sign it, and reject deployments without a current matching BOM. This is asked because a stale inventory is worse than none.
  5. How do model and data cards feed compliance obligations like the EU AI Act? They supply the required technical documentation — intended purpose, data governance, evaluation, and limitations — bound to the specific deployed model, making the system auditable. This is asked to test regulatory-to-engineering translation.

Tier 3 — Expert and Deep-Dive

  1. Signing proves integrity and origin, not safety. How do you close the remaining gap? A signed model can still be backdoored, so provenance verification must be layered with model scanning, behavioral evaluation, and trigger/backdoor testing before trust is granted. The BOM records that these gates ran, and the model card documents the results, so the assurance comes from the combination, not the signature alone. This is asked to test that you do not overtrust cryptographic provenance.
  2. How would you represent a fine-tune-of-a-fine-tune lineage in an AI-BOM, and why does depth matter? Model each artifact as a component with a dependency edge to its parent (base to fine-tune to adapter), carrying each layer's hash, training data reference, and provenance, so the graph captures full lineage. Depth matters because a compromise or license restriction three levels upstream still propagates, and only a lineage graph lets you trace it. This is asked to probe transitive-provenance reasoning.
  3. A model card claims safety evals that the deployed weights never actually passed. How do you prevent this class of drift? Bind the card to the artifact by hash and version, generate card fields from the actual eval pipeline output rather than hand-editing, and sign card and artifact together so a mismatch is detectable. Treating the card as self-reported prose without binding is an LLM09 Overreliance failure. This is asked to test card integrity, not just card existence.
  4. How do you scale AI-BOM inventory across thousands of models pulled from public hubs? Enforce hash-pinned, signature-verified ingestion at a single choke point, auto-generate a BOM entry per artifact at that gate, store in a graph/queryable index keyed by hash, and continuously reconcile deployed artifacts against the store to detect drift. This is asked to test fleet-scale supply-chain design.
  5. What can an AI-BOM not solve, and where are its limits? It is an inventory and provenance record — it does not by itself detect a backdoor, prove a dataset is unpoisoned, or make a self-reported card truthful; it only tells you what is present and where it came from. Its value depends on coverage, freshness, tamper-evidence, and pairing with scanning and evaluation controls. This is asked to separate inventory from enforcement and gauge senior judgment.

6. Key Takeaways

  • An AI-BOM = SBOM plus models, datasets, embeddings, and their provenance; it maps to OWASP LLM03 and NIST AI RMF Map/Govern.
  • Use standard formats — CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX 3.0 AI Profile — and generate the BOM automatically in CI, then sign it.
  • Model cards document intended use, evals, and limits; data cards document source, consent, and bias — both must be bound to the signed artifact.
  • The killer AI-supply-chain vector is malicious pickled weights giving RCE on load; prefer safetensors and scan/sandbox loads.
  • Provenance and signatures (sigstore/cosign, SLSA/in-toto) prove origin and integrity but not safety — layer scanning and evaluation on top.
  • The BOM's payoff is incident response: query by hash to map a CVE or bad-model advisory to affected systems in seconds, not days.
  • How to defend in production: auto-generate and sign an AI-BOM in CI, verify provenance on ingestion, bind version-locked model/data cards to each artifact, and keep the inventory queryable for supply-chain response.
