Privacy and PII Protection in AI Systems

Category: AI Security, Defenses, Data Protection
Frameworks: OWASP LLM02 (Sensitive Information Disclosure), NIST AI RMF (Map / Measure / Manage), Google SAIF, MITRE ATLAS (Exfiltration, ML Attack Staging)
Related repo notes: dlp/Content-Inspection, dlp/Data-Classification, dlp/Policy-Design

1. Overview

Privacy and PII protection in AI systems is the discipline of keeping personal data out of places it should never be, across the whole lifecycle: training corpora, fine-tuning sets, retrieval indexes, prompts, model weights, and logs. It matters because an LLM is a lossy compressor of its training data that can memorize and later regurgitate rare, sensitive records, and because production pipelines routinely log raw prompts that contain user PII. This sits primarily at the data layer (what you collect and train on) and the inference layer (what enters prompts and leaves in logs), and it spans the full trust boundary between "user-supplied data" and "durable system state." Frameworks classify it under OWASP LLM02 Sensitive Information Disclosure, the NIST AI RMF governance functions, and Google SAIF data-governance controls; the extraction techniques map to MITRE ATLAS exfiltration and membership-inference tactics. The defining challenge is that privacy is a property of the entire pipeline — a single unredacted log sink or one memorized record undoes upstream controls.

2. How It Works

2.1 Core Mechanics

Privacy engineering for AI attacks the problem at four control points, each a trust boundary where personal data can leak.

Data collection and minimization. The cheapest privacy control is not collecting the data. Purpose limitation (collect only for a declared, lawful purpose) and data minimization (collect only what that purpose needs) shrink the blast radius before any model touches the data. This is where GDPR's principles bite hardest: Article 5 mandates minimization, purpose limitation, and storage limitation.

Pre-training / pre-fine-tuning redaction. Before data enters a training set, a PII detection and redaction pass runs: regex for structured identifiers (SSN, card numbers with Luhn validation, API keys), NER/ML classifiers for free-text entities (names, addresses, medical terms), and de-identification transforms (masking, tokenization, format-preserving pseudonymization). This is conceptually identical to DLP content inspection — same detectors, different objective: DLP blocks egress, here we scrub the training corpus so the model never learns the secret.

Training-time privacy. Redaction is best-effort and misses novel formats. To bound what a model can leak even about data it did see, differential privacy (DP-SGD) adds calibrated noise to gradients and clips per-example gradient norms, giving a provable (epsilon, delta) bound on how much any single training example influences the final weights. Federated learning keeps raw data on-device and only shares model updates, moving the trust boundary to the client — but updates themselves can leak, so it is usually combined with DP and secure aggregation.

Inference-time handling. At runtime the model sees user prompts (which contain PII), retrieves documents (which may contain other users' PII), and emits responses that get logged. Controls here are input redaction before the prompt reaches a third-party model, output scanning to catch regurgitated PII, retrieval access controls so a user cannot retrieve records they are not authorized to see, and log scrubbing so the observability stack does not become a shadow PII database.

The recurring failure is memorization: neural networks over-fit rare, high-entropy sequences (a specific phone number appearing once), and an attacker with only API access can extract them via crafted prompts (the "extractable memorization" attacks against production LLMs). Deduplication of training data is one of the strongest empirical mitigations — memorization scales with how many times a sequence is duplicated.

Top 3 Critical Mitigations

1. Layered PII Detection and Redaction (pre-training and pre-prompt)

The same detector stack the DLP world uses — regex/validators for structured identifiers, NER and ML classifiers for free-text entities, and dictionary/checksum passes — runs at two boundaries: scrubbing the training corpus so secrets are never learned, and scrubbing prompts at an inference gateway before they reach an external model. Redaction can be irreversible (mask, drop) or reversible (tokenize against a vault, or format-preserving pseudonymization) when the downstream flow must later re-hydrate the value. The hard limitation is that detection is probabilistic: a novel PII format, a name that is also a common word, or PII split across fields will slip through, so redaction is a risk-reduction control, not a guarantee. In practice you pair it with deduplication (which cuts memorization more than any single detector) and treat any single-layer redaction claim with suspicion. This is the direct conceptual sibling of the repo's DLP content-inspection notes — same engines, applied to model inputs instead of egress channels.

2. Differential Privacy in Training (DP-SGD)

Differential privacy gives a provable bound on individual influence: DP-SGD clips each example's gradient to a fixed norm and adds Gaussian noise, so the trained weights satisfy an (epsilon, delta) guarantee that the presence or absence of any one record changes the output distribution by at most a factor of e^epsilon. A small epsilon (say 1–3) is strongly private; a large epsilon (10+) offers weak, mostly-theatrical protection. The trade-off is stark: lower epsilon means more noise, which hurts model utility, disproportionately harms accuracy on under-represented groups and rare-but-legitimate patterns, and raises training cost. DP is the only mitigation on this list that defeats membership inference by construction rather than by heuristic, which is why it anchors any defensible claim that a model "does not memorize" specific individuals. Contrast with k-anonymity, which only guarantees each record is indistinguishable among k others on quasi-identifiers and provably fails under linkage/auxiliary-data attacks and high-dimensional data — DP composes and degrades gracefully where k-anonymity does not.

3. Data Minimization, Retention, and Purpose Limitation (governance)

The controls that never appear in a model card are often the most effective: don't collect it, don't keep it, don't reuse it. Purpose limitation tags data at ingest with the lawful basis and use it was collected for, and blocks reuse for model training unless that purpose was declared — mapping directly to GDPR Article 5 and the data-subject's expectations. Retention TTLs plus automated deletion shrink the breach surface and satisfy storage limitation; short log retention specifically prevents the observability stack from silently becoming the largest PII store in the company. The limitation is organizational, not technical: minimization fights the ML instinct to hoard data "in case it's useful," and honoring erasure requests (GDPR Art. 17) is genuinely hard once PII is baked into weights — which is precisely why keeping raw PII out of the training set (so deletion means dropping a row and re-indexing, not re-training a foundation model) is the pragmatic answer. Machine unlearning exists but is immature; architecture that avoids the problem beats unlearning that tries to reverse it.

5. Practice Questions

Tier 1 — Fundamentals

  1. What is PII and why is it different from other data in an AI pipeline? PII is any data that identifies a natural person directly or in combination with other data; it is special because it carries legal obligations (GDPR, CCPA) and because a model can memorize and later emit it. This is asked to confirm you can define the asset you are protecting.
  2. What is data minimization? Collecting and retaining only the personal data strictly necessary for a declared purpose, and nothing more. This is asked to check you know the cheapest privacy control is not collecting the data.
  3. Where should PII redaction happen in an LLM app? At two boundaries: before data enters the training/fine-tuning corpus, and before user prompts reach the model or the logs. This is asked to test whether you see privacy as a pipeline property, not a single filter.
  4. What is model memorization and why is it a privacy risk? Models over-fit rare, high-entropy training sequences and can regurgitate them verbatim when prompted, leaking the underlying PII. This is asked to test the core AI-specific privacy failure.
  5. Which OWASP LLM risk covers PII leakage? LLM02, Sensitive Information Disclosure. This is asked to anchor you to the standard vocabulary.

Tier 2 — System Design and Trade-offs

  1. Design a redaction proxy for a customer support chatbot that calls a third-party LLM. Put an inference gateway in front of the model: run layered detection (regex + validators for structured IDs, NER for free text), tokenize reversibly against a vault where the answer must reference the value, mask irreversibly otherwise, forward only the sanitized prompt, then re-hydrate tokens in the response and scrub the transcript before logging. Fail closed on detector timeout for high-risk fields. This is asked to test end-to-end boundary reasoning, not just "add a regex."
  2. When would you use differential privacy versus just redacting the training data? Redaction handles known PII formats cheaply but misses novel ones and gives no formal guarantee; DP bounds any single record's influence provably, defeating membership inference by construction, at a real utility and cost penalty. Use redaction plus deduplication as the baseline and add DP when you need a defensible mathematical claim or train on inherently sensitive data. This is asked to test whether you know heuristic vs provable controls.
  3. How do you handle a GDPR erasure request against a trained model? If PII was kept out of weights, deletion is dropping the source row and re-indexing retrieval; if it is baked into a foundation model, options are costly re-training, machine unlearning (immature), or documenting that de-identified/DP-trained weights do not constitute retained personal data. This is asked to see if you designed for deletion up front rather than facing it as an emergency.
  4. What are the privacy trade-offs of federated learning? It keeps raw data on-device, moving the trust boundary to the client and reducing central collection — but shared gradients/updates can leak samples, so it needs secure aggregation and client-side DP, and it complicates debugging, poisoning defense, and consistent redaction. This is asked to test that you see federation as a trade, not a free win.
  5. How do you stop your logging stack from becoming a PII breach? Scrub-on-write with field-level redaction, short retention TTLs, restricted access to raw-transcript stores, and treating logs as in-scope for the same DLP policy as production data. This is asked because unscrubbed logs are the most common real-world LLM PII leak.

Tier 3 — Expert and Deep-Dive

  1. Explain what epsilon means in differential privacy and how you would choose it. Epsilon bounds the multiplicative change (e^epsilon) in the output distribution caused by adding or removing one record; smaller is more private. You choose it by balancing a defensible privacy budget (single-digit epsilon for meaningful protection) against utility loss and disparate impact on minority subgroups, accounting for composition across training runs and queries. This is asked to separate people who invoke "DP" as a buzzword from those who can reason about the budget.
  2. Why is k-anonymity insufficient for high-dimensional or linkable data? k-anonymity only hides a record among k others on chosen quasi-identifiers and provably breaks under linkage with auxiliary datasets, homogeneity attacks, and the curse of dimensionality where every record becomes unique. DP composes and degrades gracefully instead. This is asked to test depth on why the industry moved from anonymization to formal privacy.
  3. An attacker has only API access and wants to extract training PII. Walk through the attack and your defenses. They probe with prefixes and high-signal prompts to elicit memorized completions, or run membership-inference by comparing model confidence on candidate records. Defenses: deduplicate and redact the corpus, train with DP, filter output for PII, rate-limit and detect extraction-shaped query patterns, and avoid exposing raw logits/confidence. This is asked to test adversarial thinking against a black-box model.
  4. Can embeddings in a vector store leak PII, and how do you contain it? Yes — embeddings are often invertible enough to reconstruct source text, so a compromised or over-shared vector store leaks the underlying PII. Contain it by keeping raw PII out of embedded text, encrypting the store, enforcing per-user access control on retrieval, and treating vectors as sensitive data (OWASP LLM08). This is asked to probe the less-obvious retrieval-layer surface.
  5. How do you prove to an auditor that your model does not leak individual PII? Combine evidence: DP training with a stated epsilon and composition accounting, documented deduplication and redaction coverage, memorization/extraction red-team results, output-filter metrics, and data-flow/retention records tied to purpose tags. No single artifact suffices — you present a defense-in-depth argument with a provable core (DP) and measured heuristic layers. This is asked to test whether you can turn privacy engineering into an assurance narrative for governance.

6. Key Takeaways

  • Privacy is a pipeline property: training corpus, prompts, retrieval index, weights, and logs are all leak points — one unscrubbed log sink defeats upstream redaction.
  • The cheapest control is minimization — data you never collected or retained cannot leak or trigger an erasure problem.
  • Memorization is the AI-specific risk; deduplication of training data is the single most effective empirical mitigation, and it maps to OWASP LLM02.
  • Redaction is probabilistic (same detectors as DLP content inspection); differential privacy is the only control that bounds individual leakage provably, at a utility cost tuned by epsilon.
  • k-anonymity fails under linkage and high dimensionality; prefer DP for formal guarantees.
  • Design for GDPR data-subject rights up front — keep PII out of weights so erasure is a row delete, not a re-train.
  • How I would defend this in production: redact and deduplicate the corpus, DP-train when the data is sensitive, put a redaction-and-output-scan gateway in front of the model, enforce per-user retrieval ACLs, and scrub logs on write with short TTLs.
