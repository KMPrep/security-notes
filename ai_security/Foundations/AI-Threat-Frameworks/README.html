AI Security Threat Frameworks — OWASP LLM Top 10, MITRE ATLAS, Google SAIF, NIST AI RMF

Domain: AI / ML / LLM Security — Foundations
Frameworks: OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications (2025), MITRE ATLAS, Google SAIF, NIST AI RMF (AI 100-1)

1. Overview

There is no single canonical model for AI security, so senior engineers are expected to know four complementary frameworks and when to reach for each. OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications is an application-layer risk catalog for people building LLM features. MITRE ATLAS is an adversary-behavior knowledge base (an ATT&CK for ML) that names tactics and techniques attackers use across the ML lifecycle. Google SAIF is a practitioner-oriented control framework with six core elements for securing AI systems end to end. NIST AI RMF is a governance and risk-management framework organized around four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage). They sit at different altitudes: OWASP and ATLAS describe what goes wrong (vulnerabilities and attacker techniques), while SAIF and NIST AI RMF describe how you govern and control it — and a strong answer uses one from each pair.

2. How It Works

2.1 Core Mechanics

The four frameworks answer different questions, and their value is in the seams between them.

OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications targets developers and AppSec teams building on LLMs. The 2025 list is:

  • LLM01 Prompt Injection — untrusted input overrides intended instructions (direct and indirect).
  • LLM02 Sensitive Information Disclosure — model leaks PII, secrets, or proprietary data.
  • LLM03 Supply Chain — poisoned models, datasets, adapters, or dependencies.
  • LLM04 Data and Model Poisoning — tampering with training or fine-tune data to implant bias or backdoors.
  • LLM05 Improper Output Handling — downstream systems trust model output, enabling XSS, SSRF, SQLi, or code exec.
  • LLM06 Excessive Agency — the agent has too much permission, autonomy, or functionality.
  • LLM07 System Prompt Leakage — secrets or trust assumptions embedded in the system prompt are exposed.
  • LLM08 Vector and Embedding Weaknesses — RAG/embedding attacks such as poisoning, inversion, and cross-tenant leakage.
  • LLM09 Misinformation — hallucination and overreliance producing false or unsafe output.
  • LLM10 Unbounded Consumption — denial of wallet/service via cost, token, or resource exhaustion.

MITRE ATLAS models the adversary across the ML lifecycle using ATT&CK-style tactics. Tactics include Reconnaissance, Resource Development, Initial Access, ML Model Access, Execution, Persistence, Defense Evasion, Discovery, Collection, ML Attack Staging, Exfiltration, and Impact. Representative techniques: LLM Prompt Injection, LLM Jailbreak, Evade ML Model (adversarial evasion), Poison Training Data, Backdoor ML Model, Exfiltration via ML Inference API (model extraction / membership inference), and ML Supply Chain Compromise. ATLAS is backed by real-world case studies, which makes it the framework to cite when a reviewer asks "how would an attacker actually do this."

Google SAIF (Secure AI Framework) defines six core elements: (1) expand strong security foundations to the AI ecosystem; (2) extend detection and response to bring AI into the org's threat universe; (3) automate defenses to keep pace with existing and new threats; (4) harmonize platform-level controls for consistency across the org; (5) adapt controls to adjust mitigations and create faster feedback loops (red-team, test); (6) contextualize AI system risks in surrounding business processes. SAIF also ships a risk map across four areas — data, infrastructure, model, application — which is why it maps cleanly onto both OWASP and ATLAS.

NIST AI RMF is a voluntary governance framework built on four functions: Govern (culture, policy, accountability, roles — the cross-cutting function), Map (establish context and identify risks for the specific AI use case), Measure (assess, analyze, and track risks with metrics and testing), and Manage (prioritize and act on risks, allocate resources, monitor). It also defines characteristics of trustworthy AI (valid and reliable, safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable, privacy-enhanced, fair). NIST is the framework you cite for program-level structure, not for a specific exploit.

The key mental model: ATLAS and OWASP are the "attack" pair; SAIF and NIST are the "defense/governance" pair. OWASP is per-application, ATLAS is per-adversary-behavior, SAIF is per-control, NIST is per-risk-lifecycle.

3. What Can Go Wrong

Framing here is deliberately "gaps if you rely on only one framework" — the failure mode is picking a single lens and missing what the others cover.

1 No attacker technique detail OWASP alone Risk catalog, not adversary TTPs Weak detection engineering, no threat intel mapping High
2 No app-build checklist ATLAS alone Behavior knowledge base, not dev guidance Teams cannot operationalize into code reviews High
3 No governance or accountability OWASP or ATLAS alone Both are technical, not organizational No ownership, policy, or audit trail High
4 No concrete exploit examples NIST AI RMF alone Deliberately technology-neutral Program looks complete but controls untested Med
5 Training and data-pipeline attacks underweighted OWASP-only view of a chat app LLM Top 10 focuses on app layer Poisoning and supply chain missed Med

8 No measurement or metrics SAIF alone without NIST SAIF is control-oriented, lighter on measurement rigor Cannot prove risk reduction over time Med

10 Business-context risk missed Pure technical frameworks Impact depends on surrounding process Right control, wrong priority Low

4. Mitigation Controls

The mitigation here is a layered framework strategy: use each framework for what it does best rather than forcing one to cover everything.

Top 3 Critical Mitigations

1. Use OWASP and ATLAS Together for Threat Modeling

For any LLM or ML system, enumerate application risks with the OWASP LLM Top 10, then for each relevant risk pull the corresponding ATLAS technique to describe how an attacker executes it and how you would detect it. OWASP tells you LLM01 Prompt Injection is a risk; ATLAS tells you it maps to the LLM Prompt Injection technique under specific tactics with real case studies you can cite. The trade-off is effort: doing both is slower than a checklist, but it is the difference between a paper risk register and a testable, detectable threat model. This pairing is also what lets you feed AI risks into existing SOC detection engineering rather than treating AI as a silo.

2. Wrap Technical Frameworks in NIST AI RMF Governance

Technical frameworks list risks and techniques but do not assign ownership, cadence, or acceptance criteria. NIST AI RMF's Govern function establishes roles, policy, and accountability as a cross-cutting concern, while Map, Measure, Manage give you a repeatable loop: identify context and risks, quantify them, then prioritize and act. The trade-off is bureaucracy — RMF can become box-checking if it is not tied back to concrete OWASP/ATLAS findings — so the discipline is to make every Govern/Manage decision reference a specific technical risk. This is the layer auditors, regulators, and leadership care about.

3. Apply SAIF's Six Elements as the Control Backbone

SAIF is the most implementation-ready of the four for control selection: extend existing security foundations to AI, bring AI into detection and response, automate defenses, harmonize platform controls, adapt via red-teaming and feedback loops, and contextualize risk in business process. Its power in a design review is that it explicitly says "reuse your existing security program" rather than reinventing everything for AI. The limitation is that SAIF is lighter on measurement rigor than NIST, which is exactly why the two are paired — SAIF for controls, NIST for the metrics and governance wrapper. Google also publishes the SAIF Risk Self-Assessment to operationalize it.

5. Practice Questions

Tier 1 — Fundamentals (screens out ~60%)

  1. What is the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications? It is an application-security risk catalog for LLM-based systems, covering risks LLM01 through LLM10 such as prompt injection, sensitive information disclosure, supply chain, and excessive agency. This is asked to confirm you know the baseline vocabulary of LLM AppSec.
  2. What is MITRE ATLAS and how does it relate to ATT&CK? ATLAS is an ATT&CK-style knowledge base of adversarial tactics and techniques against ML systems, backed by real case studies. This is asked to check that you think in adversary behaviors, not just vulnerabilities.
  3. What are the four functions of the NIST AI RMF? Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage, with Govern as the cross-cutting function for culture and accountability. This is asked to test governance literacy.
  4. What is Google SAIF in one sentence? A six-element framework for securing AI systems by extending existing security foundations, detection, automation, platform controls, red-teaming, and business context. This is asked to see if you can frame AI security as a control program.
  5. Give an example OWASP LLM ID and what it covers. LLM06 Excessive Agency covers an agent having too much permission, autonomy, or functionality, leading to unintended actions via tool use. This is asked to confirm the IDs are more than a memorized list.

Tier 2 — System Design and Trade-offs (screens out ~90%)

  1. How do these four frameworks fit together — which do you use when? OWASP and ATLAS are the attack pair (app risks and adversary techniques), while SAIF and NIST are the defense/governance pair (controls and risk lifecycle). I use OWASP to drive code review, ATLAS to describe and detect attacker behavior, SAIF to select controls, and NIST to govern and measure. The frameworks operate at different altitudes, so relying on one leaves a blind spot; the design goal is coverage across build, attack, control, and govern. In practice I structure the answer by naming one from each pair for whatever specific risk is on the table.
  2. You are threat-modeling a customer-facing RAG chatbot. Which frameworks structure your analysis and how? I start with OWASP to enumerate app risks — LLM01 prompt injection (including indirect injection via retrieved docs), LLM08 vector and embedding weaknesses, LLM02 disclosure, LLM05 improper output handling. For each, I map to ATLAS techniques like LLM Prompt Injection and Exfiltration via ML Inference API to describe attacker execution and detection. I then select controls via SAIF (input/output guardrails, least-privilege retrieval, tenancy isolation) and wrap it in NIST Map/Measure/Manage to prioritize, test, and monitor. That gives a threat model that is both testable and governable.
  3. What does NIST AI RMF give you that OWASP and ATLAS do not? Governance, accountability, and a measurement lifecycle. OWASP and ATLAS tell you what can go wrong and how attackers do it, but neither assigns ownership, defines acceptance criteria, or tracks risk reduction over time. NIST's Govern function establishes roles and policy, and Measure forces metrics so you can prove controls work. The trade-off is that RMF alone, without technical frameworks feeding it, becomes checkbox compliance — so I always tie Manage decisions back to specific OWASP or ATLAS findings.
  4. How would you operationalize SAIF in an org that already has a mature AppSec program? SAIF's first element is explicitly "extend existing security foundations to AI," so I reuse the existing SDLC, secrets management, and detection pipeline rather than rebuilding. I add AI-specific detections (element 2), automate guardrail enforcement (element 3), harmonize controls across teams via a shared platform (element 4), stand up AI red-teaming feedback loops (element 5), and contextualize each system's risk in its business process (element 6). The win is speed and consistency; the risk is treating AI as fully novel when 80 percent is existing security hygiene.
  5. An reviewer says "just use OWASP LLM Top 10, why do you need the others?" How do you respond? OWASP is scoped to LLM applications and is a risk catalog, so it misses non-LLM ML attacks (classic evasion, extraction on classifiers), lacks adversary technique detail for detection, and has no governance or measurement layer. ATLAS covers the full ML lifecycle and adversary behavior; SAIF covers infrastructure and platform controls OWASP does not; NIST covers ownership and metrics. Relying on OWASP alone gives a build-time checklist with no way to detect, govern, or prove risk reduction. The right answer is layered use, not one framework.

Tier 3 — Expert and Deep-Dive (Staff / Principal)

  1. Map a single end-to-end attack — indirect prompt injection leading to data exfiltration via a tool call — across all four frameworks. OWASP classifies it as LLM01 Prompt Injection with LLM05 Improper Output Handling and LLM06 Excessive Agency contributing to the exfil. ATLAS describes it as the LLM Prompt Injection technique for initial access, chained with Exfiltration via a tool or inference API under the Exfiltration tactic, and I would cite an ATLAS case study for realism. SAIF drives the controls: harden input handling, apply least-privilege tool scopes, add detection for anomalous tool calls, and red-team the chain. NIST wraps it: Map identifies the retrieval trust boundary, Measure tests the guardrail bypass rate, Manage decides residual-risk acceptance. This four-way mapping is what separates a Staff-level answer from a vulnerability list.
  2. Where do the frameworks disagree or leave gaps, and how do you handle emerging risks like agentic and MCP attacks? Each framework updates on its own cadence, so novel classes — multi-agent delegation abuse, MCP server trust, confused-deputy tool chaining — often lag formal inclusion. OWASP added agency and supply-chain items in its 2025 revision, and ATLAS periodically adds LLM techniques, but there is always a window where the map is behind the territory. I handle it by treating the frameworks as a scaffold, not a ceiling: run continuous red-teaming (SAIF element 5), and feed novel findings into NIST Map/Manage so the risk register captures what the published lists have not caught up to yet.
  3. How do you turn ATLAS from a knowledge base into working detections? ATLAS techniques become detection engineering inputs: for each relevant technique I define observable signals — anomalous prompt patterns for LLM Prompt Injection, high-frequency boundary-probing queries for model extraction via inference API, embedding-space anomalies for RAG poisoning — and wire them into the existing SOC pipeline per SAIF element 2. The hard part is that many ML attacks look like legitimate traffic, so detections are probabilistic and need baselining and human triage. I measure detection efficacy under NIST Measure and iterate, rather than assuming a mapped technique is a solved detection.
  4. When would you choose NIST AI RMF over an ISO or SAIF-led approach, and can they coexist? They coexist by design and operate at different layers: NIST AI RMF gives a US-recognized, technology-neutral risk lifecycle (good for regulators, auditors, and cross-org risk language), ISO/IEC 42001 gives a certifiable management-system standard, and SAIF gives concrete AI controls. I lead with NIST for governance structure when the audience is leadership or compliance, use SAIF for the actual control catalog engineers implement, and map both to OWASP/ATLAS for technical grounding. Choosing "only one" is the wrong instinct — the frameworks are complementary layers, and Staff-level maturity is knowing which to speak in front of which audience.
  5. A regulator asks you to prove your LLM product is secure. Which frameworks do you present and in what order? I lead with NIST AI RMF to show governance: named owners (Govern), a documented risk map for the use case (Map), measured metrics like guardrail bypass and disclosure rates (Measure), and a remediation and acceptance process (Manage). I then show SAIF to demonstrate the concrete control backbone across data, infrastructure, model, and application. I ground both in OWASP LLM Top 10 coverage and ATLAS technique mappings with red-team evidence to prove the controls were adversarially tested, not just declared. The narrative arc is governance first, controls second, technical and adversarial evidence third — because regulators want to see a repeatable, accountable, tested program, not a one-time scan.

6. Key Takeaways

  • Two pairs: OWASP LLM Top 10 and MITRE ATLAS are the attack/vulnerability view; Google SAIF and NIST AI RMF are the control/governance view. Name one from each pair in any answer.
  • OWASP LLM Top 10 is the per-application risk catalog — memorize LLM01 Prompt Injection, LLM02 Sensitive Info Disclosure, LLM06 Excessive Agency, LLM08 Vector/Embedding Weaknesses at minimum.
  • MITRE ATLAS is ATT&CK for ML — adversary tactics and techniques (LLM Prompt Injection, Jailbreak, Evade ML Model, Poison Training Data, Exfiltration via ML Inference API) backed by real case studies; use it for detection and threat intel.
  • Google SAIF = six elements (foundations, detection/response, automation, harmonize platform, adapt/red-team, contextualize) — the implementation-ready control backbone that reuses your existing security program.
  • NIST AI RMF = four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) with Govern cross-cutting — the accountability and measurement wrapper you present to leadership and regulators.
  • Relying on one framework is the trap: each has a blind spot (OWASP misses adversary TTPs and governance; ATLAS misses defensive architecture; NIST misses concrete exploits; SAIF is lighter on measurement).
  • How I would defend this in production: threat-model with OWASP + ATLAS, select controls with SAIF, and govern and measure with NIST AI RMF as one continuous, re-run-every-release loop.
