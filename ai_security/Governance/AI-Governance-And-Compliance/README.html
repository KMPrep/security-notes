AI Governance and Compliance

Domain: AI/ML Security — Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)
Frameworks: NIST AI RMF 1.0, EU AI Act (Reg. 2024/1689), ISO/IEC 42001:2023, Google SAIF, NIST AI RMF Generative AI Profile (NIST-AI-600-1)

Overview

AI governance is the layer of policy, process, and control that decides which AI systems an organization is allowed to build or buy, under what conditions they may reach production, and how they are monitored once live. It sits above the technical stack — data, training, model, inference, agent/tool layers — and binds each of those to accountable owners, documented risk decisions, and evidence. It matters because AI failures are rarely just bugs: they are unreviewed models making consequential decisions, unbounded agents with excessive agency, or training data with unclear provenance and licensing. The recognized anchors are NIST AI RMF (the Govern/Map/Measure/Manage functions), the EU AI Act (a risk-tiered legal regime), ISO/IEC 42001 (a certifiable AI Management System), and Google SAIF (which frames governance as automating controls and giving leaders a clear risk picture). For a security engineer the job is operational, not clerical: turn these frameworks into an AI inventory, risk assessments, and approval gates that catch real risk without becoming a delivery bottleneck.

How It Works

Core Mechanics

Governance works by attaching accountability and evidence to trust boundaries that would otherwise cross silently. The core boundaries are: an AI use case entering the organization (build, buy, or embed a third-party model/API), data entering training or a RAG index, a model moving from experiment to production, and an agent being granted tools and permissions. Governance places a control point at each: an intake/inventory step, a risk classification, a risk assessment with mitigations, and an approval decision recorded by a named owner.

NIST AI RMF organizes this into four functions. Govern is the cross-cutting culture and accountability layer — policies, roles, risk tolerance, and it wraps the other three. Map establishes context: what the system does, who it affects, and what could go wrong. Measure analyzes and quantifies risk — evaluations, red-teaming, bias and robustness testing, metrics. Manage allocates resources to treat risks and monitors them over time. It is intentionally non-prescriptive and voluntary, so an engineer instantiates it with concrete artifacts.

The EU AI Act is law, not guidance, and classifies systems by risk tier: Unacceptable (banned — e.g., social scoring, certain biometric categorization, manipulative systems), High-risk (Annex III — hiring, credit, critical infrastructure, biometrics; heavy obligations: risk management, data governance, logging, human oversight, conformity assessment), Limited-risk (transparency duties — tell users they are interacting with AI, label deepfakes/synthetic content), and Minimal-risk (most systems — no mandatory obligations). General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models carry separate obligations, stricter for those posing systemic risk. Penalties reach up to €35M or 7% of global turnover for prohibited-practice violations.

ISO/IEC 42001 is the first certifiable AI Management System (AIMS) standard, built on the familiar Plan-Do-Check-Act loop with Annex A controls covering AI policy, impact assessment, data governance, and lifecycle management. It gives governance an auditable, third-party-certifiable backbone — the "how do we prove this operates" answer.

Model Risk Management (MRM), borrowed from the banking world (SR 11-7), adds the discipline of independent validation: the team that builds a model should not be the only team that signs off on it. For high-consequence models, a separate reviewer challenges assumptions, data, and performance before approval and on a recurring cadence.

The security engineer's operating principle is risk-tiered friction: a minimal-risk internal summarizer should self-serve through a lightweight attestation, while a customer-facing agent with tool access and PII exposure hits a full risk assessment and human approval gate. Uniform heavy process for everything is how governance gets bypassed.

Top 3 Critical Mitigations

1. A Living AI Inventory (System of Record)

You cannot govern what you cannot see, so the foundational control is a complete, continuously updated inventory of every AI system — internal models, fine-tunes, embedded vendor models, and third-party AI APIs. Technically it combines a mandatory intake (registration is required to get budget, keys, or a deploy) with active discovery — scanning egress/proxy logs and CASB data for calls to known AI endpoints, and code/dependency scans for ML libraries and model artifacts. Each record carries the owner, purpose, risk tier, data classifications touched, tools/permissions, and the current approval status, forming the join key that every other control writes back to. The trade-off is that discovery is never complete and self-registration decays without enforcement; you counter this by tying inventory to something teams need (API keys, deployment) so registration is the path of least resistance rather than an optional chore. This is the operational core of NIST Govern and SAIF's "automate defenses and manage the AI portfolio."

2. Risk-Tiered Assessment and Approval Gates

The gate is where governance meets delivery, and its design determines whether governance helps or gets routed around. The mechanism is a short triage questionnaire that classifies the use case (aligned to EU AI Act tiers and internal sensitivity) and automatically routes it: minimal-risk cases get an attestation and register-and-go, while high-risk cases trigger a full risk assessment covering data provenance, evaluation/red-team results, bias and robustness testing, human-oversight design, and residual-risk sign-off by a named accountable owner. The critical engineering choice is proportionality — a single uniform heavyweight review for everything guarantees teams will bypass it, so you invest friction only where consequence is high and give low-risk paths fast, self-serve SLAs. Trade-offs: triage questionnaires can be gamed or answered wrong (mitigate with spot-audits and discovery cross-checks), and gates add latency (mitigate by embedding them in the existing CI/CD and change-management flow rather than as a separate ticket queue). This operationalizes NIST Map/Measure and the EU AI Act's conformity-assessment logic.

3. Continuous Monitoring and Independent Validation

Approval is a snapshot; risk is continuous, because models drift, data distributions shift, new attacks emerge, and agents accumulate permissions. The control is twofold: independent validation (MRM/SR 11-7 style) where a reviewer separate from the builder challenges high-consequence models before launch, and continuous monitoring — evaluation metrics, drift and bias tracking, guardrail-block rates, and abuse signals feeding time-boxed re-reviews so approvals expire and must be renewed. Technically this means wiring model evals, output logging, and telemetry back into the inventory record and alerting on threshold breaches, plus AI-specific incident-response runbooks (containment, rollback to a known-good model, disclosure). The trade-off is cost and reviewer independence — genuine independence is organizationally expensive, so reserve full MRM for the highest tiers and use automated evals for the rest; monitoring also generates noise, so tune thresholds to consequential drift, not every fluctuation. This closes NIST's Manage loop and satisfies ISO/IEC 42001's Plan-Do-Check-Act cadence.

Practice Questions

Tier 1 — Fundamentals

  1. What are the four functions of the NIST AI RMF? Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage — where Govern is the cross-cutting accountability and culture layer, Map establishes context and risks, Measure analyzes and quantifies them, and Manage treats and monitors them. This is asked to confirm you know the primary US governance framework beyond buzzwords.

  2. What are the EU AI Act risk tiers? Unacceptable (banned), High-risk (heavy obligations and conformity assessment), Limited-risk (transparency duties), and Minimal-risk (no mandatory obligations), with a separate track for General-Purpose AI models. This is asked to test whether you understand that AI regulation is risk-proportionate law, not a single blanket rule.

  3. What is ISO/IEC 42001? It is the first certifiable AI Management System standard, giving organizations an auditable Plan-Do-Check-Act framework and Annex A controls for governing AI across its lifecycle. This is asked to see if you can point to the standard that provides third-party assurance.

  4. What is Shadow AI and why is it a governance problem? It is AI used without going through intake or being in the inventory — often staff pasting data into public chatbots or teams calling AI APIs unreviewed. It matters because you cannot risk-assess, secure, or comply for systems you do not know exist. This is asked to test whether you start governance from visibility.

  5. What is an AI inventory and why is it foundational? It is the system-of-record listing every AI system with its owner, purpose, risk tier, and data touched; it is foundational because every other control — assessment, approval, monitoring — writes back to it. This is asked to check you know governance begins with knowing your portfolio.

Tier 2 — System Design and Trade-offs

  1. Design an AI governance process that does not block delivery. Use risk-tiered friction: a mandatory but lightweight intake that auto-classifies each use case, then routes minimal-risk cases to a self-serve attestation with a fast SLA and only sends high-risk cases to full assessment and human approval. Embed the gates inside existing CI/CD and change management rather than a separate ticket queue, and back it with active discovery so the fast path is not abused. The key insight is proportionality — uniform heavy review for everything is the surest way to get bypassed, so you spend friction only where consequence is high. This is asked to see if you can balance risk reduction against engineering velocity.

  2. How would you build an approval gate for a customer-facing agent with tool access? Classify it high-risk given external exposure and tool capability, then require: least-privilege scoping of each tool, human-in-the-loop for consequential or irreversible actions, input/output guardrails, red-team and eval evidence, data-flow review for PII and exfiltration paths, and a named owner signing off residual risk with a time-boxed re-review. Record the decision and conditions in the inventory as auditable evidence. I would make the approval conditional and revocable so monitoring signals can trigger re-review. This is asked to test end-to-end thinking across policy, technical controls, and accountability.

  3. A team wants to deploy a resume-screening model next week. Walk me through governance. First classify it: hiring is EU AI Act Annex III high-risk, so heavy obligations apply — risk management, data governance, logging, human oversight, and transparency. I would run a risk assessment covering training-data provenance and bias testing, require human oversight so the model recommends rather than auto-rejects, check for prohibited practices, and get independent validation before approval. If evidence is not ready, the gate holds the launch — that is the process working, not failing. This is asked to see if you can map a concrete use case to real regulatory obligations.

  4. How do you govern third-party and GPAI models you did not build? Treat procurement as a control point: require model cards, evaluation and safety documentation, data-handling and licensing terms, and contractual assurances; assess the vendor's own controls and the model's systemic-risk profile. Record the dependency in the inventory and inherit-plus-augment its risk (you still own how you use it). For GPAI with systemic risk, note the EU AI Act imposes extra obligations on the provider that you should verify. This is asked to test supply-chain thinking for AI, not just self-built models.

  5. How do you measure whether your AI governance program actually works? Track coverage (percent of AI systems in the inventory vs. discovered via egress scanning), gate metrics (assessment completion, time-to-approval SLA adherence, bypass/exception rates), and outcome metrics (incidents, drift-triggered re-reviews, audit findings). A healthy program shows high coverage, fast low-risk SLAs, and low bypass — high bypass means the process is too heavy. I would also audit a sample of self-serve attestations against reality. This is asked to see if you treat governance as a measurable system, not paperwork.

Tier 3 — Expert and Deep-Dive

  1. NIST AI RMF is voluntary and non-prescriptive while the EU AI Act is binding law — how do you reconcile them in one program? I use NIST AI RMF as the operating model — its Govern/Map/Measure/Manage functions and lifecycle give the structure — and map EU AI Act obligations onto it as mandatory requirements that fire based on risk tier and jurisdiction. ISO/IEC 42001 provides the auditable management-system wrapper that proves the whole thing runs. In practice this is a control-mapping exercise: one internal control set that satisfies multiple frameworks, tagged so an auditor for any one of them sees coverage, avoiding three parallel programs. The hard part is jurisdictional scoping — the same model may be minimal-risk internally but high-risk when it touches EU users. This is asked to test whether you can harmonize voluntary frameworks with hard legal mandates without tripling the work.

  2. How do you prevent governance from becoming security theater? Theater happens when the artifact (a signed form) is mistaken for the outcome (reduced risk), so I tie every gate to verifiable evidence rather than attestation alone — eval results, red-team reports, data-lineage records — and cross-check self-declarations against active discovery and spot-audits. I make approvals time-boxed and revocable so they reflect current reality, and I measure bypass rate as a first-class signal that the process has lost legitimacy. Crucially, governance owns residual-risk decisions with a named accountable person, so there is a real signature behind a real trade-off, not a rubber stamp. This is asked to probe whether you can tell the difference between compliance activity and actual risk reduction.

  3. How does model risk management from banking (SR 11-7) translate to modern LLM and agentic systems? The core principle — independent validation by someone other than the builder, plus ongoing monitoring — translates directly, but the risk surface is broader: LLMs add prompt injection, non-determinism, and emergent behavior that classical statistical validation never faced. So validation must include adversarial red-teaming and eval suites, not just backtesting, and monitoring must watch guardrail-block rates and abuse signals, not just performance drift. For agents, validation must scrutinize tool scopes and the blast radius of autonomous actions — excessive agency is the new model risk. The independence principle is the durable import; the test methods must modernize. This is asked at Staff/Principal level to see if you can carry mature risk discipline into a new technology without cargo-culting it.

  4. An agent's permissions have quietly expanded over months. How should governance have caught this, and how do you fix it? This is approval-drift and it exposes a governance gap: approval was treated as a one-time event rather than a living state. The catch should come from time-boxed approvals that expire and force re-review, plus continuous monitoring that diffs an agent's current tool/permission set against its approved set and alerts on divergence. The fix is to make the approved scope a declarative, enforced artifact — least-privilege by default, capability grants recorded in the inventory, and any expansion routed back through the gate. I would also add runtime enforcement so unapproved tools simply cannot be invoked. This is asked to test whether you understand governance as continuous state reconciliation, not a launch checkpoint.

  5. Where does governance stop and technical security control begin — and why does the boundary matter? Governance decides whether and under what conditions an AI system may exist and who is accountable; technical controls (guardrails, sandboxing, least-privilege, signing) enforce how it behaves at runtime. The boundary matters because each fails without the other: perfect guardrails on an unreviewed, prohibited use case is still a legal and ethical failure, while flawless governance paperwork with no runtime enforcement is theater. The mature model is a closed loop — governance sets policy and risk tolerance, security implements and measures enforcement, and monitoring feeds evidence back to governance to renew or revoke approval. This is asked to test altitude — that you understand governance and engineering as one system rather than competing departments, and can operate at both the policy and the technical level.

Key Takeaways

  • Governance is accountability and evidence attached to trust boundaries: which AI is allowed, on what conditions, and who owns the residual risk.
  • NIST AI RMF (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage) is the voluntary operating model; the EU AI Act is binding, risk-tiered law; ISO/IEC 42001 is the certifiable management-system wrapper; SAIF frames it as automating controls.
  • Visibility comes first — a living AI inventory built from mandatory intake plus active discovery is the foundation every other control writes back to.
  • Design for risk-tiered friction: self-serve the minimal, gate the high-risk; uniform heavy process is the surest way to get bypassed.
  • Approval is a state, not an event — time-box it, monitor for drift, and reconcile an agent's actual permissions against its approved scope continuously.
  • Independent validation (MRM/SR 11-7) and human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions are the controls that keep high-risk models honest.
  • The one-line production answer: give every AI system an owner and an inventory record, tier it, gate the risky ones with evidence-backed approval, and continuously monitor so approvals expire before the risk does.
