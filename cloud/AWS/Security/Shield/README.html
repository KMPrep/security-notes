Shield — AWS Shield

Category: Security
AWS Service Type: DDoS Protection
Key Docs: AWS Shield Documentation

Overview

AWS Shield provides managed DDoS protection for AWS workloads. Shield Standard is automatically included for all AWS customers and protects against common network and transport layer attacks on services like CloudFront, Route 53, and ELB. Shield Advanced adds deeper detection, broader protections, DDoS cost protection, global threat dashboarding, advanced metrics, and access to the AWS DDoS Response Team. Shield matters most for public internet-facing services where volumetric or protocol-based attacks can create availability risk before application-layer defenses even engage.

How It Works

Core Mechanics

Shield Standard Baseline always-on L3/L4 DDoS detection and mitigation for AWS edge services
Shield Advanced Paid service with enhanced visibility, response support, and cost protections
DRT AWS DDoS Response Team available with Shield Advanced
Health-based detection Integrates Route 53 health checks and resource health signals into response
Protected resource CloudFront, Route 53, Global Accelerator, ALB, NLB, Elastic IP, and more
Cost protection Credits for scaling or usage charges triggered by qualifying DDoS events
Attack diagnostics Higher-detail telemetry on attack vectors and mitigations
Proactive engagement Option for AWS to contact customer during significant events

What Can Go Wrong

# Failure / Attack Root Cause Impact Likelihood
1 No edge architecture Origin exposed directly instead of through protected edge services Higher DDoS blast radius High
2 Shield Standard assumed sufficient App-layer abuse or business-logic floods not addressed Partial protection only High
3 No runbook for attack event Team lacks operational response steps Confused incident handling Med
4 Health signals missing Shield Advanced cannot optimize response with app health context Slower or less precise mitigation Low
5 Cost spike from attack side effects Autoscaling and data transfer surge Unexpected spend during event Med
6 Logging gaps Hard to correlate attack with origin impact Weak forensics Med
7 Unprotected secondary services Only main website protected, APIs or EIPs forgotten Attackers shift to weaker surface Med
8 Overreliance on DDoS protection Availability design not resilient otherwise Non-DDoS outages still dominate risk Med

Mitigation Controls

# Failure Mitigation Layer / Where Applied
1 No edge architecture Front internet workloads with CloudFront, Route 53, or ELB Edge design
2 Shield Standard assumed sufficient Pair Shield with WAF, rate limiting, auth, and caching Layered defense
3 No runbook for attack event Prepare DDoS response runbook and escalation paths Incident response
4 Health signals missing Configure health checks and resource health integration Shield Advanced tuning
5 Cost spike from attack side effects Consider Shield Advanced cost protection for critical services Financial resilience
6 Logging gaps Aggregate WAF, ELB, CloudFront, and app logs for correlation Monitoring
7 Unprotected secondary services Inventory all public entry points and protect them consistently Asset governance
8 Overreliance on DDoS protection Build resilient autoscaling and failover independent of attack controls Availability engineering

Top 3 Critical Mitigations

  1. Use protected edge services as the public front door: Shield is most effective when traffic hits CloudFront, Route 53, or ELB first.
  2. Layer Shield with WAF and app controls: DDoS protection handles availability pressure, not business-logic abuse or authenticated floods.
  3. Create an attack runbook before the first event: Fast decisions on traffic shifting, rate limits, caching, and escalation are what reduce damage.

Key Takeaways

  • Shield is foundational for public AWS workloads but is strongest at the edge.
  • Standard gives baseline coverage; Advanced adds visibility, support, and economics.
  • DDoS protection must be layered with WAF, caching, and resilient app design.
  • Operational readiness matters as much as service enablement.
  • Public surface inventory is essential because attackers will choose the least protected endpoint.
