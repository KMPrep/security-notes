Azure Archive Storage

Category: Storage
Azure Service Type: Object Storage — Archive Tier
Key Docs: Blob Storage Tiers

Overview

Azure Archive Storage is the lowest-cost storage tier in Azure Blob Storage, designed for data that is rarely (or never) accessed and can tolerate retrieval latency measured in hours. Archive blobs are stored offline and must be rehydrated (moved to Hot or Cool tier) before reading. Archive is accessible only at the individual blob level — you cannot read directly from Archive. It is used for long-term backup retention, compliance archives, regulatory records, and cold DR data. Pricing is pennies per GB per month but early-deletion fees apply (180 days minimum) and retrieval is charged per GB.

How It Works

Core Concepts

Concept Description
Archive Tier Offline object storage; blobs cannot be read until rehydrated
Rehydration Process of moving a blob from Archive to Hot/Cool for reading; standard (15h) or high-priority (1h)
Rehydration Priority Standard (up to 15 hours) or High Priority (under 1 hour, higher cost)
Early Deletion Fee Archive blobs deleted or tiered within 180 days incur a prorated early deletion charge
Lifecycle Management Automatic tiering policy to move blobs to Archive after a defined inactivity period
Copy to Rehydrate Alternative to set-tier: copy an Archive blob to a new blob in Hot/Cool without modifying the original
Immutable Archive Combine Archive tier with WORM policy for compliance retention
Blob Inventory Regular manifest of all blobs and their tiers for cost analysis and audit

Step-by-Step Flow — Archive and Retrieve

sequenceDiagram
    participant App as Application
    participant Storage as Blob Storage
    participant Archive as Archive Backend (Offline)
    App->>Storage: Set blob tier to Archive
    Storage->>Archive: Move blob to offline storage
    Archive-->>Storage: Blob status = Archived
    App->>Storage: Request rehydration (set tier to Hot, priority=High)
    Storage->>Archive: Begin rehydration job
    Archive-->>Storage: Blob copied to hot storage (up to 1h for high priority)
    Storage-->>App: Blob status = Hot, readable
    App->>Storage: GET blob content
    Storage-->>App: Blob data

What Can Go Wrong

# Failure / Attack Root Cause Attack Scenario Impact Likelihood
1 RTO breach — recovery requires Archive rehydration DR plan assumes all tiers are instantly readable An organization stores database backups in Archive tier to save costs. A disaster event requires immediate database recovery. The team initiates rehydration. High-priority rehydration takes up to 1 hour per blob, but there are 500 GB of backup blobs requiring multiple batches. The recovery takes 8 hours, far exceeding the stated 2-hour RTO. RTO breach on disaster recovery High
2 Cost spike from unanticipated rehydration Bulk rehydration of large Archive data set A data analyst runs a report that reads all historical data. A downstream process automatically triggers rehydration of 10 TB of archived blobs to process the query. The rehydration bandwidth and retrieval fees generate an unexpected $5,000 bill. Neither the analyst nor the billing team was aware. Unexpected cost amplification High
3 Early deletion fee accumulates Blobs tiered to Archive and quickly deleted A misconfigured lifecycle policy tiers blobs to Archive and then deletes them after 30 days. Every blob deleted before the 180-day minimum incurs an early deletion fee. For a high-volume pipeline writing millions of small blobs, the early deletion fees exceed the storage savings. Cost optimization that increases cost Med
4 No immutability on compliance archive Archived blobs can be deleted by insiders A compliance team relies on Archive tier for long-term retention but does not enable WORM. An insider with Storage Blob Data Contributor role deletes archived blobs to destroy evidence during an investigation. The blobs are gone with no recovery path after the soft-delete window. Evidence tampering, compliance failure Med
5 Lifecycle policy targets wrong blobs Overly broad prefix rule archives active data A lifecycle policy rule is defined with a prefix filter of /logs/. An application stores active configuration files in /logs/config/. The lifecycle rule tiers the config files to Archive after 90 days. The application fails to read config on next restart because the blobs are offline. Application failure from archived active data Med
6 Rehydration status not monitored Rehydration starts but completion is not tracked An automated recovery process triggers rehydration and then immediately attempts to read the blobs. The read fails because rehydration is asynchronous and the blobs are still offline. The process has no polling logic or event-based trigger on rehydration completion. Recovery script failure Med
7 No encryption at rest on archive Sensitive archived data without CMK Archived blobs containing financial or health records use only platform-managed keys. A compliance audit requires proof of key rotation and key custody. Platform-managed keys do not provide the necessary audit trail for BYOK compliance requirements. Compliance failure for regulated data Med
8 Blob inventory not configured No visibility into archived data volume and age Without blob inventory, the storage team cannot identify which blobs are in Archive tier, how old they are, or how many are approaching the early deletion threshold. Cost anomalies cannot be investigated without manually iterating the container. Operational blindness on storage costs Low
9 Archive used for active ML training data ML pipeline reads Archive blobs directly A data science team stores ML training datasets in Archive tier to save costs. An ML pipeline is configured to read the data for weekly training runs. The pipeline fails because Archive blobs cannot be read directly. The team does not know about rehydration requirements. Pipeline failure from misunderstood tier Med
10 Geo-redundant Archive data in wrong region GRS copies archived data to paired region An organization in a sovereignty-restricted jurisdiction uses GRS on an archive-tier storage account. Azure GRS replicates all data, including archived blobs, to the paired region in a different country. This violates the data residency requirement even though the data is at rest in Archive. Data residency compliance violation Med

Mitigation Controls

# Failure Mitigation Layer
1 RTO breach Keep DR-critical backups in Cool tier; document rehydration time in RTO plan; test DR annually DR planning
2 Rehydration cost spike Set budget alerts on storage accounts; restrict rehydration to authorized roles via RBAC Cost governance
3 Early deletion fees Set lifecycle Archive transition after 180+ days; review minimum retention periods Lifecycle design
4 No immutability Enable WORM (time-based or legal hold) on compliance archive containers Immutable storage
5 Lifecycle targets active data Use precise prefix/tag filters; test lifecycle rules in staging before production Lifecycle governance
6 No rehydration monitoring Poll blob status or use Event Grid blob tier change events to trigger downstream processing Event-driven monitoring
7 No CMK Apply CMK disk encryption set via Azure Policy for all sensitive storage accounts Encryption policy
8 No blob inventory Enable blob inventory reports for Archive tier analysis; use Azure Cost Management for tier breakdown Observability
9 Archive for active ML data Use Hot or Cool for active datasets; use Archive only for retired training checkpoints Tier selection
10 Data residency violation Use LRS or ZRS for sovereignty-restricted accounts; avoid GRS/GZRS when residency is required Redundancy selection

Key Takeaways

  • Archive is the lowest-cost tier but requires rehydration before reading; blobs are offline.
  • High-priority rehydration takes up to 1 hour; standard takes up to 15 hours — plan DR RTO accordingly.
  • Enable WORM on compliance archives to prevent insider deletion.
  • Set lifecycle transitions only after 180 days to avoid early deletion fees.
  • Archive tier is for truly cold data; keep DR-critical data in Cool or Cold tier for faster access.
