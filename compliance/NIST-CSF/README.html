NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF 2.0)

Category: Governance, Risk & Compliance (GRC) — voluntary risk framework
Key standards / frameworks: NIST CSF 2.0 (2024), NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5, NIST SP 800-37 Rev 2 (Risk Management Framework), NIST SP 800-30 (risk assessment)

Overview

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework is a voluntary, outcome-based framework for managing cybersecurity risk. Originally published in 2014 for US critical infrastructure, CSF 2.0 (released February 2024) broadened its audience to organizations of every size and sector and added an explicit governance dimension. It is deliberately not a control catalog; it describes cybersecurity outcomes and leaves the choice of specific controls to informative references such as SP 800-53.

Its power for a security leader is as a common language. The framework lets a CISO describe the current state of the program, define a target state aligned to business risk, and communicate the gap to executives and the board in terms they can act on. Because it maps to SP 800-53, ISO 27001, and CIS Controls, it also functions as an organizing spine that ties disparate compliance efforts together.

How It Works

The Six Functions

CSF 2.0 organizes outcomes into six Functions. Govern (new in 2.0) sits at the center and establishes risk strategy, roles, policy, and oversight. The other five form the operational lifecycle: Identify assets and risks, Protect them with safeguards, Detect events, Respond to incidents, and Recover capabilities afterward. Govern informs and is informed by all five.

Categories and Subcategories

Each Function decomposes into Categories (for example Asset Management or Identity Management and Access Control) and then into Subcategories, which are the concrete outcome statements such as maintaining an inventory of hardware. Subcategories are the anchor points that map to informative references, and there are just over 100 of them in CSF 2.0.

  G[Govern] --> I[Identify]
  G --> P[Protect]
  G --> D[Detect]
  G --> R[Respond]
  G --> C[Recover]
  I --> P
  P --> D
  D --> R
  R --> C

Implementation Tiers and Profiles

Four Implementation Tiers describe the rigor of an organization's risk management practices, from Tier 1 Partial through Tier 2 Risk Informed and Tier 3 Repeatable to Tier 4 Adaptive. Tiers are not maturity grades to maximize; they express how well practices fit the organization's risk appetite. Profiles capture the selection of outcomes for a given context. Teams document a Current Profile and a Target Profile, and the difference between them drives a prioritized action plan.

flowchart TD
  A[Current Profile] --> B[Gap analysis]
  T[Target Profile] --> B
  B --> C[Prioritized action plan]
  C --> D[Implement SP 800 53 controls]
  D --> E[Reassess Current Profile]
  E --> B

Relationship to SP 800-53 and the RMF

CSF says what outcomes to achieve; NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 provides the how as a catalog of over 1000 controls across 20 families. The Risk Management Framework in SP 800-37 provides the process, the seven steps of Prepare, Categorize, Select, Implement, Assess, Authorize, and Monitor, that federal systems follow to apply those controls. A useful mental model: CSF is the strategy layer, the RMF is the operating process, and 800-53 is the control library both draw on.

What Can Go Wrong

# Failure / Pitfall Root Cause Impact Likelihood
1 Treating Implementation Tiers as a maturity score to maximize Misreading Tiers as grades rather than risk fit Over investment in areas that do not match risk appetite High
2 Adopting CSF as a checklist and expecting it to prescribe controls Confusing an outcome framework with a control catalog Outcomes stated but no concrete safeguards implemented High
3 Govern function ignored so strategy and roles stay undefined Legacy focus on the five operational Functions only No accountability and inconsistent risk decisions Medium
4 Current and Target Profiles never documented Framework adopted in name without the gap analysis discipline No prioritized roadmap and no way to measure progress Medium
5 Subcategories mapped inaccurately to SP 800-53 controls Crosswalk done once and never validated Assurance gaps hidden behind a false sense of coverage Medium
6 Profiles set once and left static as the business changes No continuous monitoring feeding profile updates Target state drifts out of line with real risk Medium
7 Framework outputs never translated for executives and the board Security team keeps CSF as an internal technical artifact Underfunding because leadership cannot see the risk picture Medium

Key Takeaways

  • CSF 2.0 defines cybersecurity outcomes across six Functions, with the new Govern function anchoring strategy, roles, and oversight over Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.
  • The framework states what to achieve; SP 800-53 supplies the controls and the RMF supplies the process to implement and monitor them.
  • Implementation Tiers express risk-management rigor and fit, not a maturity score to maximize.
  • Current and Target Profiles turn the framework into a prioritized, measurable roadmap through gap analysis.
  • Subcategories are the mapping anchors that connect CSF to 800-53, ISO 27001, and CIS Controls for multi-framework alignment.
  • CSF is a communication tool: its greatest value is expressing cybersecurity posture to executives and boards in business-risk terms.
  • Profiles must be revisited as the business and threat landscape change, driven by continuous monitoring feedback.
