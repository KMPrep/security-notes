SOC 2 & ISO 27001

Category: Governance, Risk & Compliance (GRC) — attestation and certification
Key standards / frameworks: AICPA SOC 2 (SSAE 18 / AT-C 205, 2017 Trust Services Criteria), ISO/IEC 27001:2022, ISO/IEC 27002:2022, ISO/IEC 27005 risk management

Overview

SOC 2 and ISO/IEC 27001 are the two most widely requested assurances that a service organization runs a credible security program. They arrive at similar destinations by different routes. SOC 2 is an attestation: a licensed CPA firm examines your controls against the AICPA Trust Services Criteria and issues an opinion in a report you share under NDA with customers. ISO 27001 is a certification: an accredited certification body audits your Information Security Management System against the standard and issues a public certificate valid for three years.

The practical difference is form and audience. SOC 2 reports are detailed narratives favored in North American B2B sales; ISO 27001 certificates are concise, internationally recognized proofs favored in Europe and public tenders. Because both rest on the same underlying control families, most organizations build one control set and satisfy both, sequencing the audits to reuse evidence.

How It Works

SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria

SOC 2 is built on five Trust Services Categories. Security (the Common Criteria, CC1 through CC9) is mandatory and maps to the COSO framework. The other four are optional and included only if relevant to the service: Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, and Privacy. The auditor tests the controls the organization asserts satisfy each in-scope criterion.

Type I versus Type II Reports

A Type I report opines on whether controls are suitably designed at a single point in time. A Type II report is far more valuable: it opines on operating effectiveness across a review period, typically 3 to 12 months, by sampling evidence throughout that window. The auditor issues an opinion of unqualified, qualified, adverse, or disclaimer. Customers almost always want a Type II covering a recent period.

ISO 27001 ISMS and Annex A

ISO 27001 certifies a management system, not just controls. The organization defines ISMS scope, conducts a risk assessment, and selects controls to treat those risks. The 2022 revision restructured Annex A into 93 controls across four themes: Organizational, People, Physical, and Technological, and introduced 11 new controls including threat intelligence, information security for cloud services, and secure coding. The Statement of Applicability (SoA) lists every Annex A control with a justification for inclusion or exclusion, and the risk treatment plan records how each accepted risk is handled.

Attestation versus Certification and the Overlap

A CPA firm performs the SOC 2 examination under SSAE 18; an accredited body (itself accredited under ISO/IEC 17021) performs ISO certification through a Stage 1 documentation review and a Stage 2 on-site audit, followed by annual surveillance audits and recertification at year three. The two overlap heavily: SOC 2 Common Criteria map cleanly onto ISO 27001 Annex A controls, so a single control such as enforced access review satisfies both.

What Can Go Wrong

# Failure / Pitfall Root Cause Impact Likelihood
1 Type II report contains exceptions for controls that did not operate consistently Evidence gaps during the review period, controls run manually and skipped Qualified opinion erodes customer trust and stalls deals High
2 Statement of Applicability excludes controls without valid justification Scope trimmed to reduce effort rather than by risk assessment Major nonconformity blocks certification Medium
3 ISMS scope drawn too narrowly to be credible Attempt to minimize audit burden by excluding core systems Certificate is technically valid but commercially worthless Medium
4 Treating the report or certificate as proof of being secure Compliance mistaken for a security outcome Breach occurs through gaps the framework never covered High
5 Auditor independence or competence questioned by a customer Using an unaccredited body or a firm without security depth Report rejected during vendor security review Medium
6 Surveillance audit fails after certification ISMS not maintained once the certificate was won Suspension or withdrawal of certification Medium
7 Evidence collected reactively in a pre audit scramble No continuous control monitoring in place High cost, staff burnout, and sampling gaps High

Mitigation Controls

# Failure Mitigation Layer / Where Applied
1 Inconsistent control operation Automate controls and evidence capture so operation is continuous and logged GRC tooling and CI CD pipelines
2 Weak Statement of Applicability Drive inclusion and exclusion from a documented risk assessment per ISO 27005 ISMS governance
3 Non credible scope Define scope around the systems that process customer data end to end Program design and leadership
4 Compliance mistaken for security Layer threat modeling, penetration testing, and red teaming on top of controls Security engineering
5 Auditor credibility Engage an accredited certification body and a reputable CPA firm with security specialists Vendor selection
6 Post certification drift Run internal audits and management reviews on a fixed cadence between surveillance visits ISMS operations
7 Reactive evidence collection Deploy continuous compliance monitoring that tests controls and alerts on drift Automated monitoring

Key Takeaways

  • SOC 2 is a CPA attestation against Trust Services Criteria; ISO 27001 is an accredited certification of a management system, and both rest on the same control families.
  • Always pursue a SOC 2 Type II over a period; a Type I point-in-time report carries far less assurance for customers.
  • Security is the only mandatory SOC 2 category; add Availability, Processing Integrity, Confidentiality, or Privacy only where relevant to the service.
  • The ISO 27001:2022 Annex A has 93 controls across four themes, and the Statement of Applicability must justify every inclusion and exclusion from a risk assessment.
  • Build one unified control set and map it to both frameworks so a single control satisfies a Common Criterion and an Annex A control at once.
  • Certification and attestation are ongoing: surveillance audits, management reviews, and continuous monitoring keep the assurance valid between cycles.
  • Neither report nor certificate proves you are secure; they prove a baseline operated, so keep engineering above that baseline.
