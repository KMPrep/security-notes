Incident Response Lifecycle — NIST SP 800-61 & SANS PICERL

Domain: Security Operations / Incident Response (anchor note for incident/)
Standards: NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 2 (and Rev. 3, 2025 draft aligned to CSF 2.0), SANS PICERL, ISO/IEC 27035, ATT&CK for Enterprise
Related notes: DFIR-Forensic-Process · SOAR-and-Playbooks · Cloud-Incident-Response · Tabletop-Exercises · ../README.md

1. Overview

Incident response (IR) is the disciplined, repeatable process an organization uses to detect, investigate, contain, and recover from adverse security events while preserving evidence and learning from what happened. At scale — a sized fleet with millions of hosts, thousands of services, and dozens of clouds/regions — IR is not a heroic firefight; it is an engineered capability with defined roles, telemetry pipelines, severity taxonomies, playbooks, and automation. The dominant reference is NIST SP 800-61 (Rev. 2, "Computer Security Incident Handling Guide," 2012; Rev. 3 reframes the same work through the NIST CSF 2.0 functions Govern/Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover). The practitioner community more often speaks in the SANS PICERL mnemonic: Preparation, Identification, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Lessons Learned. The two models describe the same lifecycle; NIST collapses PICERL's middle three into one "Containment, Eradication & Recovery" phase because those activities interleave in practice.

Why it matters at scale: the difference between a contained incident and a headline breach is usually measured in dwell time (time from compromise to detection) and MTTR (mean time to respond/recover). The 2013 Target breach festered for weeks; the 2020 SolarWinds/SUNBURST supply-chain compromise went undetected across thousands of organizations for ~9 months. A mature IR program shrinks those numbers with pre-built detections, decision authority pushed to the responder, and rehearsed muscle memory. IR is also increasingly a compliance obligation: SEC 4-business-day materiality disclosure (2023 rule), GDPR Article 33's 72-hour breach notification, PCI-DSS 12.10, and HIPAA all impose clocks that start at detection, so the process has legal teeth.

TL;DR
- Know both NIST SP 800-61's 4 phases and SANS PICERL's 6 steps, and that they map 1:1 except NIST merges Containment/Eradication/Recovery.
- Preparation is where incidents are won — telemetry, playbooks, roles, and access provisioned before the event, not during it.
- Containment has a real tradeoff: isolate-now vs. observe-to-learn. Pulling the plug destroys attacker-attribution intel and may tip them off; observing prolongs risk. Decide by business impact + evidence needs.
- Chain of custody and blameless post-mortems are non-negotiable — the first protects legal/forensic value, the second protects the learning loop that actually reduces future incidents.
- A severity matrix drives everything — it sets who gets paged, comms cadence, and whether you invoke the crisis bridge.

2. How It Works

2.1 Core mechanics

The lifecycle is a loop, not a line: Post-Incident Activity feeds directly back into Preparation. NIST's four phases and how PICERL maps onto them:

NIST SP 800-61 Phase SANS PICERL step(s) Core question answered Primary artifacts
Preparation Preparation Are we ready? Runbooks, IR plan, tooling, on-call, comms tree, warm evidence store
Detection & Analysis Identification Is this real, and how bad? Alert, triage notes, severity rating, scope assessment, timeline
Containment, Eradication & Recovery Containment + Eradication + Recovery Stop it, remove it, restore safely Isolation actions, IOC list, remediation tickets, validation tests
Post-Incident Activity Lessons Learned What do we fix so it never recurs? Blameless post-mortem, corrective-action items (owners + dates)

Key objects and roles that make the machine run:

Object / Role Purpose Notes
Incident Commander (IC) Single decision authority; owns the incident, not the fix Rotates; explicitly not the person doing hands-on remediation
Scribe Maintains the authoritative timeline in real time Timestamps every action/decision; feeds the post-mortem
Subject-Matter Expert (SME) Service/domain owner doing hands-on work Reports to IC; owns technical remediation
Comms / Liaison lead Internal + external stakeholder updates Bridges to Legal, PR, Support, execs
Legal / Privacy counsel Privilege, breach-notification clocks, regulator interface Often invokes attorney–client privilege over IR work product
Severity level (SEV) Drives paging, cadence, escalation See severity matrix in §2.3
Playbook / runbook Codified response steps for a known scenario Semi- or fully-automated in SOAR
War room / bridge Synchronous coordination channel Dedicated Slack channel + video bridge; ephemeral, incident-scoped
Ticket / case System of record Jira/ServiceNow/PagerDuty; links evidence, comms, timeline

The control plane of IR is the IC + severity taxonomy + comms tree (who decides, how loud, who's told). The data plane is the telemetry (EDR, cloud audit logs, IDS/SIEM, netflow) plus the action surface (isolate host, rotate credential, block IP). Good programs separate deciding from doing so the IC keeps altitude while SMEs execute.

Severity/priority classification. Severity should be a function of impact × scope, not gut feel. Impact axes: confidentiality (data exfiltrated?), integrity (data/systems altered?), availability (service down?), plus regulatory/legal exposure and blast radius (one host vs. whole tenant). A canonical scheme:

  • SEV-1 / Critical — active, widespread compromise or major data breach; customer data confirmed exfiltrated; production down. Page execs, invoke crisis bridge, 30-min comms cadence.
  • SEV-2 / High — confirmed compromise of a sensitive system, contained or containable; likely regulatory notification. Full IR team, hourly cadence.
  • SEV-3 / Medium — suspicious activity confirmed as malicious but limited (single non-sensitive host, no data at risk). On-call handles; daily cadence.
  • SEV-4 / Low — policy violation, isolated malware auto-remediated by EDR, no spread. Ticket, no page.

2.2 Concrete configuration

Declaring and severity-tagging an incident (PagerDuty CLI-style):

# Trigger a SEV-1 and auto-page the IR escalation policy
pd incident create \
  --title "Confirmed AWS access-key compromise: AKIA... exfil to unknown IP" \
  --service SEC-IR --urgency high \
  --escalation-policy "IR-Commander-Rotation" \
  --field severity=SEV-1

Containment — cloud credential incident (AWS). The single most important action for a leaked long-lived key is to attach a deny-all, not delete the key (deletion destroys forensic linkage and may break legitimate recovery):

# 1. Quarantine the compromised IAM user WITHOUT deleting (preserves attribution)
aws iam attach-user-policy \
  --user-name svc-deploy \
  --policy-arn arn:aws:iam::aws:policy/AWSDenyAll

# 2. Deactivate (not delete) the specific leaked access key
aws iam update-access-key \
  --user-name svc-deploy --access-key-id AKIAIOSFODNN7EXAMPLE \
  --status Inactive

# 3. Scope the blast radius: what did the key DO in the last 14 days?
aws cloudtrail lookup-events \
  --lookup-attributes AttributeKey=AccessKeyId,AttributeValue=AKIAIOSFODNN7EXAMPLE \
  --start-time 2026-06-22T00:00:00Z --max-results 200

# 4. Revoke ALL active STS sessions minted from the user (defeats already-issued tokens)
aws iam put-user-policy --user-name svc-deploy \
  --policy-name RevokeOldSessions \
  --policy-document file://revoke-sessions.json

revoke-sessions.json uses the aws:TokenIssueTime trick — a deny on any session issued before "now" kills tokens the attacker already holds without waiting for the 1–12h STS expiry:

{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [{
    "Effect": "Deny",
    "Action": "*",
    "Resource": "*",
    "Condition": { "DateLessThan": { "aws:TokenIssueTime": "2026-07-06T14:00:00Z" } }
  }]
}

Endpoint isolation (EDR — CrowdStrike/Defender pattern):

# CrowdStrike Falcon: network-contain a host (keeps EDR channel, cuts all else)
falconctl device-control --action network-contain --device-id 9f3c...e1
# Microsoft Defender for Endpoint via API:
# POST /api/machines/{id}/isolate  { "IsolationType": "Full", "Comment": "IR SEV-2 case 4471" }

Network containment is preferred over "yank the cable" because it retains the EDR management channel — you can still collect memory, run scripts, and pull artifacts while the host is otherwise firewalled off from the network. This is the practical resolution of the isolate-vs-observe tension for endpoints.

Chain-of-custody hashing (immutable evidence intake):

# Acquire, then hash immediately; hash BEFORE and AFTER copy to prove integrity
sha256sum mem_dump_host42_20260706T1412Z.raw | tee custody/mem_dump.sha256
# Write-once storage: S3 Object Lock in COMPLIANCE mode = even root can't delete
aws s3api put-object --bucket ir-evidence-vault \
  --key case-4471/mem_dump_host42.raw --body mem_dump_host42.raw \
  --object-lock-mode COMPLIANCE \
  --object-lock-retain-until-date 2033-07-06T00:00:00Z

Case study 1 — Capital One (2019), a cloud-credential incident end to end. A misconfigured WAF allowed a Server-Side Request Forgery attack that reached the EC2 Instance Metadata Service (IMDSv1, which required no session-oriented token). The attacker retrieved temporary credentials for the WAF's IAM role, which had broad S3 read permissions, and used them to ListBuckets and copy ~100M credit applications. The containment that would have shrunk this: IMDSv2 (which uses a PUT-obtained session token and hop-limit, blocking SSRF token theft), plus least-privilege on the role so a single leaked credential couldn't read every bucket. From an IR-process view, the giveaway was anomalous S3 access from an unusual role/IP — exactly the GuardDuty UnauthorizedAccess:IAMUser/* finding class. Detection came late (a tip, not internal telemetry), underscoring that Preparation (behavioral cloud detections + scoped roles) is where this incident was actually lost.

Case study 2 — Colonial Pipeline (2021), containment with physical stakes. DarkSide operators entered through a legacy VPN account that lacked MFA and whose password appeared in a credential dump. Once ransomware hit IT systems, Colonial made a containment decision to proactively shut down the operational pipeline — not because OT was confirmed compromised, but to prevent spread and because billing/IT systems were down. This is the isolate-vs-observe / business-impact tradeoff at its starkest: containment itself caused fuel shortages across the US East Coast. The lesson: containment strategy must be pre-decided with business owners in the room (a tabletop deliverable, see Tabletop-Exercises), and remote-access MFA is the cheapest incident-prevention control that exists.

Case study 3 — Uber/Lapsus$ (2022), why Preparation gaps compound. An external contractor's credential was bought; the attacker spammed MFA push prompts until the user accepted one (MFA fatigue). Inside the VPN, the attacker found a PowerShell script on a network share containing hardcoded admin credentials for the Thycotic PAM system — effectively the keys to the kingdom. The IR takeaways are all Preparation failures: number-matching/phishing-resistant MFA would have stopped step one; secrets management (no creds in scripts) would have stopped the pivot; and privileged-access monitoring would have detected the PAM takeover. When Preparation is thin, Detection & Analysis inherits an impossible job.

Top 5 in depth

1. Preparation — provision access, telemetry, and authority before the event. The highest-leverage IR investment is everything you do when nothing is on fire: centralized immutable logging (so Detection & Analysis has data to work with), pre-provisioned break-glass access (so responders aren't blocked by their own least-privilege at 3am), a maintained on-call roster with an IC rotation, and playbooks tied to your top-10 likely scenarios. Concretely: ship CloudTrail/EDR/DNS/netflow to a log lake with 90+ day retention and Object Lock; pre-write and pre-authorize containment actions (host isolation, key deactivation) so on-call can act without a change-approval meeting. Tradeoff: pre-authorized destructive actions risk over-containment by a junior responder — mitigate with clear SEV thresholds and "isolate is always reversible, delete is not" guidance. Preparation is unglamorous and perpetually under-funded, which is exactly why it's the differentiator.

2. The isolate-vs-observe containment decision framework. Containment splits into short-term (immediate stop-the-bleeding: isolate host, block IP, disable account) and long-term (durable fixes that let you recover: rebuild, re-image, network re-segmentation). The hard call is isolate now vs. observe to gather intelligence. Isolate when: sensitive data is actively being exfiltrated, availability is degrading, or ransomware is spreading. Observe when: the risk is bounded, evidence is thin, and watching reveals attacker infrastructure, goals, and full scope you'd otherwise miss (isolating one host of many tips off the attacker to burn the rest). The technical enabler is network containment via EDR, which cuts the host off from everything except your management channel — you get the safety of isolation and the visibility of observation. Tradeoff: every hour of observation is an hour of accepted risk and potential regulator scrutiny ("why didn't you act sooner?"). Decide with the IC, business owner, and Legal, and document the rationale in the timeline — the decision is defensible; an undocumented gut call is not.

3. Credential and session revocation (cloud-first). For the modern top incident — leaked cloud credentials — deactivating the key is necessary but insufficient, because the attacker likely already minted STS session tokens that outlive the key. The complete containment is: deactivate the key, attach an explicit Deny *, and add a Deny conditioned on aws:TokenIssueTime < now to instantly invalidate already-issued sessions (Azure: revoke refresh tokens / Revoke-AzureADUserAllRefreshToken; GCP: gcloud iam service-accounts keys delete + disable + audit). Then rotate every downstream secret the credential could have touched, because you must assume the attacker read them. Tradeoff: aggressive revocation can break legitimate automation mid-flight; that's acceptable during a confirmed compromise but argues for short-lived credentials by default (OIDC federation, IRSA/Workload Identity) so there's less to revoke. See Cloud-Incident-Response.

4. Eradication validation before recovery. The most common IR failure after the adrenaline fades is declaring victory while the attacker's persistence remains — a scheduled task, a rogue IAM user, a webshell, a modified SSH authorized_keys, a malicious Lambda. Eradication must be driven by root-cause analysis: you cannot remove what you haven't scoped, so eradication depends on a complete Detection & Analysis. Validate with an IOC sweep across the fleet (not just the known host), re-image rather than clean where feasible, and hunt for the persistence class specific to the platform. Recovery validation then confirms restored systems are clean and hardened (patched to close the entry vector) before returning to production, with heightened monitoring for re-entry. Tradeoff: re-imaging and validation cost downtime; the business pressure is always to restore fast — resist recovering onto an un-eradicated environment, which just restarts the clock (this is how ransomware victims get hit twice).

5. Blameless post-incident review with tracked corrective actions. The lifecycle only improves if Post-Incident Activity closes the loop. A blameless post-mortem assumes people acted reasonably given what they knew, and interrogates the system (why did the alert not fire? why was the key static? why did approval take 40 minutes?) rather than the individual. The output is not a document — it's a set of corrective actions with named owners and due dates, tracked to closure in a GRC/ticketing system, plus updates to detections and playbooks. Tradeoff: blamelessness can feel like a lack of accountability to executives; frame it as "we hold the system accountable and track fixes to done," and show the closed-action metrics. Metrics to trend across incidents: dwell time, MTTD, MTTR, and % of corrective actions closed on time. See Tabletop-Exercises for validating those fixes before the next real event.

Sample detections

Detection 1 — Sigma: AWS access key used from a new/anomalous location (credential compromise):

title: AWS API Call from Untrusted IP with Sensitive Action
logsource:
  product: aws
  service: cloudtrail
detection:
  selection:
    eventName:
      - GetObject
      - ListBuckets
      - CreateUser
      - AttachUserPolicy
  filter_known:
    sourceIPAddress|cidr:
      - 10.0.0.0/8        # corp
      - 52.94.0.0/16      # known AWS service ranges
  condition: selection and not filter_known
level: high
tags: [attack.t1078.004, attack.t1552.001]

Detection 2 — KQL (Sentinel): MFA fatigue / push-bombing followed by success:

SigninLogs
| where TimeGenerated > ago(1h)
| where ResultType in ("50074","500121")   // MFA denied / not completed
| summarize deniedPushes=count(), users=make_set(UserPrincipalName) by UserPrincipalName, bin(TimeGenerated, 10m)
| where deniedPushes >= 5
| join kind=inner (
    SigninLogs | where ResultType == "0" and AuthenticationRequirement == "multiFactorAuthentication"
) on UserPrincipalName
| project UserPrincipalName, deniedPushes, IPAddress, AppDisplayName

Detection 3 — SQL/Splunk-style: anomalous S3 egress volume (exfiltration):

SELECT userIdentity_arn, sourceIPAddress,
       COUNT(*) AS gets, SUM(bytesTransferredOut) AS bytes_out
FROM cloudtrail_data
WHERE eventName = 'GetObject' AND eventTime > now() - INTERVAL '1' HOUR
GROUP BY userIdentity_arn, sourceIPAddress
HAVING SUM(bytesTransferredOut) > 5e9   -- >5 GB/hr from one principal
ORDER BY bytes_out DESC;

Response playbook — compromised cloud credential (containment → eradication → recovery)

  1. Declare & assign — open the incident, set severity (SEV-1 if customer data at risk), assign IC/Scribe/SME, spin up the war-room channel, start the Legal clock.
  2. Short-term contain — deactivate the leaked key, attach Deny *, and revoke live sessions via aws:TokenIssueTime (do not delete the user yet — preserve attribution).
  3. Scope — CloudTrail lookup on the accessKeyId for the full active window; enumerate every API call, resource touched, and secondary credential/user created.
  4. Contain the blast radius — disable any IAM users/roles the attacker created (persistence, T1136), revoke their sessions too, block attacker source IPs.
  5. Preserve evidence — snapshot affected instances, export relevant CloudTrail to the WORM evidence vault, hash everything, record custody.
  6. Eradicate — remove attacker-created principals, policies, and backdoors; rotate every secret the credential could have read (assume all exposed); patch the entry vector (e.g., the leaked-key source in CI).
  7. Validate eradication — sweep for other keys from the same leak source; confirm no residual attacker access via a fresh CloudTrail baseline.
  8. Recover — restore/rebuild affected services from known-good state, re-enable legitimate automation with new short-lived credentials, apply heightened monitoring for re-entry (72h).
  9. Post-incident — blameless review; corrective actions (kill static keys, adopt OIDC, add the detection that would have caught it earlier) with owners + dates.

7. Practice Questions

Tier 1 — Fundamentals

  1. What are the phases of the NIST SP 800-61 incident response lifecycle?
    Preparation; Detection & Analysis; Containment, Eradication & Recovery; and Post-Incident Activity, arranged as a loop where lessons feed back into preparation. SANS expresses the same lifecycle as PICERL (Preparation, Identification, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Lessons Learned); NIST simply merges containment/eradication/recovery because they interleave in practice.
    Signal: knows the canonical model cold and that the two frameworks are the same thing.

  2. What's the difference between an event and an incident?
    An event is any observable occurrence in a system (a login, a packet, a process start) — most are benign. An incident is an event, or set of events, that violates security policy or threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of assets. Triage is the process of deciding which events rise to incidents, and the severity matrix then decides how loudly you respond.
    Signal: precision of vocabulary; understands triage exists.

  3. What is the role of an Incident Commander?
    The IC owns the incident (not the fix): they hold decision authority, coordinate roles, control communication cadence, and keep altitude while SMEs do hands-on remediation. Crucially the IC should not also be typing commands — separating "deciding" from "doing" prevents the response from losing coordination. The role rotates and is explicitly about command and communication.
    Signal: understands command structure, not just tools.

  4. Why is chain of custody important, and how do you maintain it?
    Chain of custody is the documented, unbroken record of who handled evidence, when, and how — it makes evidence defensible in legal/regulatory proceedings and proves it wasn't altered. You maintain it by hashing artifacts at acquisition (and after copies), storing them write-once (S3 Object Lock/WORM), and logging every access with timestamps and identities. Break it and the evidence — and your findings — can be challenged.
    Signal: knows IR has a legal dimension, not just technical.

  5. What is short-term vs. long-term containment?
    Short-term containment is the immediate stop-the-bleeding action — isolate the host, disable the account, block the IP — accepting a rough temporary state. Long-term containment is the durable fix that lets you safely recover — rebuild clean systems, re-segment the network, replace the credential model. You do short-term first to limit damage, then long-term as part of the path to recovery.
    Signal: understands containment is two-staged, not one action.

Tier 2 — System design & trade-offs

  1. When would you observe an attacker rather than immediately contain?
    You observe when the risk is bounded and the intelligence value is high: watching reveals the attacker's full scope, tooling, C2 infrastructure, and objectives that you'd lose by tipping them off — isolating one host of ten may cause them to burn the other nine before you find them. You immediately contain when sensitive data is actively exfiltrating, availability is degrading, ransomware is spreading, or regulators would question any delay. The modern resolution is EDR network-containment, which isolates the host from everything except your management channel, giving both safety and visibility. Whatever you choose, the IC documents the rationale in the timeline, because an undocumented delay is indefensible later.
    Signal: can reason about the core IR tradeoff and knows the technical enabler.

  2. Design a severity classification scheme for a large SaaS company. What drives it?
    Severity should be a function of impact (confidentiality/integrity/availability, plus regulatory and reputational exposure) times scope (one host vs. a whole tenant vs. all customers). I'd use SEV-1 (active widespread compromise or confirmed customer-data breach — page execs, crisis bridge, 30-min cadence) down to SEV-4 (isolated policy violation auto-remediated — ticket only). The severity then drives everything downstream: who gets paged, comms cadence, whether Legal is pulled in for notification clocks, and whether the crisis-management process activates. I'd make it deterministic with examples so a tired on-call at 3am classifies consistently, and allow the IC to escalate but require justification to de-escalate.
    Signal: connects taxonomy to operational consequences; avoids gut-feel severity.

  3. How would you structure incident communications for a SEV-1 with customer impact?
    Separate audiences and cadences: an internal technical channel (the war room), a stakeholder/exec update on a fixed cadence (e.g., every 30 minutes even if "no change"), and an external track owned by PR/Legal for customers and regulators. I'd pull Legal in at declaration to start and track notification clocks (GDPR 72h, SEC 4-day materiality) and to consider attorney–client privilege over work product. Communications must be out-of-band — if the corporate network/email may be compromised, the response coordination cannot depend on it (attackers have read IR plans mid-incident). The Comms lead, not the IC or SMEs, owns external messaging so responders stay focused.
    Signal: understands comms is a first-class workstream with legal and OOB dimensions.

  4. Your team keeps having repeat incidents of the same type. What's broken in the lifecycle?
    The Post-Incident Activity loop isn't closing. Either post-mortems aren't happening, they're blameful (so people hide root causes), or — most commonly — corrective actions are identified but never tracked to closure with owners and due dates. I'd instrument it: every post-mortem produces action items in a tracked system, I'd report the on-time-closure rate to leadership, and I'd validate that the fixes actually work via tabletops and purple-teaming before the next real event. Repeat incidents are a governance/feedback failure, not a detection failure — the same detection gap keeps recurring because nobody owned closing it.
    Signal: sees IR as a learning system; diagnoses process not tools.

  5. How does incident response differ in the cloud versus on-prem?
    The unit of compromise shifts from "a host" to "a credential/identity and its blast radius." Containment is API-driven and fast (deactivate a key, attach a deny, revoke sessions) but you must remember STS/refresh tokens outlive the key, so session revocation is a distinct step. Evidence is ephemeral (instances autoscale away) so you snapshot aggressively and rely on control-plane logs (CloudTrail/Activity Logs) rather than disk. The shared-responsibility model means some layers are the provider's to investigate, and detections must be cloud-native (GuardDuty, anomalous API patterns) because on-prem SIEM rules don't see the control plane. See Cloud-Incident-Response.
    Signal: understands the paradigm shift, not just "same but in AWS."

Tier 3 — Expert & deep-dive

  1. Walk me through responding to a leaked long-lived AWS access key, step by step.
    Declare and severity-rate based on what the key could reach. Contain without destroying attribution: deactivate (not delete) the key, attach an explicit Deny *, and add a deny conditioned on aws:TokenIssueTime < now to kill already-issued STS sessions the attacker holds. Scope via cloudtrail lookup-events on the accessKeyId for the full active window — enumerate every action and, critically, any new IAM users/roles the attacker created for persistence (T1136). Disable those and revoke their sessions too. Preserve evidence (snapshots, CloudTrail export to WORM, hashes). Eradicate: remove attacker artifacts and rotate every secret the key could have read, since you must assume they were exfiltrated; patch the leak source (e.g., the key committed to a repo/CI). Validate no residual access, recover onto short-lived credentials (OIDC/IRSA), and monitor for re-entry. Post-mortem action: eliminate static keys entirely.
    Signal: this is the flagship modern IR scenario — depth and the session-revocation nuance separate seniors from juniors.

  2. Why is eradication so often incomplete, and how do you guarantee it isn't?
    Because eradication depends on scope, and scope is hard — you can only remove persistence you've discovered, so an incomplete Detection & Analysis guarantees an incomplete eradication. Attackers plant multiple, diverse persistence mechanisms (scheduled tasks, rogue accounts, webshells, modified startup scripts, malicious OAuth grants) precisely so that removing the obvious one leaves a door open. To guarantee it, I drive eradication from documented root cause, sweep the entire fleet for IOCs rather than the one known host, prefer re-imaging over cleaning (you can't be sure you found everything on a live box), and hunt specifically for the persistence classes relevant to the platform. Then I validate with a fresh telemetry baseline and heightened monitoring before recovery — because recovering onto an un-eradicated environment simply restarts the incident (how ransomware victims get re-encrypted days later).
    Signal: understands the eradication↔analysis dependency and the re-image-vs-clean judgment.

  3. How do you preserve forensic value while responding under time pressure to restore service?
    This is the fundamental IR tension: the business wants uptime now; forensics wants an untouched crime scene. I resolve it with order of volatility — capture volatile evidence first (memory, running connections, then disk, then logs) before any remediation touches the box, because rebooting or re-imaging destroys memory-resident artifacts forever. Snapshot instances/volumes before you clean them so you can restore service from a rebuild while preserving the original for analysis. Everything gets hashed at acquisition and stored WORM with a custody log. In the cloud this is easier — snapshot and let autoscaling replace the instance — so you recover fast and keep evidence. The key discipline is that acquisition precedes remediation; you can restore in parallel from a copy, but you never remediate the only copy of the evidence. See DFIR-Forensic-Process.
    Signal: knows order of volatility and how to parallelize restore vs. preserve.

  4. How would you build IR automation with SOAR without it causing an incident of its own?
    Automate the high-confidence, low-blast-radius, reversible actions first: enrichment (pull CloudTrail context, geolocate an IP, check threat intel), ticket creation, and evidence collection are safe to fully automate because they're read-only. Destructive containment (isolate host, deactivate key, disable account) I gate behind either high-confidence detections plus a human approval step, or tight scoping conditions — because a false positive that auto-isolates a fleet is a self-inflicted outage. I'd version-control playbooks, test them in a staging tenant, and add circuit-breakers (e.g., "don't auto-isolate more than N hosts without human sign-off"). The design principle: automate to reduce responder toil and latency, but keep a human in the loop for actions that are hard to reverse, and make every automated action logged and reversible. See SOAR-and-Playbooks.
    Signal: balances automation leverage against the risk of automated over-response.

  5. A regulator asks why it took your team 6 hours to contain after detection. How do you defend the timeline, and what would you have logged to make that defensible?
    I'd defend it with the Scribe's timeline showing that the "delay" was a deliberate, documented decision: e.g., the first two hours were scoping to avoid tipping off the attacker before we understood the full footprint, with the IC, business owner, and Legal signing off on the observe-then-contain strategy at a recorded timestamp. The defensibility comes entirely from Preparation: a Scribe capturing every decision and its rationale in real time, severity classification that justified the response level, and evidence that containment actions began the moment the risk crossed our documented threshold. If instead the six hours were dead time — no owner, no decisions logged — there's no defense, which is exactly why role separation (a dedicated Scribe) and a pre-agreed containment-decision framework are non-negotiable. The regulator isn't punishing a considered delay; they're punishing chaos and undocumented negligence.
    Signal: connects IR process artifacts to legal defensibility; understands that documentation is the deliverable.

9. Key Takeaways

  • NIST SP 800-61 (4 phases) and SANS PICERL (6 steps) are the same lifecycle — a loop, not a line, where Post-Incident Activity feeds back into Preparation.
  • Preparation is where incidents are won or lost — telemetry with retention, immutable logging, pre-provisioned access, defined roles, and rehearsed playbooks all have to exist before the alert fires.
  • Separate deciding from doing — the Incident Commander commands and communicates; SMEs remediate; a Scribe captures the authoritative, defensible timeline.
  • Severity drives everything downstream — paging, comms cadence, Legal involvement, and crisis activation all flow from a deterministic impact × scope matrix.
  • Containment is a real tradeoff — isolate-now protects assets but destroys intel and tips off the attacker; EDR network-containment gives you both safety and visibility; document the rationale either way.
  • In the cloud, contain the identity and its sessions, not just the key — STS/refresh tokens outlive the credential, so session revocation is a distinct, mandatory step.
  • Eradication depends on scope — you can't remove persistence you never found; sweep the whole fleet, prefer re-imaging, and validate before recovery or you'll be re-compromised.
  • Chain of custody and order of volatility make evidence defensible — hash on acquire, store WORM, capture memory before disk before remediation.
  • Blameless post-mortems with tracked corrective actions are the learning engine — repeat incidents mean the feedback loop, not the detection, is broken.
  • Regulatory clocks start at detection — GDPR 72h and SEC 4-day materiality mean Legal belongs in the war room at declaration, not after.
