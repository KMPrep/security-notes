Incident Management

Category: Security Operations, Incident Response, Crisis Management

Overview

Incident management is the structured process of detecting, containing, eradicating, and recovering from security incidents, while preserving evidence and communicating effectively. It requires both technical depth and organisational coordination.

The goal is not just to stop the immediate attack but to:

  1. Understand the full scope and root cause
  2. Ensure the attacker is fully evicted
  3. Prevent recurrence
  4. Maintain trust with users and stakeholders

At large scale, incidents affect many teams simultaneously. Effective incident management requires clear roles, pre-defined playbooks, a common communication channel, and the discipline to document decisions in real time.

Privacy Incidents vs Information Security Incidents

Type Definition Key Difference Who Gets Involved
Information Security Incident Unauthorised access, disruption, or destruction of systems or data Scope: system availability, integrity, confidentiality Security, Engineering, Operations
Privacy Incident Actual or potential exposure of personal identifiable information (PII) Regulatory consequences; legal obligations to notify affected users Security, Legal, Privacy, Compliance, Communications

Key rule: All privacy incidents are security incidents, but not all security incidents are privacy incidents. A server crash is a security incident (availability). A database breach exposing user emails is both.

Regulatory triggers for notification:

  • GDPR: Report to supervisory authority within 72 hours of awareness; notify affected users if high risk
  • CCPA, HIPAA, PCI DSS: Similar breach notification requirements with varying timelines

Incident Response Models

SANS PICERL

The most widely used IR framework:

flowchart LR
    P[Preparation] --> I[Identification]
    I --> C[Containment]
    C --> E[Eradication]
    E --> R[Recovery]
    R --> L[Lessons Learned]
    L -->|Update playbooks| P
Phase Goal Key Actions
Preparation Be ready before an incident Build playbooks, deploy logging, train responders, run tabletop exercises
Identification Detect and confirm the incident Triage alert, determine scope, classify severity
Containment Stop the bleeding Isolate affected systems, revoke credentials, block attacker IPs
Eradication Remove the attacker Delete malware, close persistence mechanisms, patch exploited vulnerabilities
Recovery Restore normal operations Rebuild systems from clean state, restore from backup, monitor for recurrence
Lessons Learned Improve for next time Post-incident review, root cause analysis, update playbooks

Google IMAG (Incident Management At Google)

Google's internal framework emphasises:

  • Incident Commander (IC): Single decision-maker with authority to direct response. Rotated as incident shifts through phases.
  • Separate communication and technical channels: Ops channel (live decision-making); incident doc (permanent record)
  • Scribe: Dedicated person documents everything in real time — decisions, hypotheses, actions, timestamps
  • Handover notes: Explicit written handover when IC changes, ensuring continuity
  • Blameless post-mortems: Focus on systemic improvements, not individual blame

Incident Severity Classification

Define severity tiers before incidents happen. Tiers drive response speed, escalation, and resources.

Severity Definition Example Response SLA
P0 / Critical Active breach with ongoing data exfiltration or system compromise Ransomware spreading across fleet Immediate; all-hands
P1 / High Confirmed breach; attacker present but contained Admin account compromised; no lateral movement yet < 1 hour to full response
P2 / Medium Suspected compromise; investigation ongoing Unusual login pattern; possible credential theft < 4 hours
P3 / Low Policy violation or minor security event Employee accessing data outside their role Next business day

Incident Commander Role

The IC owns the incident from start to close. Responsibilities:

  • Declare the incident severity and activate the response team
  • Make containment decisions under uncertainty
  • Manage communication cadence (internal stakeholders, executives, legal)
  • Avoid analysis paralysis — make decisions with incomplete information
  • Know when to stop an attack vs when to watch (watching attacker to gather intel may be valuable briefly; extended watching risks more damage)
  • Know when to involve law enforcement or external IR firms

When NOT to be the IC: If you are the technical lead debugging the root cause, you cannot also be the IC. Split roles explicitly.

Roles in an Incident

Role Responsibility
Incident Commander (IC) Overall decision authority; drives timeline; manages escalation
Technical Lead Leads investigation and eradication; reports findings to IC
Scribe Documents everything in real time; maintains incident timeline
Communications Lead Manages internal comms (exec updates) and external comms (users, press) if needed
Legal / Privacy Counsel Advises on notification obligations; determines if law enforcement involvement required
Operations / SRE Executes containment actions (isolate host, revoke creds, reimage)
Forensics Preserves evidence; performs deep investigation

Communication During Incidents

Internal Communication

Audience What They Need Frequency
Exec / Leadership Business impact, scope, ETA to resolution Hourly for P0/P1; every few hours for P2
Legal / Privacy Any PII involved; regulatory trigger assessment As soon as PII exposure is confirmed
Engineering teams Specific actions required; technical details As needed for containment/eradication tasks
PR / Communications Assess if public disclosure is needed Before any external statement

External Communication

Situation Action
PII exposure meeting regulatory threshold Notify regulatory authority within required window (e.g., 72h for GDPR)
User data exposed Notify affected users per legal/privacy guidance
Public attack targeting product Coordinate response statement with PR and Legal before publishing
Third-party supplier breached Notify your legal/procurement; assess your exposure

Rule: Never make public statements about an active incident without Legal and PR review. Premature disclosure can tip off the attacker and create legal liability.

Containment Decisions

Containment trades investigation completeness for reduced damage. This is a judgment call:

Scenario Watch vs Contain Rationale
Attacker is exfiltrating live data Contain immediately Data loss > intelligence value
Attacker is present but idle, and you have full visibility Brief watch (< 1 hour) Gather TTPs, identify full scope
Attacker may have multiple footholds Map all footholds before eviction Evicting one foothold tips off attacker; they activate backup
Ransomware encryption has started Contain immediately; network isolate all hosts Stop spread; every minute costs more hosts
Unknown initial access vector Investigate before recovery If you recover without finding the hole, attacker re-enters

Containment actions by layer:

Layer Action
Network Isolate host to VLAN with IR team access only; block attacker IPs at firewall
Identity Revoke compromised credentials; disable accounts; rotate secrets and API keys
Endpoint Quarantine via EDR; block process execution; prevent network access
Cloud Revoke IAM keys; detach IAM roles; snapshot and isolate VMs
Application Disable affected API endpoints; invalidate session tokens; rotate signing keys

Eradication

Remove every attacker foothold:

Attacker Technique Eradication Action
Malware on host Reimage from known-good baseline (do not trust AV-cleaned machines)
Persistence via scheduled task Remove task; audit all scheduled tasks on the fleet
Backdoor user account Delete; audit all accounts created in the incident window
Stolen credentials used elsewhere Rotate all affected credentials; audit their usage history
Exploited vulnerability Patch immediately; scan fleet for same vulnerability
Supply chain compromise Remove malicious dependency; audit all direct and transitive dependencies
Compromised CI/CD pipeline Rebuild pipeline from scratch; rotate all signing keys

Never eradicate before forensics: If you reimage before preserving evidence, root cause analysis becomes impossible.

Recovery

Recovery is not done when systems are back online. It is done when:

  • All attacker footholds are confirmed removed
  • Monitoring is elevated to detect recurrence
  • Root cause is understood and patched
  • Stakeholder sign-off is received

Recovery checklist:

  • [ ] Systems rebuilt from known-good baseline (not cleaned)
  • [ ] All credentials rotated (not just the ones confirmed stolen)
  • [ ] Vulnerability exploited in initial access is patched
  • [ ] Elevated monitoring running for at least 30 days post-recovery
  • [ ] Affected users notified (if required)
  • [ ] Post-incident review scheduled within 5 business days

Post-Incident Review (Lessons Learned)

A blameless post-incident review produces:

  1. Timeline of events: What happened, when, and in what order
  2. Root cause: The technical and organisational factors that enabled the incident
  3. Contributing factors: What made it worse or harder to detect
  4. Action items with owners and due dates: Specific, trackable improvements
  5. Detection gap analysis: Why wasn't this caught sooner?

Good post-mortem questions:

  • What was the first detection signal and why did it take as long as it did to act?
  • Were playbooks used? Did they help or were they out of date?
  • What information did we lack that slowed the response?
  • What would have prevented this incident entirely?

Playbooks

A playbook is a documented, step-by-step response procedure for a specific alert type or incident scenario. Playbooks:

  • Reduce decision paralysis during high-stress incidents
  • Ensure consistent response quality across team members
  • Document what evidence to preserve and what actions to take in what order
  • Include escalation criteria ("if X, escalate to P1")

Example playbook structure for ransomware:

  1. Confirm ransomware (check file extensions, ransom note)
  2. Immediately isolate affected hosts from the network
  3. Determine initial access vector (phishing email, exposed RDP, vulnerable service)
  4. Identify scope — which hosts are encrypted, which are not yet but may be reachable
  5. Preserve evidence (memory dump and disk image of one affected and one unaffected host)
  6. Notify IC; escalate to P0
  7. Do not pay ransom without legal approval
  8. Initiate recovery from backup — confirm backups are not also encrypted
  9. Patch initial access vector before recovery
  10. Post-incident review within 3 business days

Practice Questions

  • Walk me through how you would handle a ransomware incident from detection to recovery.
  • What is the difference between containment and eradication? Why must they be done in order?
  • When should you watch an attacker instead of immediately evicting them?
  • How do you decide when to involve law enforcement in an incident?
  • What is the Incident Commander role? What decisions can only they make?
  • A database containing 10 million user records was accessed by an unauthorised party. What are your immediate steps, and what are your legal obligations?
  • How do post-incident reviews improve future response? What makes them effective vs ineffective?
  • An attacker has compromised your CI/CD pipeline. Walk through your containment and eradication steps.
