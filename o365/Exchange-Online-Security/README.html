Exchange Online Security

Category: Microsoft 365 — Email & Messaging Security
Key standards / frameworks: RFC 7208 (SPF), RFC 6376 (DKIM), RFC 7489 (DMARC), NIST SP 800-177 (Trustworthy Email), CIS Microsoft 365 Foundations, MITRE ATT&CK T1114 (Email Collection), T1137 (Office Application Startup / rules), T1534 (Internal Spearphishing)

Overview

Exchange Online is the M365 mail workload and, because email is the primary channel for phishing and business email compromise (BEC), one of the most attacked surfaces in the tenant. Its security spans mail flow (how messages enter, route, and leave), mailbox access and permissions, the automation surface of transport and inbox rules, and the OAuth applications that can read mail through Microsoft Graph.

Exchange Online Protection (EOP) provides the baseline anti-spam and anti-malware layer for every mailbox, with Defender for Office 365 adding detonation and impersonation protection on top. But many of the highest-impact incidents are not malware — they are identity-driven: an attacker who owns a mailbox creates hidden inbox rules to auto-forward and delete, adds delegate or Send-As permissions for persistence, or consents an OAuth app with Mail.Read to exfiltrate mail quietly. Email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) underpins anti-spoofing but must be configured and enforced. See also the web/Email-Security notes for the authentication mechanics.

How It Works

Mail flow and connectors

Inbound mail arrives at EOP, which applies connection filtering, anti-malware, anti-spam, and (with Defender) Safe Attachments and Safe Links before delivery. Connectors define trusted mail paths to and from partner organizations or on-premises hybrid servers; a misconfigured or overly permissive inbound connector can let an attacker relay spoofed internal mail, and a rogue outbound connector can silently exfiltrate or reroute mail. Connectors and their scoping are a frequent audit finding.

Transport rules and mail-flow abuse

Transport (mail-flow) rules act on messages organization-wide: adding disclaimers, redirecting, blocking, or bypassing filtering. Attackers with admin access weaponize them — a rule that BCCs all mail to an external address, or that whitelists the attacker's domain past spam filtering, is a stealthy persistence and exfiltration technique. Rules that bypass Safe Links or set SCL to -1 for a sender should be treated as high-severity findings.

Mailbox permissions and delegate abuse

Exchange grants access three ways: Full Access (read the mailbox), Send-As (send as the mailbox identity), and Send-on-Behalf. Attackers abuse these for persistence and impersonation: granting Send-As on an executive mailbox enables convincing internal BEC, while Full Access on shared or delegate mailboxes provides durable read access that survives a password reset. These grants are logged in the Unified Audit Log via operations like Add-MailboxPermission and Add-RecipientPermission.

Malicious inbox rules and auto-forwarding

After a mailbox takeover, the classic BEC playbook is an inbox rule that moves or deletes messages containing keywords such as "invoice", "payment", or "wire", and forwards them externally — hiding the attacker's fraudulent thread from the victim. Auto-forwarding (client rules, mailbox forwarding SMTP address, or transport rules) exfiltrates mail continuously. Microsoft blocks automatic external forwarding by default via the anti-spam outbound policy, but exceptions and per-mailbox forwarding can re-open the hole.

OAuth application access to mailboxes

Beyond interactive sign-in, applications can read mail through Microsoft Graph. Delegated permissions act as the signed-in user; application permissions (like Mail.Read or Mail.ReadWrite granted app-only) let a service principal read any mailbox in the tenant with no user present. Consent phishing tricks a user into approving a malicious multi-tenant app, granting durable, MFA-independent, token-based mailbox access that persists until the consent is revoked. Application Access Policies can scope app permissions to specific mailboxes.

Email authentication and Defender integration

SPF authorizes sending IPs, DKIM signs messages cryptographically, and DMARC ties them to the visible From domain with a reject/quarantine policy and reporting. Enforced DMARC (p=reject) blocks exact-domain spoofing; EOP and Defender for Office 365 evaluate these results plus spoof intelligence and impersonation heuristics to catch lookalike and cousin-domain attacks that authentication alone cannot. See the web/Email-Security notes for the protocol details.

Detection & Telemetry

The Unified Audit Log records the highest-signal Exchange operations: New-InboxRule and Set-InboxRule (forwarding rules), Set-Mailbox with a ForwardingSmtpAddress, Add-MailboxPermission / Add-RecipientPermission (delegate and Send-As), New-TransportRule, and New-InboundConnector. Entra ID sign-in logs and consent audit events flag OAuth app grants and risky sign-ins that precede rule creation. Defender for Office 365 raises alerts for suspicious forwarding, "Email messages containing malicious URL removed after delivery" (ZAP), and BEC-related mailbox activity; Defender XDR correlates an AiTM sign-in with subsequent inbox-rule creation into a single incident. Alert specifically on new external forwarding rules and on app-only Mail.Read consents.

Key Takeaways

  • Exchange Online is a top attack surface; most high-impact incidents are identity-driven BEC, not malware.
  • Hidden auto-forwarding inbox rules are the signature BEC persistence and exfiltration technique — block external forwarding and alert on rule creation.
  • Full Access and Send-As delegation enable durable access and internal impersonation; keep them least-privilege and audited.
  • OAuth apps with application-level Mail.Read read any mailbox with no user and no MFA — restrict user consent and govern enterprise apps.
  • Transport rules and connectors are powerful org-wide controls attackers weaponize for exfiltration and spoofing; alert on changes.
  • Enforce SPF, DKIM, and DMARC p=reject to stop exact-domain spoofing; Defender adds impersonation and lookalike coverage.
  • Disable legacy SMTP AUTH and block legacy authentication to close MFA-bypassing mailbox access.
