Microsoft Teams Security

Category: Microsoft 365 Collaboration / Communication Security
Key standards / frameworks: Microsoft Purview, Entra ID External Identities, CIS Microsoft 365 Foundations Benchmark, NIST 800-53 (AC-4, SC-7), MITRE ATT&CK T1566 (Phishing), T1585 (Establish Accounts)

Overview

Microsoft Teams is a collaboration front end, not a self-contained data store. A team is fundamentally a Microsoft 365 Group, and every message, file, and meeting artifact is persisted in an underlying workload: standard channel files live in a SharePoint site, private and shared channel files live in their own SharePoint sites, one-to-one and group chat attachments live in the sender's OneDrive, and chat and channel messages plus meeting metadata are stored in a hidden Exchange Online mailbox. Securing Teams therefore means securing both the Teams control surface and the SharePoint, OneDrive, and Exchange data plane behind it.

The dominant risks are external communication and identity. Teams offers two distinct external doorways with very different trust models, and confusing them is a common and dangerous misconfiguration. On top of that, chat has become a phishing and malware channel that many organizations monitor far less rigorously than email.

How It Works

Guest Access Versus External Access

Teams exposes two separate external mechanisms. Guest access brings an external person into your tenant as a B2B guest identity in Entra ID and adds them as a member of a specific team, giving them broad membership rights inside that team including channels, files, and apps. External access, also called federation, does not create an account in your tenant at all; it lets your users chat and meet with people in other Microsoft 365 or Teams domains as their own home identity. Guest access is deeper and account-based, while external access is lighter, domain-federation based, and can be scoped by allow-list or block-list of domains. A newer capability, external access with individual accounts, extends federation to Teams personal accounts and must be governed carefully.

The Data Plane Behind Teams

Because Teams stores content in other workloads, governance policies must be applied where the data lives. Sensitivity labels can be applied to the Team container to control guest access, privacy, and unmanaged-device behavior, while DLP for Teams inspects chat and channel messages in near real time and can block sensitive content or override sharing. Retention policies for Teams operate on the substrate mailboxes and SharePoint sites rather than on a visible mailbox.

Apps Bots and Meetings

Teams apps and bots request Microsoft Graph permissions and can read messages, roster, and files depending on consent, so an over-permissioned or malicious app is a data-access risk governed by app permission policies and admin consent. Meeting security is controlled through meeting policies and per-meeting options such as lobby admission, who can present, and whether anonymous participants may join.

Detection & Telemetry

The Unified Audit Log records Teams operations including MemberAdded, TeamCreated, MessageSent, and app and bot install events, plus meeting participation records. Entra ID audit and sign-in logs show guest invitations and external sign-ins, while Defender XDR surfaces Safe Links click alerts and suspicious app consent. Purview DLP and Communication Compliance raise policy matches on message content, and Defender for Cloud Apps flags anomalous external file sharing that originated in Teams.

Key Takeaways

  • Teams is a front end; its data actually lives in SharePoint, OneDrive, and Exchange, so governance must be applied to those workloads.
  • Guest access adds an account to a team while external access is domain federation with no account; treat them as separate trust decisions.
  • Restrict federation to an allow-list of trusted domains to shut down chat-based phishing from arbitrary tenants.
  • Extend Safe Attachments and Safe Links to Teams so chat is not a monitoring blind spot next to email.
  • DLP and Communication Compliance for Teams give a control point over sensitive and inappropriate content in messages.
  • App and bot consent is a data-access path through Graph; restrict sideloading and require admin consent.
  • Lobby settings and Group lifecycle policies close the two quiet gaps: uninvited meeting guests and orphaned teams full of stale data.
