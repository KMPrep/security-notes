The core challenge is key distribution — how do both parties get the same key without an attacker intercepting it? In practice, symmetric keys are typically established via asymmetric key exchange (e.g., ECDHE in TLS).

Domain-specific points I would make

  • Separate authentication strength from session assurance: a strong login can still be defeated by weak recovery, stale sessions, or stolen cookies.
  • Prefer phishing-resistant authenticators for privileged access and remove weak fallback paths such as SMS recovery for high-value users.
  • Rotate session identifiers after authentication and require recent strong authentication for sensitive actions.

Controls I would design or verify

  • standard audited primitives rather than custom crypto
  • AEAD for confidentiality plus integrity, safe nonce handling, and strong CSPRNG use
  • short-lived certificates, automated rotation, revocation, and CT-style monitoring
  • forward secrecy and authenticated key exchange for transport protocols

Detection, response, and scale considerations

  • key use outside expected service, cert misissuance, weak algorithms, expiry risk
  • signature-validation failures and anomalous KMS decrypt volume

At production scale, I would also ask: who owns the control, where the policy is enforced, how exceptions expire, how drift is detected, and what happens during an outage or partial failure. The strongest answers usually call out the tradeoff explicitly: security posture improves only if the control is deployable, observable, and supportable by the teams that operate it.

Closer

My closing answer would be: start from the threat model, choose the control closest to the trust boundary, verify it with telemetry, and define residual risk honestly. I would avoid claiming any single control is sufficient; mature security engineering layers prevention, detection, response, and governance so one mistake does not become a systemic breach.
