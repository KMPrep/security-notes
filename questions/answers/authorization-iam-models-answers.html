enterprise data protection Authorization and IAM Models - Senior Security Engineer Answers

Source: Hour 4 - Authorization and IAM Models from 50-questions-per-hour.md
Perspective: Senior security engineer, AWS-style enterprise design review
Status: Complete
Checkpoint: Questions 1-50 drafted and validated

How To Use These Answers

Use each answer as a scaffold, not a script. A strong senior authorization answer should distinguish identity from permission, name the enforcement point, discuss object and tenant boundaries, cover failure modes, and close with auditability and residual risk.

Chunk 1 - Questions 1-10

1. Explain the difference between authentication and authorization using an enterprise example.

Authentication answers, Who are you? Authorization answers, What are you allowed to do? They are related, but they must be designed and tested separately.

Enterprise example:

Authentication:
  Alice signs in through the corporate IdP with a passkey.

Authorization:
  The payroll app checks whether Alice can view payroll records for her region.

Alice being authenticated does not mean Alice can access every payroll record. The app still needs to check role, team, region, object ownership, data sensitivity, and action type.

flowchart TD
    A[User signs in] --> B[Authentication succeeds]
    B --> C[Request resource action]
    C --> D[Authorization check]
    D --> E{Allowed?}
    E -- Yes --> F[Permit]
    E -- No --> G[Deny and log]

Common mistake: treating a valid SSO session or JWT as enough to authorize access. A token may prove identity or contain useful claims, but the application must still enforce policy for each sensitive action and resource.

AWS example: authenticating to AWS through IAM Identity Center proves the user is Alice. Authorization is the permission set, IAM policy, SCP, resource policy, session policy, and condition evaluation that decides whether Alice can call s3:GetObject on a specific bucket object.

2. When does RBAC break down in a large organization?

RBAC breaks down when role count, role scope, or business complexity outgrows simple role bundles. It starts cleanly, but large organizations accumulate exceptions, inherited roles, regional variants, project-specific roles, and stale assignments.

Failure modes:

  • Role explosion: thousands of roles with unclear ownership.
  • Overbroad roles: Admin, PowerUser, or Manager grants too much.
  • Hidden inheritance: reviewers cannot see effective permissions.
  • Stale access: movers and departed users keep old roles.
  • Cross-tenant scope mistakes: global role used where tenant-scoped role was required.
  • Toxic combinations: one person can request, approve, and execute a sensitive action.
  • Review fatigue: access reviews become rubber stamps.
  • Role names drift away from actual permissions.

RBAC works best when job functions are stable and permissions can be grouped naturally. It struggles when access depends on dynamic context such as data classification, region, device posture, relationship to an object, time, tenant, or request risk.

Mitigations:

  • Use scoped roles, not global roles by default.
  • Review effective permissions, not just role names.
  • Add ABAC conditions for context such as region, data label, and device trust.
  • Use ReBAC for collaboration and sharing relationships.
  • Use JIT access for privileged roles.
  • Automate joiner-mover-leaver cleanup.

Senior answer: RBAC fails less because the model is bad and more because governance does not keep role design, ownership, scope, and lifecycle under control.

3. Compare RBAC, ABAC, and ReBAC.

RBAC, ABAC, and ReBAC are different authorization models optimized for different access patterns.

Model Decision basis Best fit Main risk
RBAC Principal has role Enterprise job functions and admin roles Role explosion and overbroad roles
ABAC Subject, resource, action, and environment attributes Context-aware policy and data sensitivity Bad attributes and policy complexity
ReBAC Relationship graph between subject and resource Sharing, collaboration, hierarchy, ownership Hidden inheritance and graph complexity

Examples:

RBAC: User has BillingAdmin role.
ABAC: User in Finance can read invoices tagged region=US from managed device.
ReBAC: User can edit document because they are member of group that owns folder.

flowchart TD
    A[Access request] --> B{Policy model}
    B --> C[RBAC checks role]
    B --> D[ABAC checks attributes]
    B --> E[ReBAC checks relationships]
    C --> F[Decision]
    D --> F
    E --> F

In real systems, these models are often combined. A document platform might use RBAC for admin roles, ReBAC for document sharing, and ABAC for data classification or device posture. A cloud platform might use role assignments plus resource tags and conditions.

Senior answer: RBAC is easiest to operate, ABAC is most expressive for contextual rules, and ReBAC is strongest for graph-shaped access. The right choice depends on the product domain and operational maturity.

4. What is Zanzibar-style authorization and why is it useful?

Zanzibar-style authorization is a relationship-based authorization model inspired by Google's Zanzibar system. It models access as tuples such as user Alice is viewer of document X or group TeamA is editor of folder Y, then answers authorization checks by traversing direct and inherited relationships.

Tuple model:

document:roadmap#viewer@user:alice
folder:plans#editor@group:eng
group:eng#member@user:bob

Useful properties:

  • Works well for collaboration products.
  • Represents direct sharing and inherited permissions.
  • Supports group membership and resource hierarchy.
  • Centralizes authorization checks across services.
  • Can provide explainability for why access was granted.
  • Scales better than embedding sharing logic in every service.
flowchart TD
    A[User requests document] --> B[Check relation tuple]
    B --> C{Direct relation?}
    C -- Yes --> D[Permit]
    C -- No --> E[Traverse group or parent]
    E --> F{Inherited relation?}
    F -- Yes --> D
    F -- No --> G[Deny]

Challenges:

  • Schema design is security-critical.
  • Revocation freshness matters.
  • Recursive checks can be expensive.
  • Cross-tenant relationships must be impossible by design.
  • Effective permission explanations are required for support and audit.

Senior answer: Zanzibar-style systems are useful when access depends on relationships among users, groups, folders, documents, organizations, and tenants. They are powerful for sharing systems, but the relation schema and consistency model must be threat-modeled carefully.

5. Design an authorization system for a document-sharing platform.

I would design a document-sharing authorization system around object-level checks, relationship inheritance, tenant boundaries, and data sensitivity.

Core entities:

  • User.
  • Group.
  • Tenant or organization.
  • Folder.
  • Document.
  • Link share.
  • Role or relation such as owner, editor, commenter, viewer.

Authorization model:

  • Use ReBAC for document and folder sharing.
  • Use RBAC for administrative tenant roles.
  • Use ABAC for data classification, device trust, region, and external sharing policy.
  • Enforce tenant boundary in every object key and relationship tuple.
flowchart TD
    A[Request document action] --> B[Identify user and tenant]
    B --> C[Load document metadata]
    C --> D[Check tenant boundary]
    D --> E[Evaluate relation]
    E --> F[Apply classification policy]
    F --> G{Permit?}
    G -- Yes --> H[Allow and audit]
    G -- No --> I[Deny and log]

Controls:

  • Object-level authorization on every document access.
  • Inherited access from folders with explainability.
  • Link sharing with expiration, scope, and external-domain controls.
  • Separate view, download, share, edit, delete, and transfer ownership actions.
  • Step-up for external sharing, bulk export, or ownership transfer.
  • Audit trails for reads, downloads, shares, permission changes, and link creation.
  • DLP checks for sensitive labels before external access.
  • Revocation that propagates quickly for removed users or groups.

Failure modes:

  • Document ID guessed and accessed without object check.
  • Folder inheritance unexpectedly exposes sensitive docs.
  • Public link bypasses tenant policy.
  • External user remains after project ends.
  • Search index returns documents the user cannot open.

6. Design an authorization system for a multi-tenant SaaS application.

The highest priority in multi-tenant SaaS authorization is tenant isolation. Every request must be scoped to the tenant, and no user-controlled input should be allowed to select another tenant's data without authorization.

Architecture:

  • Tenant-aware identity model.
  • Tenant-scoped roles and groups.
  • Resource ownership and tenant ID on every object.
  • Service-layer authorization checks for every sensitive action.
  • Central policy engine or shared authorization library.
  • Audit logs with tenant, actor, subject, action, resource, and decision.
flowchart TD
    A[Request arrives] --> B[Authenticate principal]
    B --> C[Resolve tenant context]
    C --> D[Load resource tenant]
    D --> E{Tenant matches?}
    E -- No --> F[Deny]
    E -- Yes --> G[Check action policy]
    G --> H{Allowed?}
    H -- Yes --> I[Permit]
    H -- No --> F

Controls:

  • Derive tenant context from trusted session or token claims, not arbitrary request parameters alone.
  • Validate resource tenant from the database before access.
  • Use tenant-scoped roles such as tenant_admin for tenant A, not global admin by accident.
  • Enforce object-level authorization in service code, not only at gateway.
  • Add database row-level controls where appropriate as defense in depth.
  • Test cross-tenant access systematically.
  • Alert on denied cross-tenant attempts and unusual tenant switching.

AWS example: use separate AWS accounts for strong tenant isolation where required, or strict tenant-aware IAM and data-layer controls for pooled architectures. In both cases, application authorization must still check tenant boundaries.

7. How would you model group membership and inherited permissions?

Group membership and inherited permissions should be modeled explicitly, with bounded inheritance and explainability.

Common model:

user Alice is member of group Finance
group Finance is viewer of folder Reports
folder Reports contains document Q4
viewer of folder implies viewer of document

Design elements:

  • Direct memberships: user belongs to group.
  • Nested groups: group belongs to group, if allowed.
  • Resource hierarchy: folder contains document.
  • Relation inheritance: folder viewer implies document viewer.
  • Scope: membership and permission apply within tenant or project.
  • Deny or restriction rules: sensitive documents may block inheritance.

Controls:

  • Limit nesting depth to prevent expensive or surprising traversal.
  • Detect cycles in group membership.
  • Keep tenant boundaries explicit in group and resource IDs.
  • Log effective permission path for audits.
  • Provide an explain API: Alice can view Q4 because Finance can view Reports.
  • Recompute or invalidate cached decisions when membership changes.
  • Review high-impact groups and broad inheritance roots.

Failure modes:

  • Nested group grants more access than reviewers realize.
  • Removing a user from one group does not remove inherited access through another group.
  • Cross-tenant group accidentally grants access.
  • Cache keeps old membership after revocation.

Senior answer: inherited permissions are powerful but need bounded traversal, clear scope, cycle protection, revocation freshness, and human-readable explanations.

8. How would you prevent authorization bypass in REST APIs?

REST authorization bypass usually happens when the API authenticates the user but fails to check whether that user can access the specific object or action.

Controls:

  • Enforce authorization in the service layer for every endpoint.
  • Check object ownership or tenant membership after loading the resource.
  • Do not trust user-controlled IDs, tenant IDs, roles, or scopes without verification.
  • Use deny-by-default route policy.
  • Separate authentication middleware from authorization logic.
  • Validate action-level permissions such as read, write, delete, export, share.
  • Prevent mass assignment by allowlisting writable fields.
  • Avoid exposing sequential IDs where possible, but never rely on unguessable IDs alone.
  • Add tests for horizontal and vertical privilege escalation.
  • Log denied access attempts and suspicious object enumeration.

Example vulnerable endpoint:

GET /api/invoices/123
Code checks user is logged in
Code returns invoice 123 without checking tenant or ownership

Better flow:

flowchart TD
    A[Request API object] --> B[Authenticate user]
    B --> C[Load object]
    C --> D[Check tenant and permission]
    D --> E{Allowed?}
    E -- Yes --> F[Return object]
    E -- No --> G[Return generic deny]

Senior answer: every REST endpoint that touches a resource should answer, Can this principal perform this action on this object in this tenant right now?

9. How would you prevent authorization bypass in GraphQL APIs?

GraphQL authorization is tricky because one query can traverse many object types and fields. A gateway-level check is not enough if resolvers return nested objects with different permissions.

Controls:

  • Enforce authorization in resolvers or shared data-access layer.
  • Apply object-level checks for every returned object.
  • Apply field-level checks for sensitive fields.
  • Limit query depth, breadth, aliases, and complexity.
  • Avoid relying only on operation name or top-level query authorization.
  • Use schema directives or policy wrappers consistently.
  • Prevent introspection in production where appropriate, but do not treat that as a core auth control.
  • Test nested traversal paths for data exposure.
  • Ensure batch loaders do not bypass per-object checks.
  • Log denied field and object decisions at safe granularity.

Example risk:

User can view project
Query asks project { users { salary } }
Top-level project check passes
Nested salary field lacks field-level authorization
Sensitive data leaks

Better design:

flowchart TD
    A[GraphQL query] --> B[Authenticate user]
    B --> C[Validate query cost]
    C --> D[Authorize root resolver]
    D --> E[Authorize nested objects]
    E --> F[Authorize sensitive fields]
    F --> G[Return filtered result]

Senior answer: GraphQL authorization must be enforced at the object and field resolver level, not just at the endpoint.

10. What is IDOR/BOLA and how do you detect it?

IDOR means Insecure Direct Object Reference. BOLA means Broken Object Level Authorization. Both describe a failure where a user can access an object by changing an identifier because the server does not enforce object-level authorization.

Example:

Alice can access /api/accounts/1001
Alice changes URL to /api/accounts/1002
Server returns Bob's account

Detection approaches:

  • Security tests that swap object IDs between users and tenants.
  • Unit and integration tests for object-level authorization.
  • API fuzzing with valid IDs from different accounts.
  • Logs showing many 403s or 404s across sequential object IDs.
  • User accessing objects across many tenants or owners.
  • High ratio of denied object access to normal access.
  • Access to object shortly after guessing or enumeration pattern.

Runtime detection signals:

Signal Meaning
Sequential ID probing Possible object enumeration
Cross-tenant denied attempts Tenant boundary probing
Many object IDs in short window BOLA testing or scraping
Success after many denies Possible bypass found
Sensitive object access from unusual user Potential authorization flaw

Prevention is better than detection: enforce object-level checks centrally, design data access APIs that require principal and resource context, and test every endpoint for horizontal and vertical privilege escalation.

Chunk 2 - Questions 11-20

11. How would you design object-level authorization checks?

Object-level authorization decides whether a principal can perform an action on a specific object. It is the core defense against IDOR and tenant isolation failures.

Design pattern:

flowchart TD
    A[Request action on object] --> B[Authenticate principal]
    B --> C[Load object by ID]
    C --> D[Get object owner and tenant]
    D --> E[Evaluate policy]
    E --> F{Allowed?}
    F -- Yes --> G[Perform action]
    F -- No --> H[Deny and log]

Inputs:

  • Principal: user, service, group, role, tenant.
  • Action: read, edit, delete, share, export, approve.
  • Object: ID, owner, tenant, sensitivity, lifecycle state.
  • Context: device posture, location, risk score, auth freshness.

Controls:

  • Make authorization checks mandatory in the data access layer or service layer.
  • Never trust object IDs from request parameters without checking ownership or relation.
  • Check tenant boundary before checking detailed permissions.
  • Use centralized helper functions or policy engine to avoid inconsistent logic.
  • Log deny decisions and suspicious object enumeration.
  • Add tests where user A tries user B's object and tenant A tries tenant B's object.

Example: GET /documents/123 should not mean return document 123 if user is logged in. It should mean return document 123 if user has viewer or stronger permission on that document within the correct tenant and current policy context.

12. How would you design field-level authorization checks?

Field-level authorization controls access to sensitive fields inside an otherwise accessible object. A user may be allowed to view an employee profile but not salary, national ID, or medical accommodation fields.

Design:

  • Classify fields by sensitivity.
  • Define policies per field or field group.
  • Enforce in serializers, resolvers, or data projection layer.
  • Avoid fetching or returning sensitive fields unless authorized.
  • Redact denied fields consistently.
  • Log access to highly sensitive fields.

Example:

Field Required permission
Name Employee directory read
Email Employee directory read
Salary HR compensation read
National ID Regulated PII read
Performance notes Manager or HR role

Failure modes:

  • API returns full object and UI hides fields only client-side.
  • GraphQL nested field lacks resolver authorization.
  • Export endpoint ignores field-level policy.
  • Search index contains sensitive fields.
  • Logs and analytics capture fields user was not allowed to view.

Senior answer: field-level authorization must be enforced server-side and consistently across normal views, APIs, exports, search, analytics, and logs.

13. How would you design row-level authorization for sensitive datasets?

Row-level authorization controls which records a user can access within a dataset. It is common in analytics, data lakes, CRM systems, HR systems, and multi-tenant SaaS.

Design principles:

  • Every row has security-relevant metadata such as tenant, owner, region, classification, project, or legal hold status.
  • Policies combine subject attributes, row attributes, action, and context.
  • Enforcement happens close to the query path, not only in the UI.
  • Default deny when metadata is missing or ambiguous.
  • Queries are filtered before results are returned.
flowchart TD
    A[Query request] --> B[Resolve principal attributes]
    B --> C[Build row filter policy]
    C --> D[Apply filter in query engine]
    D --> E[Return authorized rows]
    D --> F[Audit row access]

Examples:

Sales rep can read accounts where owner_id equals rep_id.
Regional analyst can read rows where region equals assigned region.
Tenant admin can read rows where tenant_id equals current tenant.
Data scientist can query masked PII rows only.

Controls:

  • Use database row-level security or a trusted query service where possible.
  • Prevent raw table bypass through direct database access.
  • Apply column masking or tokenization for sensitive fields.
  • Audit bulk access and unusual query patterns.
  • Test policy with known users and synthetic rows.
  • Cache policies carefully because stale attributes can over-permit access.

14. How would you design delegated access?

Delegated access lets one actor grant another actor limited authority to act on their behalf or access a resource. Examples include calendar sharing, support impersonation, OAuth consent, and assistant access.

Design requirements:

  • Explicit grantor, delegate, resource, action, scope, and expiration.
  • Consent or approval for the delegation.
  • Least-privilege actions, not full account sharing.
  • Revocation by grantor and administrators.
  • Audit trail showing actor and effective subject.
  • Step-up for high-risk delegation.
  • Restrictions on delegation chaining.
  • Clear user-visible review of active delegations.
flowchart TD
    A[Grantor creates delegation] --> B[Define delegate scope]
    B --> C[Store grant]
    C --> D[Delegate acts]
    D --> E[Policy checks grant]
    E --> F[Audit actor and subject]

Example audit event:

actor = support_user_17
effective_subject = customer_123
action = view_account
delegation_id = grant_456
reason = support_ticket_789

Failure modes:

  • Delegate receives broader access than intended.
  • Delegation never expires.
  • System logs only the original user, hiding delegate activity.
  • Delegate can re-delegate authority without approval.
  • Support impersonation bypasses customer consent and monitoring.

15. What is confused deputy and how do you prevent it?

A confused deputy problem occurs when a privileged service is tricked into using its authority on behalf of a less-privileged actor in an unintended way.

Example:

User cannot read private object directly.
User sends URL to export service.
Export service has broad read permission.
Export service reads private object and returns it to user.

Root cause: the deputy uses its own broad privilege without binding the request to the caller's authority and intended resource.

Controls:

  • Bind delegated authority to caller, audience, target resource, and action.
  • Pass caller identity and enforce downstream authorization.
  • Use scoped service credentials instead of broad shared service accounts.
  • Validate that the caller can access the target before the deputy acts.
  • Use external IDs, source conditions, or audience binding in cross-account integrations.
  • Log actor and effective subject.
  • Deny user-controlled target resources unless authorized.
flowchart TD
    A[Caller asks deputy] --> B[Deputy checks caller rights]
    B --> C{Caller allowed target?}
    C -- Yes --> D[Use constrained authority]
    C -- No --> E[Deny]

AWS example: a SaaS provider assuming customer roles should use external IDs and tightly scoped trust policies so one customer cannot trick the provider into acting in another customer's AWS account.

16. How would you handle emergency break-glass access?

Break-glass access is emergency access used when normal access paths fail. It is necessary for availability, but it must be rare, controlled, monitored, and reviewed.

Design:

  • Pre-create minimal emergency accounts or roles.
  • Protect with phishing-resistant MFA and strong credential storage.
  • Store credentials or hardware keys in controlled custody.
  • Require incident or approval workflow where possible.
  • Use time-limited sessions and minimal permissions.
  • Alert immediately on use.
  • Record all actions.
  • Require post-use review and credential rotation.
  • Test periodically so it works during real incidents.

Break-glass should not be a shared password in a wiki. It should be a governed operational process.

AWS example:

  • Root account access is locked down with hardware MFA.
  • Emergency admin role exists with strict trust conditions.
  • Use is alerted through CloudTrail and Security Hub.
  • Session duration is short.
  • Every use creates an incident review.

Tradeoff: too much friction can make emergency access unusable during an outage; too little friction turns it into a permanent backdoor. The balance is controlled availability plus loud monitoring.

17. How would you audit privileged access?

Privileged access audit should answer who had access, why they had it, when they used it, what they did, and whether it was appropriate.

Audit data:

  • Role assignments and changes.
  • JIT activation requests, approvals, and expiry.
  • Break-glass use.
  • Admin login and step-up events.
  • Commands or API calls executed.
  • Resource changes made.
  • Policy changes, credential creation, logging changes.
  • Session duration and source device.
  • Ticket or business justification.

Controls:

  • Centralize admin activity logs in a tamper-resistant location.
  • Protect logs from the admins being audited.
  • Record both actor and effective role.
  • Correlate privileged actions with approvals.
  • Alert on sensitive actions, off-hours access, unusual location, and denied attempts.
  • Review high-risk privileged activity regularly.

AWS example: CloudTrail should be delivered to a separate log archive account with restricted access. Alert on IAM policy changes, role trust changes, access key creation, disabling CloudTrail, KMS policy changes, GuardDuty changes, and root account use.

Senior answer: privileged access audit is not just a list of admins. It is evidence that privileged access was granted intentionally, used appropriately, and monitored independently.

18. How would you design just-in-time privileged access?

JIT privileged access removes standing admin rights and grants elevated permissions only when needed, for a limited time, with approval and audit.

flowchart TD
    A[User requests elevation] --> B[Check eligibility]
    B --> C[Require justification]
    C --> D[Approval or policy decision]
    D --> E{Approved?}
    E -- Yes --> F[Grant time limited role]
    E -- No --> G[Deny]
    F --> H[Monitor actions]
    H --> I[Expire access]

Design elements:

  • Eligible users are pre-approved for certain roles.
  • Activation requires phishing-resistant re-authentication.
  • Request includes ticket, reason, scope, and duration.
  • Approval required for high-risk roles.
  • Access expires automatically.
  • All actions during elevated session are logged.
  • Risk signals can block activation.
  • Emergency path exists but is louder and more restricted.

AWS example: instead of permanent AdministratorAccess, users activate time-limited permission sets through IAM Identity Center or PAM tooling. CloudTrail records role assumptions and all privileged API calls. Sensitive roles may require approval and short session duration.

Residual risk: JIT reduces standing privilege but does not guarantee safe actions. You still need least privilege, separation of duties, change controls, and detection.

19. What is least privilege and why is it hard to implement?

Least privilege means each user, workload, and system receives only the minimum access needed for its function, for the minimum time necessary.

It is hard because organizations change constantly:

  • Users change teams and projects.
  • Services add features and dependencies.
  • Emergency grants become permanent.
  • Teams request broad permissions to move faster.
  • Permissions are hard to map to business need.
  • Cloud IAM policies are complex.
  • Machine identities are numerous and poorly owned.
  • Access reviews become rubber stamps.
  • Removing access can break production.

Controls:

  • Start with scoped roles and resource-level permissions.
  • Use JIT for privileged access.
  • Add expiration to temporary grants.
  • Measure actual permission usage.
  • Remove unused permissions safely through staged rollout.
  • Split service accounts by workload.
  • Use policy simulation before changes.
  • Review effective permissions, not labels.
  • Alert on broad grants and privilege escalation paths.

Senior answer: least privilege is not a one-time policy. It is an operating model that needs telemetry, automation, ownership, review, and safe removal paths.

20. How would you reduce standing privileges?

Standing privileges are permissions available all the time, even when not actively needed. Reducing them lowers blast radius for credential theft and insider misuse.

Approach:

  • Inventory privileged roles, groups, service accounts, and long-lived tokens.
  • Identify unused or rarely used permissions from logs.
  • Replace permanent admin roles with JIT activation.
  • Add approval, reason, scope, and expiry to elevated access.
  • Split broad roles into smaller task roles.
  • Use break-glass only for emergencies.
  • Add permission boundaries and guardrails.
  • Remove dormant accounts and stale group memberships.
  • Rotate or eliminate static service account keys.
  • Monitor activation and sensitive actions.

Example migration:

Before:
  40 engineers have permanent production admin.

After:
  40 engineers are eligible for production admin.
  Access requires ticket, approval, MFA, and expires in 2 hours.
  Routine tasks use narrower deployer or viewer roles.

AWS controls can include IAM Identity Center permission sets with short session duration, IAM Access Analyzer, service control policies, permission boundaries, CloudTrail monitoring, and automated review of unused permissions.

The key tradeoff is operational friction. Reduce standing access by creating fast, reliable, auditable elevation paths rather than making engineers route around security.

Chunk 3 - Questions 21-30

21. How would you detect excessive permissions?

Excessive permissions are permissions beyond what a user or workload needs. Detection requires comparing granted permissions, actual usage, peer baselines, and risk.

Signals:

  • Permissions granted but never used.
  • Broad actions such as wildcard admin privileges.
  • Access to resources outside job function, tenant, region, or project.
  • Service accounts with human-like broad access.
  • Users with many high-risk roles.
  • Toxic role combinations.
  • Permissions granted directly rather than through governed groups.
  • Dormant accounts with active privileges.

Detection approach:

flowchart TD
    A[Collect grants] --> B[Collect usage logs]
    B --> C[Compute effective permissions]
    C --> D[Compare to usage and peers]
    D --> E[Score excessive access]
    E --> F[Review or remediate]

AWS examples:

  • IAM Access Analyzer for unused access and external access.
  • CloudTrail to identify actual API usage.
  • Access advisor data for service-level usage.
  • Alerts for AdministratorAccess, wildcard actions, broad resource *, and risky trust policies.

Senior answer: excessive permission detection should focus on effective permissions and real usage, not only assigned role names. A role named viewer can still contain dangerous privileges.

22. How would you design permission reviews for thousands of employees?

At large scale, permission reviews must be risk-based, evidence-driven, and easy for reviewers to understand. Asking managers to review thousands of opaque role names creates rubber-stamping.

Design:

  • Group access by application, role, resource, and risk level.
  • Show effective permissions and last-used evidence.
  • Prioritize privileged, sensitive, external, and unusual access.
  • Route reviews to accountable owners: manager, app owner, data owner, or resource owner.
  • Provide recommended removals for unused or anomalous access.
  • Require justification for keeping high-risk access.
  • Track decisions, deadlines, exceptions, and remediation.
  • Automatically remove access when review is denied or not completed.

Reviewer experience should answer:

Who has access?
What can they do?
Why do they have it?
When did they last use it?
Is it typical for their role?
What happens if I remove it?

Controls against review fatigue:

  • Do not review low-risk stable access with the same intensity as privileged access.
  • Highlight changes since last review.
  • Use campaigns for high-risk systems.
  • Automate obvious removals after safe grace periods.
  • Measure reviewer accuracy and overdue reviews.

23. How would you automate access recertification?

Access recertification can be automated by connecting identity lifecycle data, access grants, usage telemetry, risk scoring, and workflow decisions.

Automation flow:

flowchart TD
    A[Collect access grants] --> B[Enrich with HR and owner data]
    B --> C[Add usage and risk signals]
    C --> D[Generate review campaign]
    D --> E[Route to reviewers]
    E --> F[Approve or revoke]
    F --> G[Execute changes]
    G --> H[Record evidence]

Automation inputs:

  • HR data: department, manager, employment status, contractor end date.
  • IAM data: groups, roles, direct permissions, service accounts.
  • Usage data: last login, last API call, last privileged action.
  • Risk data: admin, sensitive data, production, external sharing.
  • Ownership data: app owner, data owner, resource owner.

Automated decisions:

  • Auto-expire temporary access.
  • Auto-remove access for terminated users.
  • Auto-flag unused privileged access.
  • Auto-route high-risk access to resource owner.
  • Auto-create tickets for denied access removal.
  • Auto-verify removal was completed.

Human approval remains important for high-impact access, but automation should handle evidence collection, routing, reminders, enforcement, and audit records.

24. What evidence would prove that access reviews are effective?

Effective access reviews produce measurable reduction in risk, not just completed forms.

Evidence:

  • Percentage of high-risk access reviewed on time.
  • Percentage of reviewed access removed or reduced.
  • Reduction in unused privileged access.
  • Reduction in standing admin roles.
  • Time from review denial to actual access removal.
  • Fewer stale accounts and orphaned service accounts.
  • Lower number of toxic role combinations.
  • Reduced external or cross-tenant grants.
  • Audit samples showing reviewer understood effective permissions.
  • Incidents or near misses tied to stale access decrease over time.

Weak evidence:

  • 100 percent reviews completed with no removals.
  • Managers approved everything in seconds.
  • Reviews showed only role names with no permission detail.
  • Denied access was never actually removed.

Strong evidence links review decision to enforcement:

Reviewer denied access
Ticket created automatically
Access removed within SLA
User cannot access resource anymore
Audit record shows full chain

Senior answer: access review effectiveness is proven by risk reduction and enforcement completion, not review completion rate alone.

25. How would you design separation of duties?

Separation of duties prevents one person from having combinations of permissions that create unacceptable fraud, abuse, or control bypass risk.

Examples:

  • User can create vendor but cannot approve payment to that vendor.
  • Developer can deploy code but cannot approve their own production change.
  • Security engineer can investigate logs but cannot delete audit logs.
  • Cloud admin can manage compute but cannot modify log archive retention.

Design:

  • Identify toxic permission combinations.
  • Encode constraints in IAM policy, workflow, and approval systems.
  • Detect existing violations through effective permission analysis.
  • Prevent new violations at role assignment time.
  • Require independent approval for sensitive workflows.
  • Log and review exceptions.
flowchart TD
    A[Role assignment request] --> B[Compute effective permissions]
    B --> C[Check toxic combinations]
    C --> D{Conflict found?}
    D -- Yes --> E[Deny or require exception]
    D -- No --> F[Approve assignment]

Separation of duties should be enforced both at access grant time and transaction time. Even if a user somehow holds both roles, the workflow should prevent them from approving their own sensitive action.

26. How would you prevent privilege escalation through role chaining?

Role chaining privilege escalation occurs when a user with one role can assume or grant another role, eventually reaching higher privilege than intended.

Examples:

  • User can update IAM policy, then grants self admin.
  • Role A can assume Role B, Role B can assume Role C, Role C is admin.
  • Developer can modify CI pipeline that deploys privileged infrastructure role.
  • User can pass a role to a service that performs actions on their behalf.

Controls:

  • Model role assumption graph and detect paths to privileged roles.
  • Restrict who can create, update, pass, or assume roles.
  • Use permission boundaries or maximum permission constraints.
  • Require explicit trust policies and conditions.
  • Deny self-escalation actions such as policy attachment to self.
  • Monitor PassRole, trust policy changes, role creation, and policy version changes.
  • Test IAM changes with policy simulation before deployment.
  • Separate role administration from role usage.

AWS example: iam:PassRole is a common escalation path. A user who can pass a powerful role to Lambda or EC2 may indirectly gain that role's permissions. Restrict PassRole by resource, service, and condition, and alert on changes.

Senior answer: prevent escalation by analyzing effective permissions and paths, not just individual role labels.

27. How would you secure admin roles in cloud platforms?

Cloud admin roles have high blast radius, so they need strong authentication, least privilege, JIT access, guardrails, and monitoring.

Controls:

  • Use federated SSO with phishing-resistant MFA.
  • Avoid long-lived IAM users and static admin keys.
  • Separate admin accounts from daily user accounts.
  • Use JIT elevation and short session duration.
  • Split admin roles by function: network, security, IAM, database, break-glass.
  • Apply organization guardrails such as SCPs or equivalent.
  • Use permission boundaries for delegated admins.
  • Protect logging and security services from ordinary admins.
  • Alert on sensitive actions.
  • Review admin activity regularly.

AWS examples:

  • IAM Identity Center with permission sets.
  • SCPs to prevent disabling CloudTrail or leaving organization logging.
  • CloudTrail in a separate log archive account.
  • GuardDuty and Security Hub monitoring.
  • IAM Access Analyzer for external access.
  • Root account locked down with hardware MFA.

High-risk actions to alert:

  • Creating access keys.
  • Changing role trust policy.
  • Attaching administrator policies.
  • Disabling logging or monitoring.
  • Modifying KMS key policy.
  • Making storage public.
  • Changing organization SCPs.

28. How would you design policy-as-code for authorization?

Policy-as-code treats authorization policy as versioned, testable, reviewable code. It improves consistency and auditability, but bad policy rollout can cause broad over-permit or outage.

Design:

  • Store policies in version control.
  • Use code review and owner approval.
  • Add unit tests for allow and deny cases.
  • Add regression tests for known authorization bugs.
  • Run policy simulation before deployment.
  • Use staged rollout and canaries.
  • Version policy bundles and support rollback.
  • Generate human-readable explanations.
  • Log policy version with each decision.
flowchart TD
    A[Policy change] --> B[Code review]
    B --> C[Automated tests]
    C --> D[Policy simulation]
    D --> E[Canary rollout]
    E --> F[Production rollout]
    F --> G[Decision logging]

What to test:

  • Default deny.
  • Tenant boundary.
  • Object ownership.
  • Privileged actions.
  • Break-glass exceptions.
  • Stale attributes.
  • Conflicting rules.
  • Known abuse cases.

Senior answer: policy is production code. It deserves the same engineering rigor as application code because policy bugs can expose data instantly across many services.

29. What is the difference between centralized and decentralized authorization?

Centralized authorization means policy decisions are made by a common policy engine or service. Decentralized authorization means each service owns and evaluates its own authorization logic.

Model Strength Weakness
Centralized Consistency, auditability, reuse, policy governance Latency, availability dependency, broad impact of policy bugs
Decentralized Local context, autonomy, fewer central dependencies Inconsistency, duplicated logic, harder audit

In practice, large systems often use a hybrid model:

  • Central policy definitions and libraries.
  • Local enforcement in services.
  • Centralized decision service for complex or high-risk decisions.
  • Local caching with bounded TTL.
  • Shared audit schema.

Key design question: where is the policy decision point and where is the policy enforcement point? Even with centralized decisions, enforcement happens in the application or service receiving the request.

Senior answer: centralize policy semantics and governance where possible, but keep enforcement close to the resource and design for availability, freshness, and local context.

30. When should authorization be enforced at the service layer versus the gateway layer?

Gateway authorization is useful for coarse checks. Service-layer authorization is required for resource-specific and business-specific decisions.

Gateway is good for:

  • Token validation.
  • Authentication enforcement.
  • Basic scope or route checks.
  • Rate limiting.
  • Blocking unauthorized clients.
  • Coarse tenant routing.

Service layer is required for:

  • Object-level authorization.
  • Field-level authorization.
  • Row-level authorization.
  • Business workflow rules.
  • Tenant boundary validation against actual resource state.
  • Contextual checks based on resource metadata.
  • Side effects and transaction-specific policy.
flowchart TD
    A[Request] --> B[Gateway coarse checks]
    B --> C[Service receives request]
    C --> D[Load resource state]
    D --> E[Service policy check]
    E --> F{Allowed?}
    F -- Yes --> G[Execute action]
    F -- No --> H[Deny]

Gateway-only authorization is tempting because it is centralized, but the gateway often lacks resource state. It may know Alice has invoice:read, but it does not know whether invoice 123 belongs to Alice's tenant or contains restricted fields.

Senior answer: use the gateway for coarse admission control and service layer for final authorization decisions tied to resource state.

Chunk 4 - Questions 31-40

31. Why is gateway-only authorization insufficient?

Gateway-only authorization is insufficient because the gateway usually does not have full resource, object, field, tenant, and business workflow context. It can make coarse decisions, but final authorization often depends on data only the service can safely know.

Gateway can know:

  • Token is valid.
  • Issuer and audience are correct.
  • Client has basic scope.
  • Route requires authentication.
  • Request rate or source risk.

Gateway often cannot know:

  • Whether object ID belongs to the user's tenant.
  • Whether the user owns or has a relationship to the object.
  • Whether a field is sensitive.
  • Whether the workflow state allows the action.
  • Whether a row has legal hold or regional restrictions.
  • Whether the action is a toxic combination.

Example:

Gateway sees: user has invoice:read scope.
Service must check: invoice 123 belongs to user's tenant and user can view it.

Gateway-only controls also fail when services call each other internally and bypass the public gateway. Each service should enforce authorization for resources it owns.

Senior answer: gateway authorization is useful admission control, but service-layer authorization is the real protection for object-level and business-specific decisions.

32. How would you design authorization for internal microservices?

Internal microservices need both service identity and end-user or workload authorization context. Internal network location should not be treated as authorization.

Design:

  • Authenticate services with mTLS, workload identity, or signed service tokens.
  • Authorize service-to-service calls by caller service, target service, action, and environment.
  • Pass end-user context only when needed and protect it from spoofing.
  • Use audience-specific tokens for target services.
  • Avoid forwarding broad user bearer tokens through every service.
  • Use a central policy model or shared library for consistency.
  • Enforce authorization in each service for resources it owns.
  • Log caller service, user subject, action, resource, and decision.
flowchart TD
    A[Service A calls Service B] --> B[Authenticate service identity]
    B --> C[Validate token audience]
    C --> D[Check service permission]
    D --> E[Check user context if needed]
    E --> F{Allowed?}
    F -- Yes --> G[Process request]
    F -- No --> H[Deny and log]

Common mistakes:

  • Trusting internal headers without mTLS or signing.
  • Giving every service broad access to every database.
  • Losing original actor context.
  • Overusing one shared service account.
  • Gateway validates token but internal service never checks object-level access.

Senior answer: zero trust applies inside the platform too. Service identity, user context, target resource, and action must all be considered.

33. How would you prevent tenant isolation failures?

Preventing tenant isolation failures requires making tenant context explicit, trusted, and enforced at every layer.

Controls:

  • Include tenant ID in every tenant-owned resource.
  • Derive active tenant from trusted session or membership, not only request input.
  • Validate resource tenant before returning or modifying data.
  • Use tenant-scoped roles and groups.
  • Ensure search, cache, analytics, queues, and background jobs include tenant filters.
  • Use database row-level security or separate databases/accounts where risk requires.
  • Avoid global identifiers that bypass tenant checks.
  • Log tenant ID on every authorization decision.
  • Test cross-tenant access continuously.
flowchart TD
    A[Request with tenant context] --> B[Resolve trusted tenant]
    B --> C[Load resource tenant]
    C --> D{Tenant match?}
    D -- Yes --> E[Evaluate permission]
    D -- No --> F[Deny and alert]

Failure modes:

  • Cache key missing tenant ID.
  • Background worker processes object by ID without tenant filter.
  • Search index returns cross-tenant results.
  • Admin API accepts tenant ID parameter without checking membership.
  • Shared group or relationship edge bridges tenants.

AWS-style design: use separate accounts for strong isolation when needed, but do not assume account separation solves application-level authorization. Pooled SaaS still needs tenant-aware policy in the app and data layer.

34. How would you test tenant isolation?

Tenant isolation testing should combine automated security tests, negative test cases, fuzzing, and production detections.

Test strategy:

  • Create test tenants A and B with users, admins, groups, and resources.
  • Authenticate as tenant A user and attempt tenant B actions.
  • Swap object IDs, tenant IDs, group IDs, project IDs, and document IDs.
  • Test reads, writes, deletes, exports, searches, reports, and background jobs.
  • Test APIs, GraphQL, mobile endpoints, admin consoles, and bulk import/export.
  • Test cache behavior by accessing same object ID shape across tenants.
  • Test async workflows and webhooks.
  • Verify logs show denied cross-tenant attempts.

Example cases:

Tenant A user reads Tenant B invoice.
Tenant A admin edits Tenant B user.
Tenant A search query returns Tenant B document.
Tenant A export job includes Tenant B rows.
Tenant A webhook event triggers action in Tenant B.

Runtime detection:

  • Denied cross-tenant access attempts.
  • Same user touches many tenants unexpectedly.
  • Resource tenant differs from session tenant.
  • Cache hits across tenant boundaries.
  • Bulk export includes multiple tenants unexpectedly.

Senior answer: tenant isolation is not one test. It is a property verified across every path that reads, writes, caches, indexes, exports, or asynchronously processes tenant data.

35. How would you design authorization logs?

Authorization logs should support forensics, debugging, compliance, and detection without leaking sensitive policy details unnecessarily.

Useful fields:

  • Timestamp and correlation ID.
  • Actor principal ID.
  • Effective subject if delegated access or impersonation is used.
  • Tenant or organization.
  • Resource type and resource ID or hash.
  • Action requested.
  • Decision: permit, deny, step-up, or error.
  • Policy version.
  • Reason code or high-level rule ID.
  • Enforcement point and service name.
  • Authentication assurance and session age where relevant.
  • Source IP, device, client ID, and workload identity.
  • Request risk score.
flowchart TD
    A[Authorization decision] --> B[Normalize event]
    B --> C[Security log stream]
    C --> D[Detection]
    C --> E[Audit]
    C --> F[Debugging]

Do not log:

  • Raw tokens.
  • Secrets.
  • Full sensitive object payloads.
  • Complete policy internals that would help attackers bypass controls.
  • Excessive PII when stable IDs are enough.

Important: log denies as well as permits. Denied authorization attempts are often the early signal for probing, IDOR testing, tenant boundary attacks, and privilege escalation attempts.

36. What authorization events should generate security alerts?

Not every deny should page security, but certain authorization events are high-signal.

Alert candidates:

  • Cross-tenant access attempt.
  • Repeated object-level denies across many object IDs.
  • Privileged role assignment or activation.
  • New admin or owner added to sensitive resource.
  • Permission policy changed to broad wildcard.
  • Public or external sharing enabled on sensitive data.
  • Break-glass access used.
  • Denied attempt to disable logs or security controls.
  • Service account accesses unusual resource.
  • Role chaining or PassRole style escalation attempt.
  • Access to sensitive field or dataset outside normal pattern.
  • Data export by newly privileged user.

Alert quality depends on context. A single deny may be a user mistake. Hundreds of denies across sequential objects may be enumeration. Admin role activation during an incident may be expected; admin role activation from a new country at 3 AM may be suspicious.

Detection should include suppression, baselines, and severity tiers. High-confidence events like cross-tenant success or break-glass use should be loud. Common user mistakes should feed dashboards or low-severity triage.

37. How would you debug a denied access request safely?

Debugging denied access is risky because error handling can leak policy details, tenant existence, object existence, or sensitive attributes.

Safe process:

  • Give the user a generic denial message with a request ID.
  • Log detailed reason codes internally.
  • Provide support tooling that requires authorization to view decision details.
  • Show only information the requester is allowed to know.
  • Use an explain API for admins and support with redaction.
  • Include policy version and attributes used in decision.
  • Avoid telling attackers which exact condition failed.

User-facing message:

You do not have access to this resource. Reference ID: abc123.

Internal debug record:

actor=user:alice
action=document.read
resource=document:123
decision=deny
reason=tenant_mismatch
policy_version=2026.06.07

Support tool controls:

  • Support staff can view denial reason only for tenants they support.
  • Sensitive policy details are redacted.
  • Access to debug logs is audited.
  • Debugging cannot grant access directly without workflow.

Senior answer: separate external ambiguity from internal explainability.

38. How would you avoid leaking sensitive policy details in error messages?

Authorization errors should be useful for legitimate users but not act as an oracle for attackers.

Leak examples:

Project 123 exists but you lack FinanceAdmin role.
Document exists in tenant Acme but requires LegalHoldViewer.
User bob@example.com is a valid admin but you cannot modify him.

Safer patterns:

  • Use generic denial before the user is authorized to know resource existence.
  • Return the same response for not found and not authorized where appropriate.
  • Include request IDs for support.
  • Log precise internal reason codes.
  • Reveal more detail only to authorized administrators.
  • Avoid exposing policy names, group names, tenant names, or sensitive labels to unauthorized users.

Tradeoff: too little information frustrates users. The solution is role-aware messaging. A tenant admin can receive more actionable guidance than an unauthenticated or unrelated user.

Example:

External response: Resource not found or access denied.
Internal reason: denied because user is not member of tenant and object exists.

Senior answer: error messages are part of the attack surface. Keep them generic at untrusted boundaries and detailed in protected support and audit tooling.

39. How would you migrate from RBAC to ABAC?

Migrating from RBAC to ABAC should be gradual. A direct replacement can create policy complexity and outages. Most mature systems combine RBAC structure with ABAC conditions.

Migration plan:

  1. Inventory roles, permissions, resources, and pain points.
  2. Identify policies that need attributes: region, data sensitivity, device posture, tenant, project, time.
  3. Define trusted attribute sources and owners.
  4. Add attributes to resources and identities.
  5. Build policy decision and enforcement path.
  6. Run ABAC in shadow mode and compare decisions with RBAC.
  7. Add tests for allow, deny, missing attribute, and stale attribute cases.
  8. Start with low-risk read access or narrow policies.
  9. Gradually enforce ABAC conditions on high-value roles.
  10. Retire roles made obsolete by attributes.
flowchart TD
    A[Existing RBAC] --> B[Add attributes]
    B --> C[Shadow ABAC policy]
    C --> D[Compare decisions]
    D --> E[Canary enforcement]
    E --> F[Gradual rollout]

Key risk: ABAC is only as reliable as its attributes. If department, region, device trust, or data labels are stale or user-controlled, ABAC can fail dangerously.

40. What attributes are risky to use in ABAC policies?

Risky ABAC attributes are those that are stale, user-controlled, ambiguous, spoofable, poorly governed, or too broad.

Risky examples:

  • Client-supplied department, role, or tenant.
  • IP geolocation as sole proof of location.
  • Email domain as proof of employment.
  • Device name without device attestation.
  • Self-declared data classification.
  • Group membership with long sync delay.
  • Job title from HR used as precise authorization.
  • Time of day as a sole control for sensitive access.
  • Network location treated as trust.
  • Tags that resource owners can edit to grant themselves access.

Safer attribute design:

  • Define attribute owner and source of truth.
  • Classify attributes as trusted, derived, or untrusted.
  • Set freshness requirements.
  • Protect high-impact resource tags from self-service tampering.
  • Log attributes used in decisions.
  • Deny by default when critical attributes are missing.
  • Test policy behavior with missing, stale, and malicious attributes.

Example: resource.sensitivity=public is dangerous if any user with edit access can change the label from restricted to public. High-impact labels need controlled workflows and audit.

Senior answer: ABAC moves trust into attributes. Attribute governance becomes authorization governance.

Chunk 5 - Questions 41-50

41. How would you protect policy decision points and policy enforcement points?

The policy decision point, or PDP, evaluates policy. The policy enforcement point, or PEP, intercepts requests and enforces the decision. Both are security-critical.

PDP protections:

  • Strong authentication between PEP and PDP.
  • Authorization on who can change policies.
  • Versioned policy bundles and signed releases.
  • Tests, canaries, and rollback.
  • Protected attribute sources.
  • High availability and safe failure behavior.
  • Decision logging with policy version.
  • Rate limiting and DoS protection.

PEP protections:

  • Cannot be bypassed on protected paths.
  • Fails closed for sensitive operations.
  • Sends trusted, validated inputs to PDP.
  • Enforces obligations returned by PDP.
  • Does not let clients supply trusted attributes.
  • Logs permit and deny decisions.
  • Uses consistent libraries across services.
flowchart TD
    A[Request] --> B[Policy Enforcement Point]
    B --> C[Policy Decision Point]
    C --> D[Policy and attributes]
    C --> E[Decision]
    E --> B
    B --> F[Permit or deny]

Failure modes:

  • PEP omitted from a new endpoint.
  • PDP unavailable and service fails open.
  • Client-controlled attributes influence decisions.
  • Policy rollout accidentally permits all access.
  • PEP ignores obligations such as masking or step-up.

Senior answer: protect PDP integrity and PEP completeness. A correct policy engine is useless if enforcement is bypassed.

42. How would you handle stale group membership data?

Stale group membership can cause users to keep access after role changes, termination, or incident response. The right design depends on risk and latency requirements.

Controls:

  • Define freshness requirements by group risk level.
  • Use event-driven updates from the directory where possible.
  • Use short cache TTLs for high-risk groups.
  • Force cache invalidation on critical events such as termination or admin removal.
  • Check authoritative source for sensitive actions.
  • Track group membership version or timestamp.
  • Monitor sync failures and lag.
  • Fail closed for high-risk access if membership freshness is unknown.

Risk-tiered approach:

Group type Freshness requirement
Low-risk newsletter group Eventually consistent may be fine
App viewer group Minutes may be acceptable
Production admin group Near real time or authoritative check
Terminated user removal Immediate invalidation required

Example: removing a user from prod-admins should invalidate active sessions or at least require authorization checks to observe the new membership quickly. Waiting 24 hours for group sync is unacceptable for privileged access.

43. How would you handle authorization cache invalidation?

Authorization caching improves performance but can preserve stale access. Cache invalidation must be designed around the risk of delayed revocation.

Cache design:

  • Cache low-risk decisions longer than high-risk decisions.
  • Include principal, action, resource, tenant, policy version, and relevant attributes in cache key.
  • Use short TTLs for sensitive resources.
  • Invalidate on membership changes, role changes, policy updates, tenant changes, and resource sensitivity changes.
  • Use event-driven invalidation for critical changes.
  • Store decision version so policy rollout can invalidate old decisions.
  • Avoid caching denies too aggressively if users may gain access soon, but do not let this weaken security.
flowchart TD
    A[Authorization request] --> B{Cache hit?}
    B -- Yes --> C[Check freshness]
    C --> D{Fresh enough?}
    D -- Yes --> E[Use cached decision]
    D -- No --> F[Recompute decision]
    B -- No --> F
    F --> G[Store with TTL]

Failure modes:

  • Cache key omits tenant ID.
  • Cached permit survives role removal.
  • Policy update does not invalidate old decisions.
  • Resource classification changes but cached access remains.
  • Local service cache ignores central revocation.

Senior answer: cache authorization only with explicit freshness, invalidation triggers, and risk-based TTLs. Revocation-critical decisions need authoritative checks or very short cache windows.

44. What are the risks of embedding authorization logic directly in application code?

Embedding authorization logic directly in application code can be fine for simple apps, but in large systems it often creates inconsistent, duplicated, and hard-to-audit policy.

Risks:

  • Different services implement rules differently.
  • New endpoints forget checks.
  • Business policy changes require code deployments everywhere.
  • Tests cover happy paths but not authorization edge cases.
  • Authorization logic is mixed with UI or controller code.
  • Policy is not explainable to auditors or support.
  • Privilege logic cannot be centrally reviewed.
  • Developers copy old vulnerable patterns.

Better patterns:

  • Shared authorization library.
  • Central policy engine for common decisions.
  • Declarative policy-as-code.
  • Service-layer enforcement wrappers.
  • Data-access APIs that require principal context.
  • Tests for every endpoint and object action.

Tradeoff: centralizing everything can create latency and reliability risk. A good design centralizes policy semantics and tooling while keeping enforcement close to the resource.

Senior answer: the risk is not code itself; the risk is scattered policy with no consistency, ownership, tests, or visibility.

45. How would you review authorization logic during code review?

Authorization code review should be adversarial and object-focused. I would trace each endpoint or operation from request to data access and ask whether the correct principal can perform the exact action on the exact resource.

Review checklist:

  • Is authentication required?
  • Where is authorization enforced?
  • Does the check include principal, action, resource, tenant, and context?
  • Is object ownership or relationship checked server-side?
  • Are field-level and row-level policies enforced?
  • Can user-controlled parameters influence trusted attributes?
  • Are admin and normal paths separated?
  • Are bulk, export, search, and async paths covered?
  • Does the endpoint fail closed?
  • Are deny decisions logged safely?
  • Are tests included for unauthorized users and cross-tenant access?

High-risk patterns:

  • if user.is_admin without scope.
  • Client sends role=admin or tenant_id and server trusts it.
  • UI hides buttons but API allows action.
  • Query filters by object ID but not tenant ID.
  • Resolver returns nested sensitive fields without field checks.
  • Background job processes resources without principal context.

Senior code-review question: What prevents user A from performing this action on user B's resource?

46. What are common authorization anti-patterns?

Common anti-patterns:

  • Gateway-only authorization for object-level decisions.
  • UI-only authorization where hidden buttons are the control.
  • Trusting client-supplied roles, tenant IDs, or user IDs.
  • Role names that do not match effective permissions.
  • Global admin roles used for routine work.
  • Missing deny-by-default behavior.
  • No authorization checks on exports, search, batch jobs, or webhooks.
  • Public links that bypass tenant policy.
  • Long-lived authorization caches with no invalidation.
  • Over-trusting SAML or OIDC claims as final authorization.
  • Shared service accounts with broad permissions.
  • No audit logs for permission changes.
  • Error messages that reveal resource existence and policy details.
  • Tests that cover only successful authorized access.

Example anti-pattern:

Controller checks user is logged in.
DAO fetches resource by ID.
Response returns resource.
No tenant or object permission check exists.

Good replacement:

Controller passes principal and action to service.
Service loads resource and checks tenant and policy.
DAO returns only after authorization succeeds.
Decision is logged.

47. How would you design customer-managed roles safely?

Customer-managed roles let tenant admins define their own roles. They are valuable for SaaS flexibility but dangerous if customers can create roles that bypass provider boundaries or hidden system permissions.

Design controls:

  • Define a permission catalog with safe, documented permissions.
  • Separate provider-only permissions from customer-assignable permissions.
  • Scope roles to tenant by default.
  • Prevent roles from granting permissions the tenant admin does not have authority to delegate.
  • Support role templates for common patterns.
  • Show effective permissions clearly before save.
  • Validate toxic combinations.
  • Log role creation, updates, assignments, and deletions.
  • Provide simulation: What can this role do?
  • Version roles and support rollback.
flowchart TD
    A[Tenant admin creates role] --> B[Select allowed permissions]
    B --> C[Validate scope and toxic combos]
    C --> D{Safe role?}
    D -- Yes --> E[Save role and audit]
    D -- No --> F[Reject with safe reason]

Failure modes:

  • Customer role grants provider admin capability.
  • Role can disable audit logs.
  • Role can assign itself broader permissions.
  • Role applies globally instead of tenant-scoped.
  • Custom role hides dangerous inherited permissions.

Senior answer: customer-managed roles need a constrained permission universe, tenant scoping, explainability, validation, and audit.

48. How would you design audit trails for delegated access?

Delegated access audit trails must show both the actor who performed the action and the subject or resource owner on whose behalf they acted.

Audit fields:

  • Actor principal.
  • Effective subject or represented user.
  • Delegation grant ID.
  • Resource and action.
  • Tenant.
  • Scope of delegation.
  • Approval or consent source.
  • Reason or ticket.
  • Start and expiration time.
  • Source IP, device, and session ID hash.
  • Decision and policy version.

Example:

actor = support:caseworker17
effective_subject = customer:user123
action = account.view
delegation = support_ticket_456
tenant = customer_tenant_abc
decision = permit

Controls:

  • Never overwrite actor with effective subject.
  • Show delegated sessions distinctly in UI.
  • Require reason for support impersonation.
  • Make delegation revocable.
  • Alert on broad or unusual delegated actions.
  • Let customers review support access where appropriate.
  • Protect audit logs from delegates and admins being audited.

Senior answer: delegated access without actor-subject auditability becomes indistinguishable from account compromise.

49. How would you handle legal hold or compliance overrides in authorization?

Legal hold and compliance overrides are special authorization paths that preserve, restrict, or expose data for regulatory or legal reasons. They must be explicit, narrow, audited, and protected from misuse.

Design:

  • Model legal hold as a resource state or policy attribute controlled by authorized legal/compliance roles.
  • Prevent deletion or modification of held data even by normal owners.
  • Require strong approval for applying or removing holds.
  • Separate legal hold administration from data access where possible.
  • Log all hold changes and access under hold.
  • Apply retention and deletion rules consistently across primary storage, backups, exports, and derived data.
  • Make override decisions explainable to auditors.

Examples:

User requests deletion of record.
Record has legal_hold=true.
Policy denies deletion and logs compliance reason.

Risks:

  • Compliance override becomes a broad backdoor.
  • Legal hold blocks deletion but exports still leak data.
  • Hold status is editable by resource owner.
  • Audit logs omit who applied the hold.
  • Privacy deletion requests are mishandled because policy is inconsistent.

Senior answer: legal hold is a policy exception, not an authorization free-for-all. It needs separation of duties, tight scope, and strong evidence.

50. What are the top five authorization questions you would ask in a design review?

Top five design-review questions:

  1. What is the authorization decision? Identify principal, action, resource, tenant, and context for each sensitive operation.
  2. Where is it enforced? Name the PEP and verify it cannot be bypassed through API, batch, search, export, or internal service paths.
  3. What is the source of truth? Identify roles, relationships, attributes, policy version, and ownership of those inputs.
  4. How does revocation work? Understand cache TTLs, group sync, role removal, session invalidation, and propagation delays.
  5. How do we prove and debug decisions safely? Review logs, explainability, audit trails, error messages, and detection.

Additional questions:

  • What prevents cross-tenant access?
  • What prevents user A from accessing user B's object?
  • Can a user grant themselves more access?
  • What are the toxic role combinations?
  • What happens if the policy engine is unavailable?
  • Are service accounts least-privileged?
  • Are field-level, row-level, and export paths covered?
  • Can customer-managed roles escape tenant scope?
  • How is break-glass access monitored?
  • What tests prove authorization cannot be bypassed?

Senior closing: authorization failures usually happen at edges: new endpoints, async jobs, nested fields, cached decisions, delegated flows, and emergency exceptions. A good design review deliberately looks there.
