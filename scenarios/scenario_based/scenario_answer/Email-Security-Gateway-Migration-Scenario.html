Scenario 4 — Email Security Gateway Migration to Cloud

Context: The enterprise is decommissioning its on-prem Proofpoint appliance and moving to Microsoft Defender for Office 365 Plan 2 for 30,000 mailboxes.

0. KEY CONCEPTS — Email Security Foundations

Everything in sections 1–8 assumes familiarity with these building blocks. If any term is unfamiliar, read this section first.

0.1 How Email Actually Works (SMTP in Plain Terms)

Email is delivered using SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol) — a protocol from 1982 that was designed with zero authentication. When you send an email, your mail client hands it to your mail server, which looks up the recipient's domain's MX record in DNS to find where to deliver it, then connects over TCP port 25 and delivers the message.

The critical insight for security: SMTP has no built-in verification that the sender is who they claim to be. Anyone on the internet can connect to a mail server and say MAIL FROM: ceo@yourcompany.com — and the protocol accepts it at face value. This is why SPF, DKIM, and DMARC exist (covered below).

sequenceDiagram
    participant Sender as Sender Mail Server
    participant DNS as DNS - MX Lookup
    participant Recipient as Recipient Mail Server
    Sender->>DNS: What mail server handles @yourcompany.com?
    DNS->>Sender: MX record: mail.yourcompany.com
    Sender->>Recipient: SMTP EHLO
    Sender->>Recipient: MAIL FROM: alice@sender.com
    Sender->>Recipient: RCPT TO: bob@yourcompany.com
    Sender->>Recipient: DATA (email headers + body)
    Recipient->>Sender: 250 OK - Message accepted

MX Record — a DNS record type that says "mail for this domain should be delivered to this server." Whoever controls the MX record controls where email arrives. During the migration, you update the MX record from pointing at Proofpoint to pointing at Exchange Online — this is the cutover moment.

0.2 What Is an Email Security Gateway?

An Email Security Gateway (ESG) is a system that sits in front of your mail server and inspects every email before it reaches users. It filters out:

  • Spam
  • Malware and malicious attachments
  • Phishing links
  • Emails spoofing your domain

Proofpoint is a traditional on-premise ESG — a physical or virtual appliance you run in your own datacenter. Email flows into Proofpoint first, gets scanned, and only clean mail reaches your mail server.

Microsoft Defender for Office 365 (MDO) is a cloud-native ESG built directly into Exchange Online. Instead of a separate appliance, the scanning happens inside Microsoft's infrastructure before the email lands in any mailbox.

flowchart LR
    subgraph OldArch[Old Architecture]
        Internet1[Internet] -->|MX → Proofpoint IP| PP[Proofpoint Appliance]
        PP -->|Clean mail only| ExchangeOnPrem[Exchange On-Prem]
    end
    subgraph NewArch[New Architecture]
        Internet2[Internet] -->|MX → Exchange Online| EOP[Exchange Online Protection]
        EOP -->|Scanning layer 1| MDO[Defender for O365]
        MDO -->|Clean mail| EXO[Exchange Online Mailboxes]
    end

The migration risk: during the transition, both paths may exist simultaneously. If the old Proofpoint allow-list is not cleaned up, an attacker can route mail directly to Exchange Online, bypassing all MDO scanning.

0.3 Exchange Online Protection (EOP) vs. Defender for Office 365

These are two layers in Microsoft's email security stack that are often confused:

Component What It Does Included In
Exchange Online Protection (EOP) Basic filtering — spam, known malware signatures, connection filtering (block known bad IPs), anti-spoofing. Every O365 tenant gets this automatically. All M365/O365 plans
Defender for Office 365 Plan 1 Adds Safe Links (URL rewriting and click-time detonation) and Safe Attachments (sandbox detonation). Handles unknown and zero-day threats. M365 Business Premium, Defender Plan 1
Defender for Office 365 Plan 2 Adds Threat Explorer (hunting), Attack Simulation Training, automated investigation and response (AIR), and advanced threat tracking. The full enterprise stack. M365 E5, Defender Plan 2

This scenario uses Plan 2 — the full stack. When someone says "MDO" they usually mean the combined EOP + Defender layers.

0.4 SPF — Sender Policy Framework

SPF is a DNS record that answers the question: "Which mail servers are allowed to send email on behalf of my domain?"

You publish an SPF record in DNS like this:

v=spf1 include:spf.protection.outlook.com include:sendgrid.net -all

This says: "Only Exchange Online and SendGrid are allowed to send email claiming to be from our domain. Reject everything else (-all)."

When a recipient mail server receives email from @yourcompany.com, it checks the SPF record to see if the sending server's IP is on the approved list.

What SPF protects against: An attacker trying to send email with your domain in the MAIL FROM (envelope sender) address from an unauthorized IP.

What SPF does NOT protect against:

  • Lookalike domains (corp0rate.com — SPF for that domain may be valid)
  • The From: header in the email body (SPF checks the envelope, not what the user sees)
  • Attackers using an authorized sending service (e.g., if they compromise your SendGrid account)

SPF's 10-lookup limit: SPF records are resolved by doing DNS lookups for each include:. DNS allows a maximum of 10 lookups. Exceeding this silently breaks SPF — the record is considered invalid and all your mail starts failing SPF checks. Many large organizations hit this limit when they use many third-party senders (Salesforce, Mailchimp, HubSpot, etc.).

0.5 DKIM — DomainKeys Identified Mail

DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to every outgoing email. The mail server signs the email with a private key, and publishes the public key in DNS. Recipient mail servers verify the signature using the public key.

flowchart LR
    subgraph Sending[Your Mail Server]
        Email[Email] -->|Sign with private key| SignedEmail[Email + DKIM Signature Header]
    end
    subgraph DNS[Your DNS]
        PubKey[DKIM Public Key at selector1._domainkey.yourcompany.com]
    end
    subgraph Receiving[Recipient Mail Server]
        SignedEmail2[Receives signed email] -->|Lookup public key| DNS
        DNS -->|Returns public key| Verify[Verify signature]
        Verify -->|Pass| Deliver[Deliver to inbox]
        Verify -->|Fail| Reject[Quarantine / Reject]
    end

What DKIM protects against:

  • Email tampering in transit — if anyone modifies the email body or headers after signing, the signature breaks
  • Domain spoofing in DMARC alignment (DKIM provides the second alignment check alongside SPF)

What DKIM does NOT protect against:

  • A compromised sending server (attacker can sign with your legitimate key)
  • Replay attacks (a legitimately signed email can be re-sent; timestamps and nonces are not mandatory)

Key detail: DKIM is a signature on the email content, not a check on the sending IP. This is why DKIM and SPF are complementary — SPF checks where the email came from; DKIM checks whether it was tampered with.

0.6 DMARC — Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance

DMARC is the policy layer that ties SPF and DKIM together and tells recipient mail servers what to do when they fail. Without DMARC, SPF and DKIM failures are just logged — nothing happens. DMARC gives them teeth.

A DMARC record looks like:

v=DMARC1; p=reject; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourcompany.com; pct=100
Field Meaning
p=none Monitor only — report failures but deliver the email anyway. Used at the start of rollout.
p=quarantine Put failing email in the junk/spam folder. Middle stage.
p=reject Reject failing email at the SMTP layer — it never arrives. End goal.
rua= Where to send daily aggregate reports (XML files showing which IPs sent on your behalf and their pass/fail rates)
ruf= Where to send forensic reports (samples of failing emails)
pct= What percentage of mail to apply the policy to (100 = all)

DMARC alignment: DMARC passes only if either the SPF check passes AND the From: domain matches the MAIL FROM: domain (SPF alignment), OR the DKIM signature passes AND the d= domain in the DKIM header matches the From: domain (DKIM alignment). This alignment requirement is what stops attackers from using SPF-passing subdomains to spoof your primary domain.

DMARC aggregate reports (rua) are XML files sent daily by all major mail providers (Google, Microsoft, Yahoo) showing you every IP that sent email claiming to be from your domain and whether they passed or failed. This is the tool you use to discover all your legitimate third-party senders before advancing to p=reject.

flowchart TD
    p_none["p=none (monitoring)"] -->|30 days of aggregate reports - all senders identified| p_quarantine["p=quarantine (soft enforcement)"]
    p_quarantine -->|14 days - false positive rate < 1%| p_reject["p=reject (full enforcement)"]
    p_reject -->|Ongoing| Protection[All spoofed email rejected worldwide]

0.7 BEC — Business Email Compromise

BEC is a fraud attack where the attacker impersonates an executive or trusted party to trick employees into transferring money or sensitive data. BEC is the highest-financial-impact email attack — the FBI reports average losses of $120,000+ per incident, and large incidents exceed $50 million.

BEC does not require technical sophistication. The attacker does not need to compromise any system. They just need to send a convincing email.

Two main BEC variants:

Variant What Happens Technical Compromise Required?
Impersonation (no compromise) Attacker registers corp0rate.com or spoofs your domain (if DMARC is weak) and emails the finance team pretending to be the CFO: "Please wire $500K to this account for the acquisition." No — just a domain registration
Mailbox compromise (with access) Attacker gains access to a real executive mailbox (via AiTM or credential theft), silently monitors for ongoing wire transfer conversations, then intercepts at the moment of approval and changes the bank account details Yes — requires account takeover

The mailbox-compromise variant is far more dangerous because the attacker is operating from a real, trusted account. The email passes all authentication checks. The only signal is behavioral — the inbox forwarding rule they set to copy all emails to their own account.

0.9 AiTM — Adversary-in-the-Middle Phishing

AiTM (Adversary-in-the-Middle) is a phishing technique where the attacker acts as a real-time reverse proxy between the victim and the legitimate login page. Unlike classic phishing (which steals the password), AiTM steals the authenticated session cookie — which is only created after the user has already completed MFA.

sequenceDiagram
    participant User
    participant AiTM as Attacker Reverse Proxy
    participant O365 as Real O365 Login
    User->>AiTM: Clicks phishing link
    AiTM->>O365: Proxies request to real login
    O365->>AiTM: Returns real login page
    AiTM->>User: Shows real-looking login page
    User->>AiTM: Enters password
    AiTM->>O365: Forwards password
    O365->>AiTM: Sends MFA challenge (TOTP / push)
    AiTM->>User: Relays MFA challenge to victim
    User->>AiTM: Completes MFA
    AiTM->>O365: Forwards MFA response
    O365->>AiTM: Issues authenticated session cookie
    Note over AiTM: Attacker captures and stores session cookie
    AiTM->>User: Redirects to real O365 - victim thinks login succeeded
    Note over AiTM: Attacker replays cookie independently - fully authenticated

Why TOTP/push MFA does NOT stop AiTM: The MFA challenge is completed by the victim themselves and proxied through. The attacker never needs to break MFA — they just wait for the victim to complete it.

Why FIDO2/passkeys DO stop AiTM: A FIDO2 hardware key or passkey creates a cryptographic response that includes the origin domain (login.microsoftonline.com). The AiTM proxy's domain (attacker-proxy.com) is different, so the hardware key refuses to sign the challenge. The proxy cannot fake the origin domain because TLS certificates prevent it.

Common AiTM kits: Evilginx2, Caffeine, Greatness — these are available as-a-service, meaning even low-skill attackers can run sophisticated AiTM campaigns.

0.10 MFA Types — and Which Ones Actually Stop Phishing

MFA Type How It Works Stops Classic Phishing? Stops AiTM? Notes
SMS OTP 6-digit code sent by text Yes (attacker needs the code) No (AiTM proxies the code in real time) Also vulnerable to SIM-swapping
TOTP (Google Authenticator, Authy) 30-second rotating code from authenticator app Yes No Same real-time proxy problem as SMS
Push notification (Microsoft Authenticator, Duo) App shows approve/deny prompt Yes No — unless number matching is enabled Number matching (show matching number in the app) raises the bar but doesn't fully stop AiTM
FIDO2 hardware key (YubiKey) Cryptographic key stored in hardware; signs an origin-bound challenge Yes Yes Key physically present required; origin binding prevents proxy
Passkey (platform authenticator) Same FIDO2 protocol but stored in device TPM (Face ID, Windows Hello) Yes Yes Binds to origin domain; no proxy possible
Certificate-based auth (CBA) Client certificate installed on managed device Yes Yes Common for enterprise workstations

The bottom line: Only FIDO2, passkeys, and CBA are phishing-resistant. Everything else is bypassed by AiTM.

0.11 OAuth and Application Access Tokens

OAuth 2.0 is an authorization framework that lets users grant third-party applications permission to access their data without sharing their password. You've used it when you click "Sign in with Google" or "Allow this app to access your calendar."

In an enterprise O365 context:

  1. A user (or admin) visits a third-party app
  2. The app requests permission to access your O365 data (e.g., Mail.Read, Mail.ReadWrite.All)
  3. The user/admin clicks "Accept"
  4. The app receives an OAuth access token — a credential that lets it read/write email without needing the user's password
  5. The token has a scope (what it can access), an expiry, and a refresh token (allows getting a new access token without re-consenting)

Why OAuth is a security risk:

  • A malicious app can request Mail.ReadWrite.All — permission to read every email in the mailbox
  • If an admin consents, the app has permanent silent access to all mail
  • The access token survives password resets — resetting the compromised user's password does NOT revoke existing OAuth tokens
  • Nation-state attackers use "consent phishing" — building a convincing-looking legitimate app, getting one admin to consent, then using the token to harvest email for months

The fix: Disable user consent for sensitive scopes; require admin approval; audit and revoke all over-scoped existing tokens.

0.12 Session Cookies and Token Theft

When you log into O365 successfully, your browser receives a session cookie — a small piece of data that proves you are authenticated. Your browser presents this cookie on every subsequent request, so you don't have to log in again for each page.

Session cookies are the key to understanding AiTM:

  • The cookie is issued after MFA is completed
  • If an attacker steals the cookie, they can make requests to O365 as you — without your password, without your MFA device
  • Cookies have an expiry, but many are valid for hours or days
  • Revocation: Microsoft Entra ID can revoke all active sessions for a user (Revoke-AzureADUserAllRefreshToken), invalidating all cookies immediately — this is the first automated response step when an account compromise is detected

0.13 Legacy Authentication

Legacy authentication refers to older email protocols that do not support modern authentication (including MFA):

  • SMTP AUTH — used by applications to send email; supports only username/password
  • POP3 — retrieves email from server; no MFA support
  • IMAP — retrieves/syncs email; no MFA support
  • Basic Auth in Exchange ActiveSync — used by some older mobile devices

Why legacy auth is dangerous: Conditional Access policies and MFA requirements in Entra ID apply to modern authentication flows only. If an account has legacy auth enabled, an attacker can authenticate directly against SMTP AUTH or IMAP with just a username and password — bypassing all MFA and Conditional Access completely.

This is why credential stuffers specifically target legacy auth endpoints — they can test millions of username/password combinations against smtp.office365.com:587 and if any account still has SMTP AUTH enabled, MFA is irrelevant.

0.14 Safe Links and Safe Attachments

These are the two core MDO Plan 1/Plan 2 features that protect against unknown and zero-day threats:

Safe Links

Safe Links rewrites every URL in every email to a Microsoft-controlled URL. When the user clicks the link:

  1. The click goes to Microsoft first
  2. Microsoft checks the destination URL against threat intelligence (in real time, at click time)
  3. If malicious: blocks the user and shows a warning
  4. If safe: redirects the user to the original URL

Why this matters: A phishing email can contain a benign URL at delivery time (passes static scanning) and the attacker changes the destination to malicious content after delivery. Safe Links catches this because the check happens at click time, not at delivery time.

Safe Attachments (Dynamic Delivery)

Instead of scanning attachments statically (known malware signatures), Safe Attachments sends every attachment to a detonation sandbox — a virtual machine where the file is opened and observed for malicious behavior.

Dynamic Delivery mode: Rather than holding the entire email while the attachment is scanned (which delays delivery), Dynamic Delivery:

  1. Delivers the email body immediately
  2. Replaces the attachment with a placeholder ("Attachment is being scanned")
  3. Re-attaches the original file once it passes sandbox analysis

0.15 Zero-Hour Auto Purge (ZAP)

ZAP is a MDO feature that retroactively removes or quarantines email that was already delivered to user mailboxes but was later identified as malicious.

Why it's needed: Email scanning is not perfect at delivery time. A URL may be benign when the email arrives (before the attacker activates the phishing page), or a malware signature may be published after the email was already delivered. ZAP continuously re-evaluates delivered email against updated threat intelligence and removes or quarantines matches — even after they're in the inbox.

ZAP acts on emails up to 48 hours after delivery. After 48 hours, human intervention via Threat Explorer is needed.

0.16 The Unified Audit Log (UAL)

The UAL is Microsoft 365's central audit log — a record of every significant action taken in the tenant. It captures:

  • Every email delivered, opened, forwarded, or deleted
  • Every admin configuration change (policy edits, connector changes)
  • Every OAuth consent grant
  • Every inbox rule creation or modification
  • Every sign-in and authentication event
  • Every eDiscovery search or export

Why it's critical for security: The UAL is the only forensic record proving what happened during an incident. Without it, you cannot determine the scope of a breach, what data was accessed, or what the attacker changed. It is also required for GDPR, SOX, and HIPAA breach notification.

UAL retention: By default, UAL is retained for 90 days for E3/E5 licenses. With Microsoft Purview Audit Premium, you can extend this to 1 year (or 10 years for specific records). For a slow-burn BEC actor who operates silently for 6 months, 90-day retention means most of their activity is permanently gone before you discover the incident.

0.17 Entra ID (formerly Azure Active Directory) and Conditional Access

Entra ID (Microsoft's cloud identity platform, formerly called Azure AD) is the identity provider for all of Microsoft 365. It handles:

  • User authentication (passwords, MFA)
  • OAuth token issuance for apps
  • Group membership and role assignment
  • Sign-in risk evaluation (Identity Protection)

Conditional Access is a policy engine in Entra ID that evaluates every authentication attempt against a set of conditions before granting access:

flowchart TD
    SignIn[User attempts sign-in] --> Signals[Evaluate signals]
    Signals -->|User, Device, Location, App, Risk| Policy[Conditional Access Policy]
    Policy -->|All conditions met| Grant[Grant access]
    Policy -->|Missing condition| MFA[Require MFA]
    Policy -->|High risk| Block[Block access]
    Policy -->|Unmanaged device| Compliant[Require device compliance]

Named Locations: You can define trusted IP ranges (office IPs) as Named Locations, then build policies that treat sign-ins from unknown locations with higher scrutiny.

Sign-in risk levels: Entra ID Identity Protection evaluates each sign-in for risk signals (leaked credentials, anonymous IP, impossible travel, unfamiliar sign-in properties) and assigns a risk level (low, medium, high). Conditional Access can block or require step-up authentication for high-risk sign-ins.

0.18 Lookalike Domains and Typosquatting

A lookalike domain is a domain registered by an attacker that visually resembles a legitimate company's domain:

Technique Example Why It Works
Character substitution corp0rate.com (zero instead of 'o') Easy to miss at a glance
Homoglyph attack corрorate.com (Cyrillic 'р' instead of Latin 'p') Visually identical in most fonts
Hyphen insertion corp-orate.com Looks plausible
TLD swap corporate.net instead of corporate.com Users don't always check the TLD
Subdomain spoofing corporate.com.attacker.io Browser shows corporate.com prominently
Extra character corporrate.com Typo that's easy to miss

Attackers register these domains and send email from them. Because corp0rate.com is a real domain with valid SPF/DKIM/DMARC, all authentication checks pass — the email is perfectly authenticated for corp0rate.com. DMARC protects corporate.com but has no control over corp0rate.com.

Detection: Certificate Transparency (CT) logs — every TLS certificate issued by any trusted CA is publicly logged. By monitoring CT logs for certificates with Levenshtein distance ≤ 2 from your domain portfolio, you detect lookalike domain registrations within minutes of the certificate being issued (which happens when a domain is first set up for HTTPS).

0.19 Certificate Transparency (CT) Logs

Every TLS certificate issued by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) is publicly logged in Certificate Transparency logs — append-only, publicly auditable logs. This was introduced to detect mis-issued certificates (e.g., a rogue CA issuing a cert for google.com).

For email security, CT logs serve a different purpose: early warning for lookalike domain registrations. When an attacker registers corp0rate.com and sets up HTTPS (which they need to send legitimate-looking phishing pages), they request a TLS certificate. That certificate appears in CT logs within minutes.

Tools like Certstream provide a real-time feed of all new CT log entries. By filtering this feed for certificates that closely resemble your domains, you get near-real-time notification of lookalike domain setup.

3.2 Authentication and Session Trust Flow

The most dangerous trust boundary in this scenario is not at the email level — it is at the identity level. An AiTM attack does not phish the password; it phishes the authenticated session cookie after MFA has already been completed.

sequenceDiagram
    participant User
    participant AiTM as AiTM Reverse Proxy (attacker-controlled)
    participant EntraID as Entra ID / O365 Login
    User->>AiTM: Clicks phishing link, lands on fake login page
    AiTM->>EntraID: Proxies all requests in real-time
    EntraID->>AiTM: MFA challenge presented
    AiTM->>User: Relays MFA challenge to victim
    User->>AiTM: Completes MFA (TOTP/push)
    AiTM->>EntraID: Relays MFA response
    EntraID->>AiTM: Issues authenticated session cookie
    Note over AiTM: Attacker captures session cookie
    AiTM->>User: Redirects to legitimate Office 365 (victim thinks login succeeded)
    Note over AiTM: Attacker replays session cookie independently

This is why TOTP-based MFA does not stop AiTM. Only phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2/passkeys) stops this attack because the credential is bound to the origin domain and cannot be proxied.

3.3 Mail Flow Trust Boundary — Migration Risk Zone

During migration, a critical trust boundary misconfiguration is the most likely operational error:

flowchart TD
    Internet[Internet] -->|MX record| EOP[Exchange Online Protection]

6.2 Detection Rules

Rule 1 — AiTM Session Reuse (High-Risk Sign-in Post-MFA)

Alert when Entra ID Sign-In log shows RiskLevelDuringSignIn = 'high'
AND ConditionalAccessStatus = 'success'
AND AuthenticationMethodsUsed != 'FIDO2 security key'
for any user in FinanceGroup OR ExecutiveGroup OR SecurityAdminGroup
within a single sign-in event.
  • Severity: Critical
  • MITRE: T1557, T1539
  • Source: Entra ID Sign-In Logs
  • False positives: Low — Entra ID Identity Protection's risk model is well-calibrated; legitimate travel should not trigger high risk
  • Tuning: Enable Named Locations for known office IPs; tune Identity Protection risk model with feedback from confirmed false positives over 30 days

Rule 2 — Inbox Forwarding Rule to External Destination

Alert when UAL Operation IN ('New-InboxRule','Set-InboxRule')
AND extracted ForwardTo/RedirectTo email domain NOT IN approved-internal-domains-list
for any mailbox.
Alert immediately for executive and finance mailboxes regardless of destination.
  • Severity: Critical for executive/finance; High for all others
  • MITRE: T1114.003
  • Source: Unified Audit Log
  • False positives: Low — external forwarding rules are almost never legitimate for employee mailboxes
  • Tuning: Pre-populate an approved-destinations list for known service mailboxes that have legitimate forwarding; alert on everything else without suppression

Rule 3 — Defender Policy Modification Outside Change Window

Alert when UAL Operation IN ('Set-AntiPhishPolicy','Set-SafeLinksPolicy',
'Set-SafeAttachmentPolicy','New-TransportRule','Set-TransportRule',
'Remove-TransportRule','Set-MalwareFilterPolicy')
AND event time NOT WITHIN approved_change_windows
OR event is NOT correlated to an open change ticket in ITSM system
  • Severity: High
  • MITRE: T1562.001 (Impair Defenses: Disable or Modify Tools)
  • Source: Unified Audit Log
  • False positives: Low with ITSM correlation; medium without
  • Tuning: Integrate ITSM change window data into SIEM; auto-correlate by change ticket number in UAL actor field

Rule 4 — DMARC Pass Rate Drop

Alert when daily DMARC aggregate report shows
(SPF pass count + DKIM pass count) / total_messages < 0.97
for your primary domain
after post-migration baseline period (30 days post-cutover).
  • Severity: Medium
  • Source: DMARC Aggregate Reports (rua inbox → SIEM ingestion)
  • False positives: High during migration; near-zero post-baseline
  • Tuning: Disable during migration period; enable 30 days post-cutover; threshold tightens from 97% to 99% after 90 days

Rule 5 — Lookalike Domain Certificate Issued

Alert when CT log feed returns a new certificate
WHERE CommonName or SAN has Levenshtein distance <= 2 from any domain in your_domain_portfolio
OR contains Unicode homoglyphs matching any monitored domain
AND certificate NotBefore > now - 24h
  • Severity: High
  • MITRE: T1583.001
  • Source: Certificate Transparency log feed (crt.sh, Certstream)
  • False positives: Medium — subsidiaries, legitimate partners, common word domains; tune with a known-legitimate exclusion list

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and Policy-as-Code

At Google scale, email security configuration for millions of mailboxes is not managed through an admin console — it is expressed as code with automated validation gates.

  • Email security policy as IaC: Anti-phishing policies, Safe Links configurations, transport rules, and Conditional Access policies are defined in Terraform or Pulumi manifests (via Microsoft Graph API). All changes go through code review, automated test against a shadow tenant, and canary deployment to a 1% user pilot group before fleet rollout.
  • DMARC progression automation: The p=none → p=quarantine → p=reject advancement is governed by an automated pipeline that reads DMARC aggregate reports, computes pass rates, and automatically opens a change ticket for advancement only when the pass rate threshold is met for 7 consecutive days.
  • Detection-as-code: All SIEM detection rules (inbox forwarding, AiTM patterns, OAuth consent anomalies) are checked into version control as YAML/SPL/KQL. Changes require code review, unit tests against synthetic log data, and staging deployment with canary traffic.
  • OAuth app governance pipeline: Any new OAuth consent generates an automated review ticket. Scope risk is auto-scored (Mail.ReadWrite.All = Critical, Mail.Read = High, Calendar.Read = Medium); critical-scope apps require CISO approval with a justification document before tokens are issued.

8.2 Fleet-Wide Impact

A misconfigured Safe Links policy or a global transport rule deployed to all mailboxes simultaneously is a service-affecting event at scale.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

The core challenge here isn't just "migrate email scanning to a new vendor" — it's that you're shifting from an on-prem appliance where you control the inspection pipeline entirely to a shared-cloud service where the trust model is fundamentally different. When I think about the top threats, two stand out. The first is AiTM phishing — during the migration window, users are conditioned to receive legitimate "verify your account" emails, making them more susceptible to phishing lures; and because most users will still be on TOTP-based MFA rather than FIDO2, a well-crafted AiTM proxy can capture an authenticated session cookie after MFA is satisfied and reuse it indefinitely. The second is a migration artifact risk: the inbound connector allow-list that whitelisted the old Proofpoint IP range must be deleted the same day Proofpoint is decommissioned. If it's left in place, any attacker who learns the old relay IP range can route email directly to Exchange Online bypassing every Defender inspection layer.

My top three controls before go-live are: first, FIDO2 hardware keys for all finance and executive mailboxes — the only phishing-resistant MFA that stops AiTM cold because the credential is cryptographically bound to the origin domain. Second, a transport rule blocking all external auto-forwarding before any user accounts are migrated, so the moment an account is compromised the attacker can't set a forwarding rule. Third, DMARC at p=none on day one with a formal progression plan to p=reject within 60 days — you need the monitoring data from aggregate reports before you can safely enforce. For detection, the most important signal is inbox rule creation with external forwarding destination — that event in the Unified Audit Log is the fingerprint of virtually every BEC post-compromise action, and it should page the SOC within five minutes. The residual risk I'd be honest about is that general users without FIDO2 remain vulnerable to AiTM for the duration of the hardware key rollout, and a sophisticated BEC actor doesn't need to compromise email at all — they can run the attack entirely from a lookalike domain, which is purely a social engineering problem that no email security tool fully solves.

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: An attacker registers a lookalike domain one day after your SPF record is published. Walk me through your detection and response.

The detection comes from a Certificate Transparency log feed — specifically Certstream or the crt.sh API, which I have integrated into the SIEM with a watchlist of all registered corporate domains plus their computed typosquats and Unicode homoglyphs. When the attacker registers corp0rate.com and requests a TLS certificate, that certificate appears in CT logs within minutes and triggers an alert. My immediate response is to add the domain to Defender for Office 365's Tenant Allow/Block List so any email from that domain is blocked, and simultaneously add it to Safe Links' blocked URL list. I then register the lookalike domain defensively if still available — approximately $10 and it permanently closes the vector. If the DMARC aggregate report or any employee reports phishing activity from that domain targeting our customers, I issue a customer advisory, engage the registrar for UDRP takedown, and notify the CA if a fraudulent cert was issued claiming our brand.

Follow-up 2: DMARC p=reject breaks a critical third-party payroll system that sends email on behalf of your domain. How do you handle it?

This is exactly why you run DMARC at p=none for 30 days before advancing to p=reject — the aggregate reports (rua) identify every IP sending on behalf of your domain, including the payroll system's cloud mail relay. When the payroll vendor shows up in the report as failing SPF alignment, you have two options: add their sending IP range to your SPF record with an include: statement, or — better — require them to sign outbound mail with DKIM using a dedicated selector for your domain and add that key to your DNS. You do not roll back DMARC policy. The payroll system gets a 48-hour remediation SLA; if they cannot comply, they get a temporary pct=0 carve-out in the DMARC record while the fix is implemented, which affects only their sending volume without touching enforcement for all other senders.

Follow-up 3: How do you model "time to complete BEC" risk in this architecture after migration?

I model it as a kill chain with time estimates: a BEC actor typically needs 3–5 days to establish a foothold via AiTM or credential stuffing, 1–2 days to monitor the target mailbox silently and identify an active payment workflow, and then they execute on a real payment approval window — which typically means they need 10–30 minutes to intercept and redirect one wire transfer instruction before anyone notices. My target is to collapse that timeline with detection: inbox rule creation fires an alert within 5 minutes, AiTM session anomaly detection within 15 minutes, and automated session revocation within 30 minutes. The residual window I accept is the 5-minute gap between rule creation and alert; to compensate, I implement an out-of-band verification policy requiring a phone callback for any wire transfer above a defined threshold — because that control works even if the email security stack is completely bypassed.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

Mistake Why It Is Weak What a Stronger Answer Should Say
"Enable MFA and the phishing problem is solved" TOTP and push-based MFA are bypassed by AiTM attacks which are now widely available as-a-service (Caffeine, Greatness, Evilginx2). Stopping at "MFA is on" misses the most active phishing technique in use today. Distinguish phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2/passkeys — bound to origin domain, cannot be proxied) from TOTP/push MFA (session cookie is still stealable post-authentication). Specify FIDO2 for high-risk roles immediately; Entra ID Conditional Access sign-in risk policy as compensating control for remaining users.
"DMARC blocks spoofing of our domain" DMARC only protects against spoofing of the From: header domain — it does nothing against lookalike domain attacks (corp0rate.com passes DMARC for corp0rate.com), reply-to attacks, or compromised legitimate partner domains. Treating DMARC as the complete anti-spoofing solution misses the other attack paths. Explain DMARC's exact scope (header-from domain alignment); separately address lookalike domains via CT log monitoring and domain registration monitoring; separately address compromised-legitimate-sender threats via Defender's reputation and ML models.
Forgetting the migration connector allow-list cleanup Many candidates design a perfect future-state architecture but miss operational security during and immediately after migration. The window between "Proofpoint decommissioned" and "connector allow-list removed" is when the most common migration-related bypass occurs. Explicitly include a day-one cutover checklist item: connector allow-list removal must be a hard gate before decommission sign-off; verify with a mail trace that no traffic routes through the old path.
Overlooking the OAuth app threat surface Candidates focus on email-level phishing and miss that an attacker who compromises an admin account or tricks one admin into consenting a malicious OAuth app gains persistent Mail.ReadWrite.All access that survives password resets and MFA changes. Address OAuth app governance explicitly: disable user consent for sensitive scopes; require admin consent workflow for any mail-access permission; monthly audit of all consented apps; immediate revocation of anomalous apps.
No logging retention strategy Answering the detection question without addressing retention means that a sophisticated BEC actor operating for 6 months is invisible — the 90-day default UAL retention has already expired by the time the incident is discovered. Specify UAL retention strategy: Purview Audit Premium for 1-year retention on critical mailboxes, or real-time streaming of UAL to SIEM for long-term archival; explain why this matters for forensic scope determination and breach notification obligation compliance.
