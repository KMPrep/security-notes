Scenario 5 — Enterprise PKI Modernization

A 15-year-old two-tier PKI (offline Root CA, issuing CA) is being replaced. The new design uses an HSM-backed Root, two subordinate CAs, and an OCSP responder. Certificate lifecycle is managed by a new automated platform.

Candidate questions:

  • What are the top three threats to the offline Root CA during its rare activation windows?
  • How do you model a rogue certificate issuance threat?
  • What controls prevent a compromised service account from requesting certificates for sensitive systems?
  • How do you monitor for certificate mis-issuance or unexpected CT log entries?
  • What is your revocation strategy if a subordinate CA key is compromised?
  • Follow-up: Your OCSP responder has a 4-hour cache TTL. What is the residual risk window?

1. ASSETS — What are we protecting?

Asset Sensitivity Why It Matters What Goes Wrong If Compromised
Root CA private key (in HSM) Critical The anchor of all trust in the PKI hierarchy; its signature validates every subordinate CA An attacker with this key can issue any certificate trusted by every relying party — TLS certs, code-signing certs, client auth certs — for any domain or identity for the key's lifetime (typically 20+ years)
Subordinate CA private keys Critical Used to issue end-entity certificates for TLS, code signing, and client auth at scale A compromised sub-CA key enables rogue certificate issuance for any subject the CA is authorized to sign; revocation requires entire sub-CA revocation, cascading to all live certs it issued
Root CA activation ceremony materials Critical Includes HSM operator cards, PINs, ceremony scripts, key custodian identities; required to bring the offline Root online Exposure before or during a ceremony enables unauthorized Root operations; loss can cause key unavailability, blocking future sub-CA issuance
Certificate lifecycle automation platform High Issues, renews, and revokes certificates on behalf of thousands of services; holds service-account credentials A compromised automation platform can request certificates for arbitrary subjects, creating a scalable rogue-issuance channel without direct HSM access
OCSP responder High Real-time revocation status service; clients query before trusting a certificate Compromise allows an attacker to return false "good" status for revoked certs, preventing revocation from taking effect; DoS attack prevents revocation checks, causing soft-fail clients to accept revoked certs
CRL distribution points (CDPs) High Offline revocation list fallback; clients use when OCSP is unavailable Corruption or DoS prevents revocation propagation to clients not supporting OCSP stapling
CA database and audit log High Authoritative record of every certificate ever issued; required for incident scoping and forensic investigation Tampering hides rogue issuance; deletion prevents determining scope of a compromise; gaps in continuity undermine legal and compliance use
Certificate templates / enrollment policies High Define what subjects, key usages, SANs, and lifetimes can be requested Overly permissive templates allow service accounts to request certificates for out-of-scope subjects (e.g., requesting a cert for *.google.com from a low-trust template)
HSM management interfaces High Remote management of HSM firmware, policies, and admin roles Unauthorized HSM admin access can extract keys, wipe the device, or downgrade to vulnerable firmware
Service account credentials (automation platform) High Authenticate automated certificate requests; bound to enrollment policy Exfiltrated credentials allow arbitrary cert requests limited only by the template's scope; long-lived secrets increase dwell time
PKI operator admin accounts Medium Manage CA policy, templates, and enrollment rules Privilege escalation to CA admin enables template tampering, policy bypass, or unauthorized issuance
Network infrastructure (CA segment) Medium Isolates CA servers and OCSP responder from general enterprise traffic Lateral movement from adjacent networks enables attacks against subordinate CAs that have network connectivity
CT log monitoring configuration Medium Ensures all issued certificates are detected and attributed Gaps in domain coverage allow rogue certs to go undetected until they are used

3.2 Root CA Activation (Ceremony) Trust Flow

The ceremony is the highest-risk event in the entire PKI lifecycle. Trust flows from physical custodians through a tightly controlled air-gapped environment.

    participant AirGap as Air-Gapped Root CA Workstation

    Custodian1->>Ceremony: Announce presence and authenticate identity (biometric + photo ID)
    Custodian2->>Ceremony: Announce presence and authenticate identity

    Custodian1->>HSM: Insert operator smart card (shard 1 of M-of-N)
    Custodian2->>HSM: Insert operator smart card (shard 2 of M-of-N)

    AirGap->>AirGap: Sign sub-CA CSR / revoke sub-CA cert
    AirGap->>HSM: Store updated CRL / new sub-CA cert
    Custodian1->>HSM: Remove smart card
    Custodian2->>HSM: Remove smart card
    AirGap->>AirGap: Power down, verify disk state
    Auditor->>Ceremony: Sign ceremony transcript

3.4 Highest-Risk Boundary Crossing

Root CA activation window is the single highest-risk trust boundary crossing.

Dimension Detail
Why it is high risk The offline Root CA must be physically activated to sign sub-CA certificates or issue CRLs. During this window, the Root CA's key is temporarily accessible. Any compromise here is irrecoverable without a full PKI re-anchor.
Assumptions that must hold All key custodians are non-colluding; the air-gapped workstation has not been tampered with since last use; HSM firmware is authentic; smart card chain of custody is unbroken; ceremony location is physically secure.
Required controls M-of-N quorum (minimum 3-of-5); dual-control physical access; independent auditor present; recorded ceremony with timestamped video; stripped-down OS with no network interfaces; HSM tamper-evidence check before activation; signed ceremony transcript stored offline.
Logs that prove correct operation HSM audit trail (timestamps, operator card identities, operations performed); independent auditor sign-off; video recording hash stored separately; signed ceremony transcript; CA audit log showing only expected operations during the window.

6. MONITORING & DETECTION STRATEGY

6.1 Top Log Sources

Log Source Key Fields Why It Matters Example Detection Use Case
CA Audit Log (ADCS Event Log / EJBCA Audit DB) Certificate serial, requester identity, template used, subject CN, SANs, issue timestamp, request source IP Primary record of every certificate issued, revoked, or denied; proves or disproves any rogue issuance claim Detect issuance of cert with out-of-scope SAN; detect issuance rate spike; detect revocation of a cert not in the expected workflow

6.2 Detection Rules

Rule 1 — CT Log: Rogue Certificate for Monitored Domain

Alert when a certificate appears in a public CT log for any SAN matching
[*.corp.example.com, corp.example.com, *.example.com] (monitored domain list)
AND the issuing CA certificate OID does NOT appear in the authorized CA OID registry
within 5 minutes of CT log ingestion.
  • Severity: CRITICAL (P0)
  • MITRE: T1588.003 (Obtain Capabilities — Code Signing Certs), T1557 (Adversary-in-the-Middle)
  • Log source: CT log API feed
  • False positives: Low — only fires on unexpected CA OIDs; known CAs are allowlisted
  • Tuning: Maintain authoritative CA OID allowlist; update when new sub-CAs are added; suppress during planned sub-CA transitions with a time-bounded exception

Rule 2 — CA Template Modification Without Approved Change Ticket

Alert when Windows Security Event ID 4899 (certificate template modified) OR EJBCA
audit event (CERTIFICATE_PROFILE_EDIT) fires on any CA server
AND no ITSM change ticket in APPROVED state exists for that CA server at the time of the event.
  • Severity: HIGH (P1)
  • MITRE: T1484 (Domain Policy Modification)
  • Log source: ADCS Security Event Log; ITSM API for change ticket correlation
  • False positives: Low — any unticketted template modification is a policy violation
  • Tuning: SOAR queries ITSM at alert time to check open change tickets; tickets must reference CA server hostname in scope field

Rule 3 — Subordinate CA Issues Certificate During Off-Hours Without Change Window

Alert when the CA audit log records a certificate issuance
AND the request timestamp falls outside business hours (18:00–08:00 local CA time, weekends, holidays)
AND no automated renewal job ID is associated with the request (manual or unusual requester)
AND the certificate subject is NOT in the automation platform's pre-approved renewal queue.
  • Severity: HIGH (P1)
  • MITRE: T1078 (Valid Accounts)
  • Log source: CA issuance log; automation platform renewal queue API
  • False positives: Medium — some legitimate after-hours renewals; tuning requires automation platform to stamp a job correlation ID on every automated request
  • Tuning: Automation platform injects a signed job token into every CSR; CA audit log records it; alert suppressed when valid job token present

Rule 4 — OCSP Serial Query Spike for Revoked Certificate

Alert when OCSP responder logs show a single certificate serial number (known revoked)
receives more than 50 unique client IP queries within any 10-minute window.
  • Severity: MEDIUM (P2)
  • MITRE: T1557 (Adversary-in-the-Middle)
  • Log source: OCSP responder access log
  • False positives: Low — revoked certs should not receive active OCSP queries in production
  • Tuning: Maintain revoked serial list in SIEM; query spike indicates active use of a revoked certificate post-revocation

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and Policy-as-Code

At Google scale, manual PKI operations (template changes, enrollment policy updates, revocation) become bottlenecks and error sources. Every PKI policy must be expressed as code:

  • Certificate template policy-as-code: Templates defined in Terraform or a custom PKI-as-code DSL; changes go through PR review with required approvals from PKI team lead and a security reviewer; applied via CI/CD pipeline with automated validation against schema and policy constraints.
  • Enrollment policy-as-code: Service account → allowed template → allowed SAN regex mappings stored in a policy repository (e.g., OPA/Rego); automation platform queries OPA at request time; policy changes require PR merge and do not require manual config changes on CA servers.
  • CT monitoring-as-code: Domain allowlists, CA OID registries, and detection rules stored in version-controlled configs; deployed to monitoring infrastructure automatically; PRs to add domains or CA OIDs are the only way to suppress alerts for new issuances.
  • Certificate health dashboards: Automated daily scan of all certificates in the issuance database; dashboard shows certs expiring within 30 days, certs missing CT log entries, certs with non-standard SANs, and certs with EKUs that don't match their assigned template.
  • Canary enforcement for policy changes: New enrollment policy changes applied to a 1% canary of service accounts first; rollout blocked if issuance failure rate increases more than 0.1% above baseline.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-Second Version

Spoken answer for opening the response:

"PKI modernization is deceptively complex because the trust anchor — the Root CA — can never be taken online carelessly, and a single misstep creates irrecoverable trust damage across the entire organization.

My threat model centers on three core risks: first, the Root CA activation ceremony itself, where physical access, custodian collusion, or supply-chain compromise of the HSM are the primary attack vectors; second, rogue certificate issuance enabled by misconfigured certificate templates — specifically ESC1-style attacks where a low-privileged service account can request a certificate for an arbitrary subject; and third, a compromised subordinate CA key, which is operationally the most dangerous because revocation cascades to every certificate that sub-CA ever issued.

My top three controls are: FIPS 140-2 Level 3 HSMs with M-of-N ceremony quorum for the Root CA — minimum three-of-five custodians from different teams — to prevent any single insider from activating the root; strict certificate template enforcement with policy-module-validated subject constraints, disabling user-controlled SANs and requiring enrollment agent authorization; and mandatory Certificate Transparency submission with automated CT log monitoring that pages on any unexpected CA OID within ten minutes of log ingestion.

For detection, my anchor is CT log monitoring as an out-of-band signal that catches rogue certs from any CA in the trust store, not just mine. I back this with CA audit log alerting for template modifications outside change windows and OCSP synthetic monitoring to verify revocation is working.

The honest residual risk is the four-hour OCSP cache window — a revoked cert is still accepted by cached clients. I mitigate this with OCSP stapling and short certificate lifetimes, but I would clearly document this window in the risk register for the CISO to own. The other residual risk I cannot fully solve is a nation-state attacking the HSM supply chain before delivery — I mitigate that with vendor attestation and FIPS validation, but accept it as a residual risk requiring executive sign-off."

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: Your OCSP responder has a 4-hour cache TTL. What is the residual risk window?

The practical revocation window is up to 4 hours from the moment we publish a revocation until all cached OCSP responses expire across relying parties. During that window, an attacker holding the compromised certificate can still successfully complete TLS handshakes with clients that cached a "good" response before revocation. For high-sensitivity certificate classes — admin client auth, code signing — I would reduce the TTL to 15 minutes, accepting the 16× increase in OCSP responder load as a trade-off. For bulk TLS certs, I accept the 4-hour window and compensate by enforcing OCSP stapling so the server must refresh the stapled response before it expires, which bounds the client-side cache effect. The residual risk that cannot be eliminated is clients that do not support OCSP at all — a separate problem addressed by short cert lifetimes.

Follow-up 2: What is your revocation strategy if a subordinate CA key is compromised?

The sub-CA certificate is signed by the Root CA, so revocation requires an emergency Root CA ceremony to add the sub-CA serial to the Root CA CRL. The ceremony takes a minimum of 4 hours from the point we convene the key custodians, which is why the ceremony runbook is drilled quarterly and custodians are reachable 24/7. In parallel: immediately suspend the compromised sub-CA's enrollment API so no new certificates can be issued; revoke all end-entity certificates issued by that sub-CA en masse using the automation platform's bulk revocation API; stand up a replacement sub-CA signed by a clean Root CA CRL; and initiate re-issuance for all affected services. The most painful aspect is that even after Root CRL publication, the 4-hour OCSP cache means browsers may still trust certs from the revoked sub-CA for a short window — which is why OCSP stapling on all server-side TLS is critical, as servers must re-validate their stapled response before it expires.

Follow-up 3: How do you prevent a compromised service account from requesting certificates for sensitive systems?

The control is two-layered: first, the automation platform enforces a pre-approved SAN/CN allowlist per service account using OPA policy — the request never reaches the CA if the subject is out of scope. Second, the CA itself is configured with a custom policy module that independently validates subject constraints: only subjects matching the pre-approved enrollment policy for the authenticating service account are accepted. This defense-in-depth means a bypass of the automation platform still hits the CA policy module. For the highest-sensitivity templates — admin auth, code signing — I require an additional enrollment agent authorization step: a second service account with the "enrollment agent" role must countersign the CSR before the CA accepts it, implementing four-eyes control at the CA level. This is ADCS-native via the "enrollment agents restrict" setting, or equivalent custom logic in EJBCA.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

Mistake Why It Is Weak Stronger Answer
"Enable certificate transparency logging" without explaining detection CT submission is useless without monitoring. Submitting to logs and never watching them means a rogue cert could exist for the full certificate validity period (1–2 years) before discovery. Specify automated CT log feed ingestion, domain allowlist-based alerting, authorized CA OID registry, and a MTTD target of < 10 minutes for unexpected certificate detection.
"Revoke the sub-CA certificate" without explaining the ceremony dependency Revoking a sub-CA requires the offline Root CA to issue a new CRL — this is not a button click. Saying "just revoke it" signals unfamiliarity with how PKI revocation actually works at the Root CA level. Explain the emergency Root CA activation ceremony (M-of-N quorum, minimum 4-hour timeline), parallel actions (suspend sub-CA, bulk-revoke end-entities, stand up replacement), and the OCSP cache window residual risk.
Focusing only on network controls (firewalls, network segmentation) for CA security PKI security is primarily about key custody, access control, and operational procedures — not network controls. A nation-state or insider with physical access bypasses network controls entirely. Lead with HSM design (FIPS level, M-of-N quorum, air-gap), ceremony controls (dual-person, auditor, recording), and identity controls (FIDO2 for CA admins, RBAC for templates) before mentioning network controls.
Accepting default certificate validity periods (1–2 years) Long-lived certificates extend the blast radius of any key compromise — a 2-year cert issued the day before a sub-CA compromise is exploitable for nearly 2 years even after revocation (due to client caching). This means OCSP DoS is a valid revocation bypass.
