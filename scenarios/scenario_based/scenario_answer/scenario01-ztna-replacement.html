Scenario 01 — Corporate VPN Replacement with Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA)

Scenario: A 20,000-employee enterprise is replacing its hub-and-spoke VPN with a ZTNA product. Users access internal SaaS, on-prem SAP, and cloud workloads from managed and BYOD devices.

Candidate questions:

  • What trust boundaries change when VPN is removed? How do you model device identity?
  • How do you handle lateral movement risk if a ZTNA connector is compromised?
  • What controls prevent a BYOD device with a stolen credential from gaining access?
  • How do you monitor for impossible-travel or context-anomaly events in ZTNA logs?
  • What residual risk remains if the identity provider (IdP) itself is compromised?
  • Follow-up: The IdP team says MFA is enabled. Is that sufficient? Why or why not?
  • Follow-up: How would you model the ZTNA vendor as a third-party threat?

3.4 Highest-risk boundary crossing

The most dangerous trust boundary is the ZTNA cloud broker ↔ on-prem connector tunnel. In the old VPN model, the network perimeter was the trust boundary — once inside, you were trusted. ZTNA moves trust decisions to identity and policy, but the connector still represents a hard internal network foothold. If an attacker gains code execution on the connector host (via a CVE in connector software), they bypass all ZTNA policy controls because the connector host has pre-authorized network routes to application subnets. At that point, the attacker is operating within the internal network as if they had VPN access — and the ZTNA controls are completely moot.

The second critical boundary is IdP ↔ ZTNA broker trust. The entire ZTNA architecture assumes the IdP's authentication assertions are trustworthy. If the IdP is compromised — whether through admin account takeover, malicious SAML configuration, or a supply-chain attack on the IdP itself — the attacker can forge identity assertions that ZTNA will accept unconditionally. This is why defense-in-depth must include anomaly detection on ZTNA access patterns even after authentication succeeds.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

At 20,000 users, manual ZTNA policy management breaks down within months. Every ZTNA access policy must be defined in Terraform + OPA Rego policies, stored in GitHub, with mandatory PR review and automated pre-merge linting via opa check and conftest. All policy changes are diff-audited: an automated pipeline generates a human-readable risk diff showing net-new access grants, wildcard expansions, and connector-to-subnet changes before merge. Access review cycles are fully automated using HR system (Workday) as source of truth: when a user's role changes or they offboard, an automated job triggers ZTNA policy entitlement revocation within 4 hours. At Google's BeyondCorp scale, every policy evaluation is logged, and anomaly detection on policy evaluation outcomes (DENY rate changes) provides early warning of policy drift.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A single compromised ZTNA connector servicing SAP ERP at a 20,000-person enterprise gives an attacker access to the financial backbone of the organization — potential blast radius is every SAP record, financial transaction, and HR data point. At Google scale (100,000+ employees, global infrastructure), a compromised ZTNA cloud broker (e.g., Zscaler ZPA platform breach) could expose every enterprise customer simultaneously — this is precisely why Google operates BeyondCorp Enterprise as first-party infrastructure rather than relying on a third-party SaaS broker. Blast radius must be minimized by scoping each connector to the minimum set of applications it can reach — a connector for SAP should have no route to cloud workloads, and vice versa. Connector proliferation (one connector per application group) trades operational complexity for blast radius reduction.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

ZTNA exception requests ("please allow BYOD access to SAP for 3 days") cannot go through manual approval queues at scale. Replace with a self-service policy engine: user submits a time-limited exception request through a JIRA-integrated portal; the request triggers automated risk scoring (device posture + user risk score from IdP + data classification of target app); requests below risk threshold are auto-approved with automatic expiry; high-risk requests escalate to security team. Emergency connector deployments must be zero-touch: connectors deployed as immutable GCP Compute Engine instances via Terraform, certificates issued automatically by Vault PKI secrets engine backed by Cloud KMS, no SSH access to connector hosts (management via Cloud Identity-Aware Proxy with MFA). Human approval gates should exist only for: Critical application access grants, policy changes affecting >1,000 users, and connector deployments to new application subnets.

"The key shift in ZTNA is that we're moving from network-perimeter trust to identity-plus-device-plus-context trust. The assets we're protecting are user identities, the ZTNA connectors, the IdP that underpins all access decisions, and the applications — especially SAP which is the highest-value on-prem target.

The top three threat actors I'd focus on are: nation-state APTs targeting SAP via IdP compromise, ransomware operators using ZTNA connectors as their internal pivot point, and AiTM phishers who can bypass legacy MFA entirely using session cookie replay.

The riskiest trust boundary is the ZTNA connector itself — if it's compromised, the attacker bypasses all ZTNA policy and gains internal network access. So I'd segment each connector to the minimum required app subnets, run connectors as immutable instances with no inbound Internet exposure, and have NSX-T microsegmentation as the backstop.

For controls, I'd layer phishing-resistant FIDO2 MFA at the identity layer, device certificate enrollment plus continuous posture assessment at the device layer, and least-privilege per-app ZTNA policies with OPA policy-as-code at the policy layer. For detection, I'd instrument impossible-travel in the SIEM, session token replay detection, and connector availability monitoring.

Residual risk I can't fully eliminate: IdP supply-chain compromise removes the root of trust for everything, and a ZTNA vendor platform breach could affect all tenants simultaneously. LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: The IdP team says MFA is enabled. Is that sufficient? Why or why not?

No — MFA enabled is necessary but not sufficient. The critical question is which MFA method. TOTP, SMS, and push notification MFA are all vulnerable to AiTM proxy phishing (EvilGinx2 can intercept a TOTP token in real-time) and SIM swapping. The only MFA that defeats AiTM is phishing-resistant MFA: FIDO2/WebAuthn, where the cryptographic assertion is origin-bound to the legitimate domain and cannot be replayed by a proxy. "MFA is enabled" without specifying phishing-resistant methods is a false sense of security — I'd require FIDO2 for all admins immediately and have a roadmap to roll it out to all 20,000 employees within 12 months.

Follow-up 2: How would you model the ZTNA vendor as a third-party threat?

I'd treat the ZTNA vendor as an untrusted third party with privileged access to the control plane — similar to how you'd model a cloud provider in a shared responsibility model. The specific threats are: (1) malicious update to the connector binary (T1195.002 — supply chain compromise), (2) breach of the vendor's SaaS platform giving attacker visibility into all tenant configurations and access logs, and (3) vendor insider planting a backdoor in connector firmware. Mitigations: require SBOM for each connector release, pin connector versions and test updates in staging before fleet-wide push, monitor ZTNA vendor security advisories with automated CVE tracking, include breach notification SLA (< 4 hours) in contract, and design the architecture to limit what the vendor platform itself can see (data in transit encrypted end-to-end, not visible to broker).

Follow-up 3: What happens if an attacker compromises a ZTNA connector — what is your next layer?

The connector is inside the corporate network, so the next layer must be network-level. I'd have NSX-T microsegmentation policies that restrict the connector host to only the TCP ports and destination IPs required for its specific application group — it should not be able to reach AD, other application subnets, or management networks. I'd also have network flow log monitoring to alert on any connection from the connector host to out-of-scope destinations. If compromise is confirmed, the response is: automated NSX-T firewall block on the connector VLAN, revoke connector credentials from the ZTNA cloud broker, deploy a replacement connector from an immutable image, and snapshot the connector VM for forensics. The key is the blast radius is limited to the connector's assigned application subnet — not the full internal network.

Follow-up 4: You mentioned FIDO2 phishing-resistant MFA. What happens if that control fails — what is your next layer?

FIDO2 itself is extremely resistant to compromise — the private key never leaves the hardware token and the assertion is origin-bound. But failure modes exist: (1) the user registers an attacker-controlled authenticator (account takeover during enrollment), (2) the hardware key is physically stolen and PIN is compromised, (3) a zero-day in the FIDO2 stack itself. The next layer is continuous access monitoring: even with a valid FIDO2 assertion, ZTNA access is continuously evaluated against behavioral baselines. Impossible travel detection, device posture checks, and session duration limits would catch a stolen token being used from an attacker's location. Additionally, short session lifetimes (4-hour max with re-authentication) limit the window of exploitation. The IdP conditional access policies also provide a backstop — high-risk sign-in signals trigger step-up challenges even for users with valid MFA.

Follow-up 5: If you were the attacker, what is the cheapest path through your control set?

The cheapest path is an AiTM phishing attack targeting a BYOD employee who's using push notification MFA instead of FIDO2. I'd send a convincing phishing email with a reverse-proxy landing page (EvilGinx2), capture the session cookie after the employee completes MFA on the legitimate IdP, then replay that session cookie to the ZTNA broker before it expires. If the ZTNA broker doesn't implement token binding and session cookies aren't short-lived, I have a 24-hour window of authenticated access. The detection gap is that from the ZTNA broker's perspective, this looks like a legitimate authenticated session. My detection relies on the impossible travel rule firing — which only works if the attacker is operating from a geographically impossible location. A sophisticated attacker using residential proxies in the same city would evade that detection and likely go undetected until UEBA behavioral anomalies flag the unusual app access pattern.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Saying "MFA protects against credential theft" without qualifying the MFA method. SMS, TOTP, and push MFA are all defeated by AiTM proxies. Always specify FIDO2/WebAuthn when discussing phishing-resistant MFA, and explain why it's origin-bound.
  • Conflating "network access removed" with "lateral movement eliminated." ZTNA removes network-level VPN access but a compromised ZTNA connector is still a network-layer foothold inside the corporate network. Microsegmentation around the connector is a distinct and necessary control.
  • Treating the IdP as a trusted black box. The IdP is the highest-value attack target in a ZTNA architecture because its compromise invalidates every downstream control. Candidates who don't model IdP compromise as a top residual risk are missing the single biggest architectural dependency.
  • Forgetting BYOD as a distinct threat surface. BYOD devices run unmanaged software, may be infected, and have weaker posture guarantees than managed devices. A common mistake is applying the same access policy to managed and BYOD devices without differentiation.
  • Describing ZTNA as "zero trust" without explaining what trust decisions are still being made. ZTNA makes explicit trust decisions on every session based on identity, device, and context — it doesn't eliminate trust, it makes it continuous and explicit. Interviewers will probe this distinction.
  • Not addressing the ZTNA vendor as a threat actor. The vendor's cloud broker has visibility into all authentication events and access logs. A vendor platform compromise is a Tier 1 incident affecting all tenants. Candidates who don't mention supply chain risk for the ZTNA vendor are missing a critical threat.
  • Overlooking policy management as an attack surface. ZTNA policies define what access is allowed — an attacker or insider who can modify policies can silently grant themselves access to any application. Policy-as-code with GitOps is not just an operational best practice; it's a security control.
  • Not distinguishing session token theft from credential theft. FIDO2 eliminates credential phishing, but session tokens issued post-authentication can still be stolen (T1539 — browser credential theft). Short token lifetimes and DPoP token binding are the next-layer mitigations — missing these shows shallow understanding of the attack surface.
