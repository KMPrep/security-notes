Corporate DNS Security — Threat Exploitation and Blue-Team Control Review

Source file reviewed: scenario14-corporate-dns-security.md
Generated artifact: scenario14-corporate-dns-security-Attack-Review-Enhanced.md
Reviewer role: Cybersecurity Architect / Engineer Lead — 15+ years purple-team experience
Scenario: Enterprise uses split-horizon DNS with on-prem recursive resolvers, a public authoritative DNS, and endpoints permitting DNS-over-HTTPS; recent questions focus on DNS tunneling, cache poisoning, DoH visibility loss, and zone data leakage.

1. Review Objective

This document analyzes how DNS can be abused for reconnaissance, C2, exfiltration, and domain takeover, and evaluates controls to prevent or detect DNS-based attacks, including DoH considerations, DNSSEC management, registrar protection, resolver hardening, RPZ usage, and telemetry required for detection.

2. Highest-Risk Design Decision

The highest-risk design decision is allowing endpoints to use encrypted DNS (DoH/DoT) to external providers or failing to centralize and enforce a managed DoH resolver. When endpoints resolve via external DoH, enterprise visibility is lost and detection/mitigation controls (firewall, RPZ, DLP) are bypassed, enabling stealthy DNS C2 and data exfiltration. The safe choice is to mandate enterprise-managed DoH with enforced MDM/Chrome policies and firewall blocks for external DoH providers.

3. Attack-Tree Legend

  • Red nodes (:::attack): attacker actions or attacker-controlled state.
  • Blue nodes (:::control): defensive control decision points.
  • Green nodes (:::blocked): attack stopped or materially contained.
  • Yellow nodes (:::impact): business or security impact if the chain succeeds.

6. Threat-by-Threat Technical Review

T1 — DNS Tunneling (exfil/C2)

STRIDE category: Information Disclosure
Risk: Critical likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Establish a DNS-based covert channel (TXT, NULL, long subdomain) to exfiltrate data or maintain command-and-control when other channels are monitored.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: attacker registers a domain under their control that resolves to a DNS server capable of handling tunneled queries.
  • Step 1: malware on endpoint encodes data in subdomain labels and issues DNS queries to the attack domain.
  • Step 2: resolver forwards the query to public authoritative server; attacker decodes the data from the qname or TXT response.
  • Step 3: attacker responds with encoded commands in TXT or A records.
  • Step 4: attacker maintains a low-and-slow channel to avoid volume-based detection.
  • Step 5: impact: exfiltration bypasses endpoint DLP and HTTP/HTTPS monitoring.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: block direct outbound UDP/TCP 53 from endpoints; force all DNS through managed resolvers.
  • Control 2: detect tunneling via entropy analysis, long subdomain length, and excessive NXDOMAIN rates per host.
  • Control 3: RPZ sinkholing for known malicious domains and aggressive block-lists for suspicious TLDs.
  • Control 4: inspect DoH endpoints and ensure DoH is enterprise-managed; block external DoH providers.
  • Control 5: Egress filtering to block plaintext DNS to external resolvers and known DoH IPs.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • SIEM DNS analytics: high NXDOMAIN counts, long qnames, high entropy in subdomains for a single host.
  • Resolver logs: many TXT queries to same external domain from single client IP.
  • Proxy logs/Firewall: HTTPS flows to known DoH provider IPs flagged.
  • Host EDR: process creating DNS queries from uncommon binaries.

Purple-team validation commands

# Simulate DNS tunneling detection by generating many NXDOMAIN queries with high-entropy labels
for i in {1..600}; do dig +short random$(openssl rand -hex 16).malicious.example @<corp-resolver>; done

// Detect tunneling patterns: high NXDOMAIN + entropy
DnsQueryLogs
| where ClientIP in (<corporate_range>)
| extend LabelLen = strlen(qname)
| summarize NXCount = countif(ResponseCode == "NXDOMAIN"), Total = count(), AvgLen = avg(LabelLen) by ClientIP
| where NXCount > 200 and AvgLen > 30

T2 — DoH Bypass to External Provider

STRIDE category: Tampering
Risk: High likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Use DoH to encrypt DNS traffic to an external provider, bypassing enterprise resolvers, RPZs, and DNS inspection, enabling C2 and obfuscated exfiltration.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: endpoint supports DoH and is not constrained by MDM/Chrome policies; firewall allows HTTPS to DoH provider.
  • Step 1: malware configures DoH client to external DoH provider or uses browser default DoH.
  • Step 2: DNS queries are encapsulated in HTTPS, evading UDP/TCP 53 inspection and RPZ blocking.
  • Step 3: attacker receives queries from endpoint via their authoritative domain on DoH channel.
  • Step 4: impact: threats bypass enterprise DNS controls entirely.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: enforce enterprise DoH endpoint via MDM/Chrome policy and block external DoH provider IPs at firewall.
  • Control 2: TLS SNI inspection and allowlist of approved DoH hostnames with enterprise-controlled certificate pinning.
  • Control 3: detect HTTPS flows to known DoH providers from endpoints that should only talk to internal services.
  • Control 4: monitor for DoH client processes and block unauthorized DoH clients via EDR.
  • Control 5: use enterprise-managed DoH that integrates RPZ and SIEM logging.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Firewall/proxy TLS logs: HTTPS flows to DoH provider IPs or hostnames.
  • Endpoint MDM/Chrome policy reports showing enforced DoH server settings.
  • SIEM correlation: client has DNS activity patterns but resolver logs show no queries for that client.
  • EDR: process-level indicators of DoH client usage or non-browser DoH clients.

Purple-team validation commands

T3 — DNS Cache Poisoning / Forged Responses

STRIDE category: Spoofing
Risk: Critical likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Inject forged DNS responses into a recursive resolver cache so internal clients receive attacker-chosen IPs for legitimate hostnames, enabling credential harvesting, man-in-the-middle, and lateral movement.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: resolver is predictable (transaction ID, source port) or does not perform source-IP validation; attacker can race legitimate authoritative responses.
  • Step 1: attacker floods the resolver with spoofed responses matching a query's TXID and source port before the authoritative answer arrives.
  • Step 2: resolver accepts forged response and caches it for TTL duration.
  • Step 3: internal clients resolve hostname and connect to attacker infrastructure.
  • Step 4: attacker harvests credentials or intercepts traffic.
  • Step 5: impact: widespread internal redirection and stealthy interception.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: use source port and TXID randomization on resolvers and enable DNS cookies where supported.
  • Control 2: enable DNSSEC validation to detect forged records for zones that are signed.
  • Control 3: vendor patches for recursion and response handling to mitigate Kaminsky-class exploits.
  • Control 4: monitor resolver for sudden TTL or record discrepancies and high response spoof attempts.
  • Control 5: rate-limit external responses and require EDNS0 buffering where necessary.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Resolver logs: sudden change in A record values for high-value hostnames, high rate of malformed/fake response attempts.
  • Netflow: internal clients connecting to unexpected external IP addresses after a DNS change.
  • SIEM correlation: spike in failed TLS validations or certificate mismatches when connecting to redirected hosts.

Purple-team validation commands

# Validate resolver rejects forged responses and validates DNSSEC where available
dig @<corp-resolver> +dnssec example.corp

T4 — Registrar Compromise / Domain Hijack

STRIDE category: Elevation of Privilege
Risk: Critical likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Compromise the registrar account or leverage social engineering to transfer domain ownership or change NS records, resulting in full control of public DNS and potential interception of email and web traffic.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: attacker obtains registrar credentials via credential stuffing, phishing, or social engineering with registrar support.
  • Step 1: attacker initiates domain transfer or changes NS records to point to attacker-controlled authoritative servers.
  • Step 2: corporate services (mail, web) point to attacker infrastructure enabling credential capture or service disruption.
  • Step 3: attacker may obtain certificates for domains and impersonate services.
  • Step 4: impact: enterprise loses control of customer-facing services and email, enabling wide-scale phishing.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: enforce FIDO2 + IP restriction and registrar lock on all domain registrar accounts.
  • Control 2: use registrar-level 2-person approval for transfers or NS changes and record via ticketing system.
  • Control 3: enable domain transfer locks (clientTransferProhibited) and monitor registrar change notifications.
  • Control 4: maintain an out-of-band contact with registrar and enable abuse contact verification.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Registrar notification emails: transfer initiation, NS change attempts.
  • CT logs: new certificates issued for corporate domains.
  • Monitoring of authoritative NS delegations via periodic scans.
  • SIEM alerts on unexpected changes to public DNS records.

T5 — AXFR Zone Transfer Abuse

STRIDE category: Information Disclosure
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Use an open AXFR transfer or misconfigured secondary to pull internal zone data and enumerate internal hosts, targets, and zone structure.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: authoritative server allows AXFR to any requesting IP or misconfigured ACL.
  • Step 1: attacker queries AXFR and receives entire zone data.
  • Step 2: attacker enumerates hosts and finds internal naming conventions, SRV records, and administration hosts.
  • Step 3: attacker crafts targeted attacks using discovered hosts.
  • Step 4: impact: reconnaissance significantly lowers attack costs and increases success chances.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: restrict AXFR to explicit secondary server IPs and use TSIG for zone transfers.
  • Control 2: separate internal-only zones from public authoritative servers; ensure split-horizon rules prevent AXFR leaks.
  • Control 3: monitor AXFR requests and alert on unexpected source IPs.
  • Control 4: periodic external scans to confirm no AXFR leakage.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Authoritative server logs: AXFR requests, source IP, and success/failure codes.
  • SIEM alerts on AXFR attempts from non-authorized IPs.
  • Configuration audit: TSIG keys rotation status and AXFR ACLs.

T6 — DNSSEC Key Compromise (ZSK/KSK Stolen)

STRIDE category: Repudiation
Risk: Critical likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Steal DNSSEC signing keys to generate valid-looking signed records, undermining DNS integrity and enabling MITM and large-scale forgery.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: attacker extracts keys from key management system or HSM via privileged access or supply-chain compromise.
  • Step 1: attacker crafts fraudulent records and signs them with the stolen ZSK/KSK.
  • Step 2: validating resolvers accept the forged signed responses as authentic.
  • Step 3: attacker redirects traffic en masse to malicious endpoints.
  • Step 4: impact: trust in DNS responses collapses and incident response must rely on alternate channels.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: store all KSK/ZSK in a dedicated HSM with strict MFA and admin separation.
  • Control 2: enforce hardware-backed key usage, limit key exportability, and log all HSM operations.
  • Control 3: rotate ZSK frequently and have an automated KSK rollover plan.
  • Control 4: monitor for unusual signing events and CT/monitor alerts for unexpected signed records.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • HSM access logs: operator identity and operation timestamps for sign operations.
  • DNSSEC signing logs: sudden spike in signature operations outside maintenance windows.
  • CT feed: signed records that were not in planned deployments.
  • SIEM alert: anomalous signed record TTL or content changes.

T7 — Subdomain Takeover via Dangling CNAME

STRIDE category: Spoofing
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Claim a third-party host referenced by a CNAME in corporate DNS (dangling CNAME) and host phishing/malicious content on the corporate subdomain.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: corporate zone has CNAME to an external service or resource that has been removed; attacker provisions resource at that external host.
  • Step 1: attacker identifies dangling CNAME entries via scan or CT feed.
  • Step 2: attacker provisions hosting at the CNAME target, obtains cert, and serves content under corporate subdomain.
  • Step 3: attacker launches phishing or malware distribution using trusted corporate subdomain.
  • Step 4: impact: high trust phishing and brand damage.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: periodic scan for dangling CNAMEs and CT monitoring for unexpected certificates.
  • Control 2: avoid wildcard or broad CNAMEs to third-party-hosted services where possible.
  • Control 3: require lifecycle checks in provisioning pipelines to remove stale CNAMEs when services decommissioned.
  • Control 4: alert on new TLS certificates issued for corporate subdomains.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • CT monitor alert: new certificate for subdomain not in change record.
  • DNS scan reports: presence of CNAME to deleted external resources.
  • SIEM web-proxy logs: HTTP requests to newly provisioned external host under corporate subdomain.

T8 — Dynamic DNS Abuse (TSIG Key Theft)

STRIDE category: Tampering
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Steal TSIG keys used for authenticated dynamic DNS updates and create or modify DNS records for internal hosts.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: TSIG keys stored in CI/CD, config files, or poorly protected systems.
  • Step 1: attacker exfiltrates TSIG key and crafts signed UPDATE packets to authoritative server.
  • Step 2: attacker adds A records or modifies hostnames to point to attacker infrastructure.
  • Step 3: attacker harvests credentials or intercepts traffic to misdirected hosts.
  • Step 4: impact: internal host resolution changed without need for AXFR or registrar compromise.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: store TSIG keys in secrets manager and rotate frequently; do not bake into images.
  • Control 2: restrict dynamic updates to specific management hosts and require IP allowlisting.
  • Control 3: monitor update logs and alert on updates from unexpected sources.
  • Control 4: use per-zone TSIG keys with minimal scope.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Authoritative server update logs: TSIG identity and source IP for UPDATEs.
  • Secret manager access logs showing TSIG secret retrievals.
  • SIEM alert: dynamic update for protected names outside change window.

T9 — Split-Horizon Misconfiguration Exposing Internal Zones

STRIDE category: Information Disclosure
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Misconfigure split-horizon so internal-only names leak to public authoritative servers, revealing internal topology and naming conventions.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: admin error merges internal zone records into public zone or replication misapplies rules.
  • Step 1: attacker queries public authoritative servers for internal hostnames and receives valid answers.
  • Step 2: attacker enumerates internal hosts and targets services that would otherwise be internal-only.
  • Step 3: impact: reconnaissance accelerates targeted attacks and reduces stealth for attackers.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: strict CI/CD for DNS zone changes and automated tests that detect leakage of internal names.
  • Control 2: split-horizon zone templates that explicitly forbid publishing internal-only labels.
  • Control 3: automated external queries to detect internal names resolving publicly.
  • Control 4: change control and peer review for any zone changes affecting split-horizon.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • External DNS scan reports detecting internal names on public servers.
  • CI/CD pre-deploy tests failing when internal-only names appear in public zone files.
  • SIEM alerts on queries for internal names reaching public authoritative servers.

T10 — DNS Rebinding Leading to Internal Service Access

STRIDE category: Spoofing
Risk: Medium likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Use DNS rebinding to cause a browser to treat an attacker-controlled endpoint as a trusted internal host, enabling access to internal APIs and services via the victim's browser.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: victim visits attacker-controlled web page and DNS resolves attacker domain to the victim's internal resource via rapid DNS changes and short TTLs.
  • Step 1: attacker uses JavaScript in browser to call internal IPs or hostname under the attacker's domain, bypassing same-origin restrictions.
  • Step 2: attacker issues commands to internal services accessible to browser-origin requests.
  • Step 3: attacker reads responses if CORS and other protections are weak.
  • Step 4: impact: exfiltrate data from internal services or execute privileged API actions.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: enforce CORS, CSRF protections, and API authentication for all internal services.
  • Control 2: block cross-origin access via browser hardening and enterprise policies; restrict external web content on sensitive endpoints.
  • Control 3: use DNS pinning defenses and require host header validation on internal APIs.
  • Control 4: detect and block rapid DNS TTL flips in SIEM DNS logs.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Web proxy logs: external page loading internal hostnames and subsequent anomalous API calls.
  • Application logs: unexpected origins or missing authentication headers.
  • SIEM DNS logs: frequent qname changes for same domain from single client.

T11 — BGP Hijack Affecting Authoritative Provider

STRIDE category: Spoofing
Risk: Medium likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Hijack or reroute IP prefixes for the authoritative DNS provider or corporate authoritative nameservers via BGP route poisoning, allowing interception or manipulation of DNS responses at scale.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: attacker manipulates BGP announcements (malicious AS path injection or misconfiguration) to cause traffic to route through attacker-controlled AS.
  • Step 1: DNS queries for corporate domains are directed to attacker infrastructure or probed en route.
  • Step 2: attacker can respond to queries or perform MitM on authoritative traffic.
  • Step 3: attacker issues forged responses or filters legitimate queries.
  • Step 4: impact: large-scale domain-level hijack with global impact.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: announce authoritative nameserver IPs via multiple diverse providers and use Anycast to reduce single-path hijack risk.
  • Control 2: monitor BGP RPKI and route origin validation to detect anomalous announcements and rapidly notify providers.
  • Control 3: have DR plan with failover authoritative providers and registrar contacts ready.
  • Control 4: monitor global DNS resolution from multiple vantage points for anomalies.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • BGP monitoring feeds (RouteViews, RIPE RIS): detection of unexpected origin AS for authoritative IPs.
  • Global DNS checks (DNSMON): inconsistent answers from different regions.
  • ISP abuse contact logs and rapid mitigation tickets.

T12 — DNS Reflection/Amplification for DDoS

STRIDE category: Denial of Service
Risk: Medium likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Abuse open recursive resolvers under corporate control as amplifiers for DDoS against third-party targets, or have external actors use corp resolvers for amplification.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: corporate resolvers respond to spoofed source IPs for certain qtypes (ANY historically) and have high amplification ratios.
  • Step 1: attacker crafts spoofed queries with victim IP as source to corporate resolver.
  • Step 2: resolver responds with larger payload to victim, amplifying traffic volume.
  • Step 3: victim experiences DDoS sourced from corporate resolver IPs, leading to potential blacklisting.
  • Step 4: impact: corporate resolvers used as DDoS vector or collateral damage to reputation and bandwidth.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Control 1: disable recursion for queries from internet-facing clients; limit recursion to internal networks.
  • Control 2: implement response rate limiting (RRL) on authoritative resolvers to limit amplification.
  • Control 3: ensure EDNS0 and qtype handling avoids large amplification responses to unauthenticated sources.
  • Control 4: monitor for high outgoing UDP 53 traffic and unusual response rates.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • Netflow: high-volume UDP 53 responses from resolver IPs with many unique destination IPs.
  • DNS server metrics: spikes in qps with low source variety indicating spoofed targets.
  • SIEM alerts: detected reflection patterns and external complaints.
