Scenario 20 — Insider Threat Detection Program

Scenario: A formal insider threat detection program is launched using UEBA, DLP, and HR data correlation for 15,000 employees. Legal and HR have co-ownership.

Candidate questions:

  • How do you model a malicious insider with legitimate elevated access?
  • What behavioral signals differentiate a negligent insider from a malicious one?
  • What controls prevent the insider threat program itself from being abused for surveillance?
  • How do you handle legal and privacy constraints in different jurisdictions (EU GDPR)?
  • What residual risk remains for a privileged user who knows how the detection works?

3.4 Highest-risk boundary crossing

The highest-risk boundary crossing is the HR-to-UEBA data integration. HR data contains some of the most sensitive personal information about employees: disciplinary history, mental health leave, performance improvement plans, financial difficulties, and personal relationships. When this data feeds the UEBA risk scoring engine, it can create risk score elevations based on protected characteristics or medical conditions — which may constitute discriminatory surveillance under employment law in many jurisdictions. The governance of this integration must ensure that HR signals used for risk scoring are strictly job-function-related (active notice period, privilege level, data access history) and that protected characteristics never influence risk scoring. A secondary risk is that the investigator team, which has access to the combined HR+behavioral data, could misuse it for personal purposes or discriminatory decisions.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

At Google scale (15,000–100,000+ employees), the UEBA baseline computation cannot be manual — it must be automated ML pipelines that continuously update per-user, per-peer-group behavioral baselines without human intervention. Peer group definitions are automated from the HRMS org structure and updated daily. Alert triage at scale requires a tiered automation system: UEBA alerts below risk score 60 are auto-dismissed with a log entry; alerts 60–80 are auto-enriched with context (HR signals, recent access changes) and queued for Tier-1 review; alerts >80 are escalated immediately to the investigation team. DLP rules and UEBA policies are managed as code with automated compliance testing to ensure changes don't introduce detection gaps or legal constraint violations.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A recruited nation-state insider at a company with 15,000 employees can access information whose value to the adversary dwarfs any external intrusion. A single insider with access to M&A data can leak information enabling insider trading affecting billions of dollars in market value. A developer with access to core product IP can transfer the equivalent of years of R&D investment in a single file transfer. At Google scale (1B users, all critical infrastructure), a recruited insider in the infrastructure team can introduce vulnerabilities affecting every customer. The insider threat detection program must be calibrated to the value of the assets at risk, not just to the detection rate — a false negative for a nation-state recruited insider in the security team is categorically more damaging than a false positive.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

At 15,000 employees, UEBA generates hundreds of alerts daily. A human team cannot triage all of them — most are false positives. The Google-scale solution is ML-based alert prioritization that learns from investigator decisions: when investigators mark an alert as false positive or true positive, the model updates and improves future precision. Only alerts with >70% model confidence of being true positives reach human reviewers. DLP rule exceptions require automated approval workflows (manager + HR + security) rather than email chains. Quarterly access recertification is automated via Sailpoint/Saviynt with automatic escalation for overdue reviews — no manual reminders.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual behavioral baseline management — at 15,000 employees across hundreds of roles, manually defining "normal behavior" per role is impossible; requires ML-based per-user baselines. Manual departure monitoring — manually tracking employees in notice period fails with hundreds of departures monthly; requires automated HR event integration that triggers monitoring tier elevation without human intervention. Legal review per alert — if every alert requires legal review, the program is paralyzed; requires pre-approved investigation protocols per jurisdiction that legal has already reviewed and approved, so investigators can follow protocol without per-alert legal consultation. Per-employee DLP policy tuning — different roles legitimately handle different data types; a blanket DLP policy generates massive false positives for legitimate power users; requires role-based DLP policies auto-assigned from HRMS role data.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

Use this to open your answer before going deep on any section.

"Insider threat detection is one of the most technically and legally complex security programs to run correctly. The core challenge is that insiders use legitimate access — the detection can't rely on 'this person is doing something unauthorized' because by definition they are authorized. You have to detect behavior that is anomalous relative to what the person normally does and what their peers do.

My detection framework has three signals: behavioral anomaly (UEBA with peer comparison), event pattern (specific high-risk events like email forwarding to personal domains or bulk download during notice period), and contextual enrichment from HR (is this person departing, under PIP, accessing data they've never touched before?). The combination of all three drives meaningful risk scoring.

The differentiator between negligent and malicious is intent-proxying signals: time of day, data sensitivity, destination, and pattern. A negligent insider emails a file to themselves for convenience — once, to their own email, in business hours. A malicious insider sets up a forwarding rule for all email, transfers bulk data at 2AM, and stages it in personal cloud storage over multiple weeks.

The hardest design challenge is proportionality and legal compliance. EU employees under GDPR have much stronger protections against behavioral monitoring — my program must be jurisdiction-aware, with tiered monitoring depth based on applicable law. The program itself must also have a governance board with Legal and HR co-ownership to prevent misuse for discrimination or personal surveillance.

The residual risk I accept: a sophisticated insider who understands the detection system and deliberately evades UEBA via gradual baseline normalization. My defense there is periodic data access audits that look at cumulative patterns rather than real-time anomalies — harder to manipulate."

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: How do you model a malicious insider with legitimate elevated access?

The threat model for a privileged malicious insider must account for their ability to act within authorized parameters while pursuing unauthorized goals. A DBA exfiltrating customer data via authorized SELECT queries at volumes slightly above their normal pattern — each individual query is legitimate, but the cumulative effect is a data breach. My detection approach: peer group normalization (comparing the DBA's query volume to other DBAs with the same access level), sensitivity-weighted access scoring (accessing a table containing 10M customer records scores higher than accessing a 100-row config table, even if both are authorized), and temporal correlation (access behavior changes in the two weeks before/after job change announcements). The access review control adds another layer: quarterly recertification requires managers to actively re-approve sensitive database access, creating regular checkpoints.

Follow-up 2: What behavioral signals differentiate a negligent insider from a malicious one?

Negligent insiders exhibit convenience-driven behavior: sending work files to personal email for home access (once, small volume, regular working hours), using unsanctioned tools because sanctioned tools are too slow (consistent patterns, same user behavior), or misconfiguring sharing settings due to ignorance. Malicious insiders exhibit intentional obfuscation patterns: exfiltrating at unusual hours (2AM, weekends), staging data in multiple locations before bulk transfer, using encrypted channels or encoding techniques to bypass DLP, gradually increasing exfiltration volume to normalize the baseline, and accessing data outside their job function scope. The clearest differentiating signal is destination: a negligent insider sends data to their own personal email; a malicious insider sends it to a new account, encrypts it, or stages it via a personal cloud service they've never used before.

Follow-up 3: What controls prevent the insider threat program itself from being abused for surveillance?

The program governance architecture is the primary control: (1) strict role-based access to the case management system — only cleared investigators with need-to-know can view specific cases; (2) dual-party investigation requirement — no single investigator can open and manage a case alone; requires a second investigator or legal co-sign for escalation; (3) immutable audit log of all access to the case management system — every investigator action is logged and reviewed quarterly by the oversight board; (4) program oversight board with Legal, HR, and CISO representation that reviews program activity quarterly for proportionality; (5) explicit prohibition in program policy on using the program for disciplinary investigations unrelated to security threats; (6) regular third-party audits of the program's use of employee data. The program must also have a defined escalation path for employees to raise concerns about potential abuse without retaliation.

Follow-up 4: How do you handle legal and privacy constraints in different jurisdictions (EU GDPR)?

My approach is jurisdiction-tiered monitoring. For EU employees: I cannot perform broad behavioral monitoring without specific legal basis — consent is not appropriate for employment monitoring, so I rely on legitimate interest with a documented DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment), limiting monitoring scope to: access logs for systems containing personal data (Article 32 security obligation), DLP events that trigger based on data classification labels (not behavioral profiling), and HR-triggered elevated monitoring for specific risk indicators with time limits. For US employees: broader monitoring is permissible with employee notice (handbook/acknowledgment). For all employees: I must provide transparency about monitoring (employee notice), proportionality (monitor only what is necessary for the stated purpose), and data minimization (don't collect behavioral data that isn't needed for detection). GDPR Article 88 allows EU member states to regulate employee monitoring differently — Germany's Betriebsverfassungsgesetz (Works Council Act) requires works council approval for monitoring systems, which must be negotiated before deployment.

Follow-up 5: If you were the attacker (insider), what is the cheapest path through your control set?

As a sophisticated insider who knows the UEBA system: I'd use behavioral normalization — I'd gradually increase my baseline data access over 3–4 months before the actual exfiltration, keeping each week's increase below the anomaly threshold. I'd use approved tools (SharePoint, OneDrive with corporate account) to stage data with plausible business justification, then move it to personal cloud storage in small chunks over time. I'd avoid any action that triggers high-specificity rules: no email forwarding, no USB, no personal Dropbox. I'd work during business hours, from my regular endpoint, on my regular day. The detection gap I'm exploiting is that UEBA baselines adapt — I'm teaching the system that my behavior is normal. My defense against this: cumulative access audits that look at total data accessed over 6 months (not just anomaly deltas), and periodic manual reviews of highest-privilege accounts by the investigation team regardless of UEBA score.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Treating insider threat detection as purely a technical problem. The program requires Legal and HR co-ownership, a governance board, DPIAs for EU employees, employee notice, and proportionality analysis. Candidates who only discuss UEBA and DLP without the legal and ethical framework will fail in a real organization.
  • Not differentiating monitoring depth by jurisdiction. A single global monitoring policy violates EU GDPR and employment laws in Germany, France, and other EU jurisdictions. The program must be jurisdiction-aware.
  • Assuming UEBA alone is sufficient. UEBA is tunable and gameable by a sophisticated insider who understands behavioral baselines. Physical exfiltration (printing, photography), slow-burn campaigns, and legitimate access patterns all reduce UEBA's effectiveness. Defense in depth requires physical DLP controls, access audits, and peer reviews.
  • Not addressing the insider threat program itself as an attack surface. The program has access to aggregated behavioral data on all 15,000 employees — this is extraordinarily sensitive. Access controls, audit logging, and dual-party investigation requirements for the program itself are essential.
  • Ignoring the problem of slow exfiltration below detection thresholds. Most UEBA systems are tuned to detect spikes, not slow steady exfiltration. A threat actor with patience can exfiltrate massive amounts of data in small chunks over months, staying below all statistical thresholds.
  • Not integrating HR departure events. The highest-risk period for insider exfiltration is the notice period — once employees know they're leaving (or are about to be terminated), their behavior changes dramatically. HR system integration for departure signals is one of the highest-ROI controls.
  • Using HR data (disciplinary, mental health, financial stress) to drive UEBA scores without legal review. Using protected health information or financial status as risk scoring inputs may constitute discrimination under employment law in many jurisdictions, even if the intent is security.
