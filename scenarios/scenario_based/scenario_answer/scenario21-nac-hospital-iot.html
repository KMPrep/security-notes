Scenario 21 — Network Access Control (NAC) for Guest and IoT Devices

Scenario: A hospital campus deploys 802.1X NAC for wired and wireless access. The network hosts medical devices (infusion pumps, imaging systems), guest Wi-Fi, and staff workstations.

Candidate questions:

  • What attack surfaces exist when medical IoT and guest devices share physical infrastructure?
  • How do you model a compromised guest device pivoting to a medical device VLAN?
  • What controls compensate for medical devices that cannot support 802.1X?
  • How do you monitor for VLAN hopping or MAC spoofing attacks?
  • What residual risk do you document for life-critical devices that cannot be patched?

3.4 Highest-risk boundary crossing

The highest-risk boundary crossing in this architecture is between the Guest VLAN and the Clinical IoT VLAN. These two segments share the same physical switching infrastructure (wiring closets, patch panels, access switches) while hosting devices at opposite ends of the sensitivity spectrum — an unauthenticated visitor's smartphone on one side, and life-critical infusion pumps on the other. VLAN separation relies entirely on correct switch port configuration; a single misconfigured trunk port or a DTP (Dynamic Trunking Protocol) negotiation attack can bridge these two segments. The stakes are uniquely high: this is not a data breach risk — it is a direct patient safety risk. Compromising an infusion pump's network interface could allow manipulation of dosing parameters in a way that could cause immediate patient harm, making this the highest-consequence threat boundary in any healthcare network.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

In a large hospital system (50+ campuses, 100,000+ networked devices), NAC policy management cannot be manual. Every switch port configuration, VLAN assignment, and ACL rule must be managed via infrastructure-as-code (Ansible/Terraform with Cisco IOS/NX-OS modules), committed to Git, and deployed through a change management pipeline. Device profiles in ISE are automatically updated when biomedical engineering adds a new device type to the approved inventory — no manual RADIUS policy editing. MAC allow-lists for clinical devices are auto-populated from the CMDB via API integration, and any new MAC on the clinical VLAN that isn't in the CMDB triggers an automated quarantine and biomedical engineering alert.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A RADIUS server compromise at a 50-campus hospital system doesn't just affect one building — it affects every 802.1X-authenticated device in the entire network. If the RADIUS server is taken offline or manipulated to assign all devices to Guest VLAN, clinical staff lose access to EHR systems simultaneously across all campuses. A ransomware infection on the clinical VLAN that reaches infusion pumps could affect hundreds of patients simultaneously — this is the healthcare equivalent of a mass-casualty incident triggered by a cyber attack. The 2020 Düsseldorf Hospital ransomware attack, which contributed to a patient death, demonstrates this is not theoretical. Blast radius minimization here is not about data loss — it's about patient mortality.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

The human bottleneck in healthcare NAC is biomedical engineering reviewing and approving every new medical device for network admission. With hundreds of new devices purchased annually across a large hospital system, this review cannot be manual. The solution: an automated device classification pipeline where Claroty/Medigate's ML-based device fingerprinting automatically classifies new devices by type, and the security team pre-approves device classes (not individual devices) with associated network policies. A new infusion pump model gets automatically assigned to Clinical IoT VLAN based on device class without per-device approval. Only unknown/unclassifiable devices require human review.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual MAC allow-list management — maintaining MAC allow-lists for 100,000 clinical IoT devices across 50 campuses manually fails immediately; requires CMDB integration with automated NAC policy sync. Per-port VLAN review — checking every switch port's VLAN assignment for 10,000 switches manually is impossible; requires automated configuration compliance scanning (Cisco DNA Center, Ansible audit playbooks) that runs nightly and alerts on deviations. Manual vendor session scheduling — with hundreds of medical device vendors requiring maintenance access, individual session scheduling and approval via email fails; requires self-service PAM portal with pre-approved vendor-to-device mappings and automated scheduling.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

Use this to open your answer before going deep on any section.

"This is a scenario where security failure can directly cause patient harm — the stakes are uniquely high. The core problem is that guest devices, staff workstations, and life-critical infusion pumps share the same physical switching infrastructure. VLAN separation is the primary control, but it's enforced in software on shared hardware, which means it's vulnerable to VLAN hopping attacks.

My top concern is MAC address spoofing to defeat MAB authentication — an attacker who sniffs the MAC of an infusion pump from the guest network can impersonate it and be admitted to the Clinical IoT VLAN. My defense is layered: MAB with multi-signal profiling (DHCP fingerprint + OUI + device behavior), strict ACLs that block even clinical VLAN traffic from reaching the pump's management interface except from the clinical management VLAN, and passive OT/IoMT monitoring via Claroty Guardian that baselines each medical device's normal traffic and alerts on any deviation.

The 802.1X gap is real: most medical devices cannot support certificate-based authentication. I compensate with network isolation making the Clinical IoT VLAN a no-inbound zone — even if a device is admitted, it can only talk to specific allow-listed destinations. A compromised pump can't initiate connections to anything outside its normal DICOM or HL7 endpoints.

The residual risk I document is the unpatched OS on legacy devices — infusion pumps running Windows XP with known CVEs. I cannot patch them without FDA re-clearance. I accept this risk with a documented compensating controls framework and a device replacement timeline, and I report it to the CISO and clinical leadership quarterly."

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: How do you model a compromised guest device pivoting to a medical device VLAN?

The pivot path I model most carefully is: guest device performs passive ARP/mDNS monitoring to collect MAC addresses of clinical devices visible through broadcast leakage, then disconnects from wireless and reconnects on a wired port spoofing the clinical device MAC. If DTP is enabled (auto trunk), the guest device can negotiate a trunk and access all VLANs directly. My preventive controls address both: disable DTP (hardcode all ports as access or trunk), set native VLAN to an unused ID to prevent double-tagging, and enforce port-security with max 1 MAC per port with violation=shutdown. Post-admission, Claroty detects that the traffic from the "infusion pump" doesn't match the expected DICOM/HL7 protocol baseline and fires an immediate alert.

Follow-up 2: What controls compensate for medical devices that cannot support 802.1X?

MAC Authentication Bypass (MAB) is the admission mechanism, but I don't trust MAC alone. I use Cisco ISE's device profiling to require multi-signal match: OUI must match known medical device manufacturers, DHCP fingerprint must match the expected device OS signature, and HTTP user-agent (if device has a web interface) must match the expected device model. Only when 3/3 signals match is the device admitted to Clinical IoT VLAN. Post-admission, Claroty provides continuous behavioral monitoring — if the "infusion pump" starts making DNS queries or TCP connections that don't match its baseline behavior, it's immediately flagged. The network-side compensating control is the default-deny inter-VLAN firewall: even if an attacker is admitted to Clinical IoT VLAN, they can only reach destinations on the firewall allow-list, which is restricted to the EHR integration endpoints only.

Follow-up 3: How do you monitor for VLAN hopping or MAC spoofing attacks?

Three detection layers: (1) Cisco ISE endpoint analytics — when a MAC address that was previously assigned to Clinical IoT VLAN appears on a guest network port, or when the same IP appears with a different MAC within minutes, ISE fires a profiling anomaly alert; (2) switch port-security syslog — any port-security violation (new MAC on a locked port) generates a syslog event that feeds the SIEM; (3) Claroty passive monitoring — if a device that Claroty knows as an infusion pump starts generating traffic that doesn't match its behavioral baseline (new ports, new protocols, TCP scan patterns), Claroty alerts within seconds. For DTP-based VLAN hopping, I detect via switch syslog events showing DTP trunk negotiation on access ports — these should never occur if DTP is correctly disabled, making any such event a high-fidelity alert.

Follow-up 4: You mentioned Claroty for IoMT monitoring. What happens if that control fails — what is your next layer?

If Claroty is unavailable (outage, sensor failure), the next layer is the inter-VLAN firewall log — all traffic between VLANs passes through the Palo Alto firewall, and any guest-to-clinical traffic generates a firewall deny log that feeds the SIEM. The SIEM rule fires on any source IP in the guest VLAN range attempting to reach any IP in the clinical VLAN range. The layer after that is the endpoint detection on clinical workstations (CrowdStrike): if the clinical workstation VLAN is reached via a lateral movement path, EDR detects the scanning behavior. Finally, the switch port-security violation log provides a last-resort signal if an attacker physically recables a device to a wrong port. The defense is not dependent on any single monitoring tool.

Follow-up 5: If you were the attacker, what is the cheapest path through your control set?

The cheapest path is physical: walk to an unmonitored networking closet (visiting the hospital as a "patient family member"), identify the patch panel for the Clinical IoT VLAN based on cable labels or panel markings, and physically patch a guest VLAN port to a clinical VLAN port. With a laptop plugged into that port, I'm directly on the Clinical IoT VLAN with no 802.1X challenge (since the port was already authenticated by the medical device that was previously connected). My detection relies on port-security (new MAC = port shutdown) and RADIUS re-authentication, but if the attacker simply disconnects the medical device and connects their laptop, the port may not immediately re-authenticate. The fix: 802.1X re-authentication timer set to 60 seconds, port-security enabled, and CCTV in all wiring closets with motion alerts for after-hours access.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Treating VLAN isolation as cryptographic isolation. VLANs are a logical separation on shared physical infrastructure, enforced by switch configuration. They are not equivalent to physical network separation. DTP attacks, double-tagging, and switch misconfiguration can all break VLAN isolation.
  • Relying solely on MAB for medical device admission. A MAC address is trivially spoofable. MAB without multi-signal device profiling and post-admission behavioral monitoring is insufficient for life-critical device protection.
  • Not addressing DTP disablement. The most common VLAN hopping vector in real environments is DTP auto-negotiation on access ports. If a candidate doesn't mention disabling DTP (switchport nonegotiate), they're missing a fundamental network security control.
  • Underestimating the patient safety stakes. Candidates who approach this as a "data breach" scenario miss the point. The primary concern is patient safety — a compromised infusion pump is a weapon that can directly harm or kill patients. This changes the risk calculus fundamentally.
  • Ignoring the FDA regulatory constraint on medical device patching. Patching a medical device OS may invalidate its FDA 510(k) clearance and require re-submission, which takes months to years. Candidates who say "just patch the devices" reveal they don't understand the healthcare regulatory environment.
  • Not specifying a passive monitoring solution for medical devices. Standard active vulnerability scanners can crash legacy medical devices. Candidates who propose running Nessus against infusion pumps reveal insufficient healthcare security experience. Passive monitoring (Claroty, Medigate) is the correct approach.
  • Forgetting about the DICOM protocol security gap. DICOM, the standard protocol for medical imaging, was designed without authentication. A candidate discussing imaging systems without noting this protocol security limitation is missing a significant vulnerability.
  • Not having a physical security layer. VLAN security controls are meaningless if an attacker can physically recable patch panels. Physical security of wiring closets (badge access, CCTV, dual-person requirement for patching) is a required control layer.
