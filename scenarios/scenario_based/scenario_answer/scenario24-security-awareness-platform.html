Scenario 24 — Security Awareness Training Platform

Scenario: A third-party phishing simulation and security awareness platform is granted access to production email infrastructure to send simulated phishing emails.

Candidate questions:

  • What access should the platform have and what is the minimum required privilege?
  • How do you prevent the platform vendor from abusing its access to real user data?
  • What controls prevent simulation emails from being misused for real phishing?
  • How do you measure the effectiveness of training — and what are the measurement risks?
  • What residual risk remains if the vendor's platform is compromised?

3.4 Trust boundary analysis

The critical trust boundary is the authorization given to the simulation platform to send email on behalf of (or through) the enterprise email infrastructure. Unlike most third-party integrations, this one explicitly expands the trusted sender list — the simulation platform's sending IPs or subdomain are added to SPF records, and the platform may receive a DKIM signing credential. An attacker who compromises the simulation vendor inherits this trusted sender authorization and can send emails to all employees that will pass SPF, DKIM, and DMARC checks — exactly the properties that make them indistinguishable from legitimate corporate email to both filters and employees.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

At Google scale (100,000+ employees), phishing simulation campaigns cannot be manually scheduled and managed. Campaign scheduling, target list selection (stratified sample by department, tenure, previous click rate), template rotation, and results analysis are automated. Templates are generated with machine assistance to ensure variety and avoid pattern-learning. The security awareness platform API integration is managed via Terraform (vendor OAuth app configuration, SPF record management), and any changes to vendor permissions are code-reviewed and deployed through CI/CD.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A compromised simulation vendor with authorized sending access to a 100,000-employee organization represents the most effective spearphishing delivery mechanism imaginable: authenticated emails (pass DMARC), sent from a trusted subdomain employees recognize from simulations, at the exact moment employees are psychologically primed to expect them. The blast radius is the entire employee population. The vendor must be treated as a tier-1 critical supplier with the same security assessment requirements as cloud infrastructure vendors.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

The human bottleneck in security awareness programs is the security admin manually scheduling campaigns and reviewing results. At scale: automated risk-based targeting (higher simulation frequency for recently promoted executives, employees who failed last simulation, employees in high-risk roles), automated training assignment triggered by simulation failure (click → immediate targeted training module), and automated management reporting without individual employee-level disclosure.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual SPF/DKIM updates — if vendor IPs change, manual SPF updates across thousands of domains take days; requires automated DNS management with vendor IP change webhook notification. Individual employee tracking — at 100,000 employees, tracking individual click rates in security reports creates legal and HR liability; aggregate by role/department only. Manual vendor credential revocation — if vendor is compromised, revoking SMTP credentials across all sending channels manually takes time; requires automated revocation via API with SLA of <5 minutes.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

"This scenario has a fundamental tension: the simulation platform needs access to your email infrastructure to do its job, but that same access is the thing that makes it dangerous if misused. My first principle is: the vendor should never be authorized to send email that claims to be from your primary domain. Use a dedicated simulation subdomain, and make that subdomain's SPF/DKIM separate from the primary domain's. That way, even if the vendor is fully compromised, the attacker cannot send email that passes DMARC for your primary domain.

Second, the OAuth or API access the vendor needs must be scoped to Mail.Send only — not Mail.Read, not Contacts.Read. An over-permissioned vendor with Mail.ReadAll access can read every employee's inbox, which is a catastrophic privacy and security failure.

Third, I'd put monitoring on the vendor's sending behavior: any email sent via the vendor's relay outside the scheduled campaign window generates an immediate alert. This detects vendor compromise or credential misuse quickly.

The residual risk I document is vendor supply chain compromise. If the platform vendor is breached and the attacker abuses the sending credential to deliver real malicious payloads — which now look like simulations — employees may be more likely to click, not less. This is the highest-consequence failure mode. I mitigate it with rapid revocation capability and contractual breach notification obligations, but I cannot eliminate the risk entirely."

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: What is the minimum access the vendor needs?

Minimum required: (1) ability to send email via SMTP relay — scoped to a dedicated simulation subdomain, not the primary domain; (2) per-campaign target list (employees for the specific campaign), provided by the security admin at campaign creation time, not a standing API pull of the full directory; (3) ability to host a tracking URL and fake login page on their own infrastructure (not on enterprise domains). The vendor should not have: access to read employee email, access to the full employee directory at rest, DKIM signing keys for the primary domain, or standing access to any enterprise system between campaigns.

Follow-up 2: How do you prevent simulation emails from being misused for real phishing?

Three controls: (1) Sending channel restriction — vendor can only send via authorized relay for approved subdomain; no direct sending to @company.com bypassing controls; (2) Campaign scheduling enforcement — SIEM alert on any email from vendor relay outside scheduled campaign window; (3) Behavioral monitoring — if click rates, submission rates, or employee reports are anomalously different from expected for a given template type, investigate immediately. An attacker using the vendor's sending credentials would likely send in volume outside campaign windows — that's the detection signal.

Follow-up 3: How do you measure effectiveness without creating HR liability?

Measurement principle: report security posture by cohort (department, tenure, role), never by individual in any report shared outside the security team. Within the security team, individual tracking is legitimate for targeted intervention (high-risk users get extra training), but HR and management see only aggregate metrics. I also recommend against submitting individual click data to HR for disciplinary purposes — this creates perverse incentives (employees never click anything, even legitimate links) and may create legal liability in some jurisdictions.

Follow-up 4: What residual risk remains if the vendor's platform is compromised?

The worst case: attacker has vendor's SMTP relay credential and can send authenticated simulation-looking emails to the entire employee population at any time. The emails pass SPF and DKIM for the simulation subdomain, which employees have been conditioned to associate with security training. The attack would likely be more effective than a cold phishing campaign precisely because of the conditioning. My residual risk documentation: probability = low (depends on vendor security posture); impact = critical (every employee a target via trusted channel). Compensating controls: monitoring for off-schedule sends (primary detection); employee training that real phishing can look like simulations too; out-of-band verification for any email requesting credentials or urgent action. Contractual: vendor must notify within 1 hour of detected compromise.

Follow-up 5: Walk me through your design for a simulation that tests executive impersonation.

Executive impersonation is one of the highest-risk templates because it exploits authority bias. My design: (1) Use an internal executive's name as the sender display name, but from the simulation subdomain (not the real exec's address); (2) Template: urgent wire transfer request or credential validation, targeting finance and executive assistants; (3) Landing page: enterprise-branded page asking for Okta credentials (no actual credential collection — system captures that credentials were entered, not the credentials themselves); (4) Controls: legal and HR pre-approval for use of executives' names; simulated credential page marked as simulation immediately on submission; results reported to CISO only (individual employee results never shared with the executive whose name was used). (5) Timing: never schedule during M&A, earnings, or other high-stress periods where a real wire transfer might actually be in flight.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Authorizing the vendor to send from the primary corporate domain. This gives the vendor (and any attacker who compromises the vendor) the ability to send email that is indistinguishable from legitimate corporate email. Always use a dedicated subdomain.
  • Over-provisioning vendor API access. Mail.Send does not require Mail.Read. Candidates who accept broad API permissions without scoping reveal insufficient third-party risk management awareness.
  • Using individual click data in HR performance management. This creates legal liability, destroys psychological safety, and incentivizes behaviors that undermine security (never clicking anything, using VPNs to avoid tracking, creating safe-phrase word lists).
  • Not monitoring for off-schedule vendor sending. The core security control for vendor abuse is detecting email sent outside campaign windows — a trivially implementable SIEM rule that many organizations skip.
  • Treating security awareness training as a compliance checkbox. Measuring click rates and declaring the program effective conflates compliance (people completed the training) with behavior change (people recognize phishing in the wild).
  • Not having a vendor breach response plan. If the simulation vendor is compromised, the response is time-critical: revoke SMTP credentials, remove vendor IPs from SPF, notify employees. These steps should be documented and tested annually.
  • Confusing simulation effectiveness with security posture. A low click rate on simulations may reflect template fatigue or employees forwarding simulation emails to colleagues, not genuine phishing resistance.
