Scenario 25 — Certificate Transparency Monitoring Program

Scenario: Your security team wants to detect certificate mis-issuance for all corporate domains using CT log monitoring. The domain portfolio has 10,000+ registered domains.

Candidate questions:

  • What threats does CT log monitoring help detect?
  • How do you prioritize alerting across 10,000 domains given alert volume?
  • What controls are triggered when a rogue certificate is detected?
  • How do you model a threat actor who uses a certificate from a trusted but non-monitored CA?
  • What residual risk remains for internal-only certificates not published to CT logs?

3.4 Trust boundary analysis

The CA/Browser Forum's CT requirement creates a publicly observable transparency layer for all publicly trusted TLS certificates. This transparency is the primary control: any certificate for any domain appears in CT logs within hours of issuance. The trust boundary being protected is the CA-to-certificate relationship — CT monitoring detects when a CA issues a certificate for a domain it should not have issued for, regardless of whether the certificate was obtained via DV, OV, or EV validation. The residual gap is in non-CT-logged certificates: private enterprise CAs, code-signing CAs, and any CA that issues HTTPS certificates without CT compliance (increasingly rare for publicly trusted CAs, but still exists for internal CAs and some enterprise-sold products).

6.3 Handling the 10,000-domain scale

At 10,000 domains, raw CT log monitoring generates significant alert volume. Scale management strategy:

  1. Domain priority tiering (see 6.1) — only Tier 1 requires immediate human review; lower tiers are batch-processed
  2. Approved certificate inventory — every legitimate certificate is registered in a certificate management system (Venafi, CertManager, or custom); any CT alert for a domain where the cert SHA matches the inventory is auto-suppressed
  3. Fuzzy domain watchlist — dnstwist generates all known typosquats of Tier 1 domains; those typosquats are added to the watchlist for CT monitoring even if the enterprise doesn't own those domains
  4. False positive suppression — new certificate for a domain with a valid JIRA ticket for certificate renewal (same domain, same owner, same CA) is auto-suppressed; anomalies (different CA, different SANs, no ticket) require human review

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

Google's Certificate Transparency project was invented at Google precisely because of this problem at scale. At Google scale (millions of domains across all products), manual CT monitoring is impossible. The entire certificate lifecycle is automated: certificates are issued via ACME protocol with Let's Encrypt or internal ACME CA, tracked in a central certificate inventory, monitored via automated CT log polling, and auto-rotated on a 90-day schedule. Any certificate not in the central inventory that appears in CT logs for Google domains triggers automated investigation.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A CA compromise event (like the 2011 DigiNotar breach) at Google scale means that every HTTPS certificate issued by that CA for any Google domain is potentially compromised — thousands of certificates across hundreds of products. Emergency response requires identifying every affected certificate, rotating them simultaneously, and coordinating with browser vendors to distrust the compromised CA. This is a fleet-level incident requiring a pre-planned playbook and automation for mass certificate rotation.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

The human bottleneck is manual CA contact for revocation. At scale, this is replaced by: automated CA abuse reporting via ACME revocation API (rfc8555); automated revocation requests via CAA iodef; and a pre-negotiated emergency response SLA with all approved CAs in the CAA records. Browser vendors (Chrome, Firefox) also have direct channels for emergency CA distrust decisions — maintaining those relationships is a prerequisite.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual certificate inventory management — at 10,000 domains with hundreds of certificates each, manual inventory tracking is impossible; requires automated certificate discovery (network scanning, CT log reconciliation) and a certificate lifecycle management platform. Manual typosquat monitoring — with 10,000 owned domains, manual typosquat enumeration is not feasible; requires automated dnstwist scanning with auto-enrollment of discovered typosquats into the CT monitoring watchlist. Manual emergency cert rotation — if a CA is compromised and 5,000 certificates need rotation, manual rotation takes weeks; requires automated ACME-based rotation with parallel execution.

CT monitoring detects the rogue cert within an hour of issuance, but the MITM could operate for that window. LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: What threats does CT log monitoring help detect?

CT monitoring detects: (1) certificate mis-issuance — a CA issues a certificate for a domain it should not have; (2) unauthorized DV issuance — an attacker passes domain validation for a corporate subdomain they've taken over; (3) typosquat campaigns — attacker obtains a certificate for a visually similar domain and builds a phishing site; (4) compromised CA events — a large-scale mis-issuance event (DigiNotar-style) where many certs are issued fraudulently; (5) internal PKI leakage — enterprise CA starts issuing public-facing certs that appear in CT logs unexpectedly. It does not detect: use of private CA certs not submitted to CT; valid certificates that are later compromised without re-issuance; or code-signing certificate fraud (different trust store).

Follow-up 2: How do you prioritize alerting for 10,000 domains?

Four tiers: Tier 1 (authentication, payment, API — ~20 domains) gets immediate paging with a 1-hour SLA. These have real-time CT log stream monitoring. Tier 2 (customer-facing production, ~200 domains) gets same-day alerting via batch processing. Tier 3 (brand protection, subsidiaries, ~1,000 domains) gets weekly review. Tier 4 (defensive registrations, typosquat blockers) gets monthly scan. The key scaling mechanism is the approved certificate inventory: any cert in inventory is auto-suppressed. An alert on Tier 1 with no matching inventory entry is the signal that matters — everything else is operational noise.

Follow-up 3: What controls are triggered when a rogue certificate is detected?

Immediate steps in order: (1) Verify — is this an authorized certificate we didn't know about? Check with PKI team and DevOps team owning the domain; (2) Assess — is the rogue cert currently in use? Check production systems for deployed cert; check for evidence of DNS manipulation suggesting active MITM; (3) Revoke — contact the issuing CA via abuse contact and ACME revocation API to request revocation; document the issuance process failure; (4) Hunt — if there's evidence of MITM, hunt for credential theft, investigate all traffic that may have traversed the rogue cert endpoint; (5) Prevent recurrence — add CAA record if missing; review how the attacker passed DV validation; rotate any credentials that may have been exposed.

Follow-up 4: How do you model a threat actor who uses a certificate from a trusted but non-monitored CA?

This is the CT monitoring blind spot. My model: the attacker identifies CAs in the browser trust store that are not included in my monitoring program (e.g., a regional CA with its own CT log that I haven't subscribed to). They obtain a certificate for my domain from that CA, submitted only to the CA's own CT log, which my monitoring misses. My mitigation is threefold: (1) CAA records limit which CAs can issue certs for my domains — even if I'm not monitoring a CA's CT log, that CA should refuse issuance if it's not in the CAA record; (2) I subscribe to all major CT log feeds (Google, Cloudflare, DigiCert) and use crt.sh as a catchall aggregator; (3) I review CT log coverage annually against the current CA/B Forum trusted CA list and add new logs as they become relevant.

Follow-up 5: What residual risk remains for internal-only certificates not in CT logs?

This is a significant gap. Enterprise CAs issue certificates for internal services, often including certificates for hostnames that are also resolvable externally (e.g., api-internal.company.com). If a CA cert is in the trust store of corporate laptops (pushed via Group Policy or MDM), it can be used to issue certificates that pass browser validation for corporate users without appearing in CT logs. A MITM attack using an enterprise CA cert within the corporate network is completely invisible to CT monitoring. My residual risk documentation: I require all enterprise CA certificates to be managed via a certificate lifecycle management platform with quarterly audits of all issued certificates. Any enterprise CA cert that issues a certificate for a domain that is also in the CT-monitored Tier 1 domain list requires review. I also consider marking internal CAs as not-externally-trusted (not installed in user browsers for external HTTPS validation) to limit their MITM scope.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Not deploying CAA records. CAA is a preventive control that CT monitoring alone cannot replace. Many candidates describe CT monitoring as the primary control without mentioning CAA, which prevents unauthorized issuance before it happens.
  • Treating CT monitoring as real-time prevention. CT monitoring is detection, not prevention. A rogue certificate is valid from the moment it's issued — CT monitoring detects it within hours, but the MITM window is real.
  • Missing the typosquat certificate threat. CT monitoring for owned domains is necessary but not sufficient. Typosquat domains with valid certificates are one of the most common phishing enablement vectors, and they require a separate watchlist.
  • Not having an approved certificate inventory. Without knowing which certificates should legitimately exist, CT alerts produce enormous false positive volumes. The inventory is what makes the monitoring actionable.
  • Overlooking internal CA certificates. Candidates who say "CT logs cover everything" reveal they don't understand that private CAs are not CT-required and create a significant monitoring gap.
  • Not knowing the CT log aggregators. Candidates should know about crt.sh, censys.io, and Google's CT API as practical tools for querying CT logs at scale.
  • Missing the browser trust evolution. Chrome and Firefox have moved to requiring CT compliance for all new publicly trusted TLS certificates — but this doesn't cover older certificates, private CAs, or code-signing. Candidates should know what CT requirements do and don't cover.
