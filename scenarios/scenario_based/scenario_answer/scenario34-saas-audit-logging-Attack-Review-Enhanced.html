# Scenario 34 — SaaS Audit Logging for Enterprise Customers — Threat Exploitation and Blue-Team Control Review

Source file reviewed: scenario34-saas-audit-logging.md
Generated artifact: scenario34-saas-audit-logging-Attack-Review-Enhanced.md
Reviewer role: Cybersecurity Architect / Engineer Lead — 15+ years purple-team experience
Scenario: Enterprise customers require immutable, tamper-evident tenant-scoped audit logs retained for 7 years, exportable with strong tenant scoping. Key risks: insider tampering, integrity bypass, export leakage, and long-retention key compromise.

1. Review Objective

Translate the scenario into attacker-centric exploitation chains and a blue-team control review: for each threat provide STRIDE, attacker objectives and steps, specific preventive/detective/corrective controls (naming logs, rules, or product features), safe purple-team validation commands, and mermaid attack trees with control breakpoints.

2. Highest-Risk Design Decision

The single most consequential decision is allowing privileged operators or a single control plane to both change retention/export policies and have direct write/delete capability on the immutable store. That combination creates a single point where tampering can occur before any external anchor detects it. Preferred safe choice: strict separation of duties (governance-only for retention/export), dual authorization for retention edits, and external periodic anchoring of hashes to an independent verifier.

3. Attack-Tree Legend

  • Red nodes (:::attack): attacker actions or attacker-controlled state.
  • Blue nodes (:::control): defensive control decision points.
  • Green nodes (:::blocked): attack stopped or contained.
  • Yellow nodes (:::impact): business or legal impact.

6. Threat-by-Threat Technical Review

T1 — Privileged deletion of log partitions

STRIDE category: Tampering
Risk: Critical likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Remove incriminating audit records from tenant partitions to cover activity.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: admin console or storage account with delete privileges.
  • Initial action: access storage admin API and request delete on specific partition objects.
  • Core exploitation: delete or mark objects as removed before anchor runs.
  • Escalation: repeat across multiple partitions to widen cover.
  • Impact realization: loss of forensic evidence and failed compliance.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: WORM/append-only object lock for audit buckets (e.g., object lock/immutability) with legal hold enforcement.
  • Preventive: Separate roles for retention management (governance-only) vs storage operators; no single role can both change retention and delete objects.
  • Detective: Storage audit logs (WORMStorageAudit) integrated into SIEM with ArchiveDeleteAnomaly rule.
  • Corrective: Automated freeze on delete actions that affect >N objects and an automated snapshot to secondary immutable archive.
  • Preventive: Dual-authorization (two-person control) and ticket binding for any retention or deletion actions.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • WORMStorageAudit entries with actor, action, object_path, retention_state.
  • SIEM rule output ArchiveDeleteAnomaly listing actor and object_count.
  • Secondary immutable archive replication logs showing last successful replication timestamp.
  • Object store access logs showing attempted deletes and KMS decrypt attempts.

Purple-team validation commands

# Attempt to delete a partition in staging and ensure object lock prevents removal
aws s3 rm s3://audit-tenant-123/partition-202401 --recursive

# Detect large-scale delete attempts on audit storage
WORMStorageAudit | where action == "Delete" and object_count > 10 | project actor, object_count, timestamp

T2 — Retention policy downgrade

STRIDE category: Tampering
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Reduce retention to eliminate long-term evidence, often prior to deletion.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: access to governance console or CI/CD that applies retention policies.
  • Initial action: push a retention change or approve a downgrade request.
  • Core exploitation: new policy shortens retention enabling subsequent deletions or purges.
  • Escalation: schedule large purges during low-observability windows.
  • Impact realization: failure to meet contractual retention obligations.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Policy-as-code with signed baselines and CI gating; retention changes must originate from repo and pass policy lint.
  • Preventive: Dual-approval workflow and ticket binding for any retention reduction that impacts SLA (e.g., less than 7 years).
  • Detective: PolicyChangeLog alert on retention field reduction with correlation to actor and ticket ID.
  • Corrective: Automated revert to last signed baseline and emergency notification to compliance owners.
  • Preventive: Immutable deployment pipeline for retention policy changes (no console edits without CI artifact).

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • PolicyChangeLog entries with actor, change, ticket_id, and signed_baseline_id.
  • CI/CD pipeline audit showing deployment artifact and commit hash for retention changes.
  • SIEM RetentionPolicyDowngrade alerts with actor and affected buckets.
  • Governance ticketing linkage showing approvals and justification.

T3 — Integrity chain service bypass

STRIDE category: Repudiation
Risk: Critical likelihood, Critical impact
Attacker objective: Cause events to be written without proper hash chaining or anchor publication, enabling undetectable tamper.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: ability to push events downstream of hash chain step or to alter chain metadata before anchoring.
  • Initial action: inject events directly into storage bypassing HashChainService or mark chain entries as anchored.
  • Core exploitation: modified storage contains events with forged chain metadata.
  • Escalation: remove or alter anchor runs or attack the anchor store.
  • Impact realization: tampered history without detection by internal verifier.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Enforce ingest path that computes hash and sequence server-side before any write; disallow any bypass paths by network policy.
  • Preventive: Publish periodic external anchors (e.g., sign and publish root hashes to independent verifier or public ledger) to make post-hoc change detectable.
  • Detective: IntegrityVerifierLogs that run continuous verification and alert on any hash mismatch or missing sequence IDs.
  • Corrective: Freeze affected partitions and perform secondary verification using replicated immutable archives.
  • Preventive: Harden HashChainService with dedicated service account and access control; log all hash computations to separate immutable store.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • IntegrityVerifierLogs with fields chain_id, sequence_id, validation_result and mismatch_reason.
  • Anchor publish logs showing signed root hash and publish timestamp.
  • Ingest path metrics showing bypass_attempt counters and source addresses.
  • Secondary archive replication consistency checks.

Purple-team validation commands

# Detect integrity mismatches

T4 — Dropped events due to schema failures

STRIDE category: Repudiation
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Cause ingestion to drop or route events to dead-letter queues to create gaps in tenant history.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: event fields or payload formats that trigger schema validators to reject.
  • Initial action: send malformed events or exploit parser edge cases to cause silent drops.
  • Core exploitation: events sent by legitimate systems are dropped and not counted.
  • Escalation: combine with retention downgrade to remove backups later.
  • Impact realization: undetected missing events impair investigations.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Fail-closed ingest pipeline that routes malformed events to quarantined DLQ and alerts ingest owners.
  • Preventive: Schema evolution gating and consumer contracts with CI regression tests.
  • Detective: IngestMetrics and DLQ monitoring with IngestLossAnomaly alert when success ratio drops.
  • Corrective: Automatic replay capability from buffer store and reconciliation jobs comparing source counts to ingested counts.
  • Preventive: Harden parsers and apply fuzzing tests to ingestion code.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • IngestMetrics with ingest_success_count, ingest_failure_count, and dlq_count per source.
  • DLQ object logs showing quarantined payloads and error reasons.
  • Source system event counters to reconcile expected vs actual counts.
  • SIEM rule IngestLossAnomaly when ingest success ratio < baseline.

Purple-team validation commands

# Send malformed events to staging ingest and ensure DLQ captures them and alert fires

T5 — Cross-tenant export filter bug

STRIDE category: Information Disclosure
Risk: Critical likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Cause an export job to include logs from other tenants, leaking sensitive history.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: export service with complex filter logic and multi-tenant query paths.
  • Initial action: craft export request or exploit filter parsing bug to widen tenant scope.
  • Core exploitation: package includes other tenants' objects and deliver to requester.
  • Escalation: use bulk exports to exfiltrate large sets of tenant data.
  • Impact realization: cross-tenant data breach and major compliance failure.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Enforce server-side tenant scoping with immutable manifest generation that includes tenant_id and signed checksum.
  • Preventive: Export packaging must verify that all included objects are tenant-scoped (policy enforcement in query engine).
  • Detective: ExportAPIAudit logs and CrossTenantExportMismatch SIEM rule when requester_tenant != export_manifest.tenant_id.
  • Corrective: Automatic halt of export and immediate revoke of delivered access; forensic manifest verification tools.
  • Preventive: Deliver exports only via tenant-authenticated short-lived channels and require manifest verification by customer.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • ExportAPIAudit with fields requester, tenant_scope, manifest_id, signed_checksum.
  • Delivery logs and TLS session metadata for export transfer.
  • SIEM alert CrossTenantExportMismatch with manifest vs requester correlation.
  • Customer checksum verification receipts confirming correct manifest.

Purple-team validation commands

T6 — Direct archive bucket read by broad role

STRIDE category: Elevation of Privilege
Risk: High likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Use broad IAM roles to enumerate or read archived logs across tenants.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: an operator or role with wide objectRead permissions on archive buckets.
  • Initial action: list and read objects across tenant prefixes.
  • Core exploitation: download historical logs and extract sensitive info.
  • Escalation: exfiltrate or combine with other leaks to forge evidence.
  • Impact realization: large-scale privacy and evidence exposure.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Enforce least-privilege IAM; archive read keys must be tenant-scoped and gated by export workflow.
  • Preventive: Encrypt archive objects with tenant-scoped keys (KMS with tenant context) and require KMS decrypt audit for read.
  • Detective: ObjectStoreAccess and KMSAccessLogs correlated with UnauthorizedKeyDecrypt rule.
  • Corrective: Revoke access keys and require multi-factor attestation for archive reads above threshold.
  • Preventive: Use access broker service that issues ephemeral access tokens with explicit manifest scoping for downloads.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • ObjectStoreAccess logs with principal, object_prefix, operation.
  • KMSAccessLogs showing decrypt requests and principals.
  • Export broker issuance logs with manifest ids and requester identity.
  • SIEM UnauthorizedKeyDecrypt alerts for cross-tenant decrypts.

T7 — Timestamp manipulation from compromised host

STRIDE category: Tampering
Risk: Medium likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Alter source timestamps to reorder events or break sequence integrity.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: compromised event source or ability to control clock/time fields prior to ingest.
  • Initial action: send events with altered timestamps to create confusion in ordering.
  • Core exploitation: create plausible sequences to mask actions or backdate events.
  • Escalation: combine with deletion to make later timeline inconsistent.
  • Impact realization: unreliable timelines and weakened forensic cases.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Require monotonic sequence IDs generated at ingest and use server-side timestamp overlay; reject events with future/old timestamps beyond tolerance.
  • Detective: TimeSyncAlerts when source clock drift exceeds threshold and EventTimestampAnomaly when timestamp jumps exceed baseline.
  • Corrective: Reconstruct timeline using ingest_server_time and sequence ids; mark tampered ranges for investigation.
  • Preventive: Enforce secure, authenticated ingest channels and sign source fingerprints to tie origin to clocks.
  • Preventive: Use monotonic counters per source to validate ordering.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • EventSourceLogs showing source_time, ingest_server_time, sequence_id.
  • TimeSyncAlerts and NTP/chrony metrics for host drift.
  • SIEM EventTimestampAnomaly detection queries comparing server vs source timestamps.
  • Sequence gap detection output from IntegrityVerifierLogs.

Purple-team validation commands

T8 — Backup deletion by ransomware

STRIDE category: Denial of Service
Risk: Medium likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Destroy long-term backups and snapshots, removing recovery options and evidence.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: compromise of backup account credentials or snapshot management plane.
  • Initial action: delete snapshots and purge backup archives.
  • Core exploitation: remove recovery points and historical logs.
  • Escalation: encrypt archives and demand ransom while preventing restore.
  • Impact realization: inability to restore logs and loss of compliance evidence.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Isolate backup accounts with no direct admin network access and use immutable snapshot features with legal hold.
  • Preventive: Use separate credentials for backup lifecycle operations with NO delete rights in day-to-day accounts.
  • Detective: BackupLifecycle logs with delete requests monitored and ArchiveDeleteAnomaly SIEM alerts.
  • Corrective: Maintain air-gapped cold backups and test restore recovery processes regularly.
  • Preventive: Multi-region replication and restricted restore keys with approval workflows.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • BackupLifecycle events with snapshot_id, lock_state, delete_requester.
  • Snapshot immutability logs and legal hold state.
  • SIEM ArchiveDeleteAnomaly with region and account context.
  • Restore test logs proving recovery capability.

STRIDE category: Information Disclosure

How the attacker would exploit it

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

T10 — SIEM parser injection to hide events

STRIDE category: Repudiation
Risk: Medium likelihood, Medium impact
Attacker objective: Construct logs or payloads that cause SIEM parsers to fail or drop alerts, masking malicious events.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: SIEM parser that does brittle extraction or a field that influences parsing.
  • Initial action: send carefully crafted payloads (e.g., huge fields, broken encoding) that trigger parser errors.
  • Core exploitation: SIEM drops or mis-parses events, lowering detection.
  • Escalation: combine with other attacks to remain undetected.
  • Impact realization: reduced detection and slower response.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Harden SIEM parsing with schema validation, safe extraction libraries, and size limits; route parser failures to separate queue for manual triage.
  • Detective: Monitor ParserErrorMetrics and correlate with drop in alerts; create ParserErrorSuppressionPattern rule.
  • Corrective: Fail-safe ingestion path that stores raw events for forensic analysis regardless of parser success.
  • Preventive: Rate-limit dangerously large fields and apply sanitization at ingest.
  • Preventive: Regular fuzzing of parsers and parser regression tests in CI.

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • ParserErrorMetrics and parser exception logs with source and error_count.
  • Raw event archive showing unparsed events for manual review.
  • SIEM ParserErrorSuppressionPattern alert correlated to missing detection alerts.
  • Ingest pipeline metrics showing rerouting to manual triage.

T11 — Break-glass misuse on log admin roles

STRIDE category: Elevation of Privilege
Risk: Medium likelihood, High impact
Attacker objective: Abuse emergency elevation to gain sustained or broad access to log operations and perform tampering.

How the attacker would exploit it

  • Prerequisite: break-glass mechanism with weak controls or lacking session recording.
  • Initial action: request emergency access and perform delete/retention changes.
  • Core exploitation: actions are taken under plausible incident cover and harder to challenge.
  • Escalation: cover up evidence by altering logs and removing traces.
  • Impact realization: privileged misuse with plausible deniability and suppressed alerts.

Blue-team control review — what must stop the chain

  • Preventive: Require ticket binding, short TTL, mandatory session recording, and dual approvers for break-glass operations affecting log integrity.
  • Detective: BreakGlassAudit with requestor, justification, expiry, and real-time SIEM correlation.
  • Corrective: Automated rollback and preservation of pre-change snapshots when break-glass used.
  • Preventive: Limit break-glass scope and require post-incident attestation and review before any retention edits are considered final.
  • Preventive: Enforce independent verification of break-glass requests by a separate team (e.g., legal/compliance).

Evidence and telemetry to validate controls

  • BreakGlassAudit logs, session recordings, and associated ticket IDs.
  • SIEM correlation of break-glass sessions with storage admin actions.
  • Recorded snapshots of partition state taken pre-change.
  • Post-incident attestation logs and review outcomes.
