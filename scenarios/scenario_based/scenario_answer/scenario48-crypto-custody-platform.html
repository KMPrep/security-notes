Scenario 48 — Blockchain / Crypto Custody Platform Security

Scenario: A fintech builds a crypto custody platform holding $500M in digital assets. Private keys are split using Shamir Secret Sharing stored in HSMs across three geographies.

Candidate questions:

  • What is the threat model for a quorum of key holders being simultaneously compromised?
  • How do you model an insider threat who is one of the key custodians?
  • What controls govern the signing ceremony for large transactions?
  • How do you monitor for unauthorized signing requests or policy bypass?
  • What residual risk do you document for cryptographic algorithm deprecation (e.g., ECDSA)?

3.4 Highest-risk boundary crossing

The highest-risk boundary crossing is the moment of transient key reconstruction in the HSM when shares from a quorum of custodians are combined. This is the only point in the entire system where the full private key exists — even briefly. An attacker who has compromised the HSM firmware, controls the HSM network interface, or has achieved RCE on the signing enclave host can capture the key at this moment. All other controls in the system (geographic distribution, SSS, ceremony procedures) protect the path to this moment but cannot prevent the key from existing transiently. This is why HSM firmware integrity, tamper detection, and post-ceremony destruction verification are the most critical technical controls in the entire architecture.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

At Google scale with large numbers of custodial wallets and transaction types, every transaction policy rule must be expressed as formally verified code, not manual procedures. Policy-as-code for transaction velocity limits, destination allowlisting, and quorum requirements must be deployed through a code review pipeline with independent security review. Any policy change must trigger an automated re-verification against the full policy test suite.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A policy engine vulnerability or quorum configuration error at Google scale could expose every wallet in the custody platform simultaneously. Blast radius minimization requires that each high-value wallet vault be governed by an independent policy engine instance, that wallet groups have independent key hierarchies rather than a shared root, and that a single compromise cannot authorize withdrawals across all vaults without separate independent ceremonies.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

Manual out-of-band custodian confirmation via phone call, ad hoc transaction review for large withdrawals, and ceremony scheduling bottlenecks cannot support a high-throughput custody platform. Automate policy enforcement at the HSM layer so that transactions meeting policy are auto-approved below threshold, require human ceremony only above defined risk thresholds, and build an on-call custodian rotation that guarantees quorum availability within defined SLAs.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual ceremony audit review, per-transaction security team sign-off, and ad hoc custodian vetting fail above a few dozen custodians or thousands of daily transactions. Replace with automated ceremony audit verification, continuous behavioral analytics on custodian authentication patterns, and formal custodian onboarding with background investigation requirements codified as policy.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

Use this to open your answer before going deep on any section.

For a crypto custody platform holding $500M with Shamir Secret Sharing across geographically distributed HSMs, the core threat is quorum compromise — whether through coordinated custodian attacks, insider collusion, or HSM supply chain backdoors.

The architecture is inherently sound: geographic distribution means a single data center compromise is insufficient. The residual threat is simultaneous compromise of the quorum, which I model as nation-state level coordination or insider collusion. Controls I'd add beyond the base architecture: enhanced quorum thresholds for large transactions (3-of-5 instead of 2-of-3), mandatory time-delay execution windows with any-custodian cancellation rights, transaction destination allowlisting, and out-of-band confirmation for all high-value ceremonies.

The HSM must never allow the reconstructed key to exist outside its secure enclave boundary — transient reconstruction and immediate post-signing destruction are non-negotiable. Ceremony audit logs must be append-only with cryptographic hash chaining to prevent insider tampering.

Residual risk I'd document: ECDSA deprecation risk from quantum computing requires a PQC migration plan for wallet key material — the most complex long-term residual risk in this architecture.

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: What is the threat model for a quorum of key holders being simultaneously compromised?

The threat is coordinated compromise — nation-state actors recruit, bribe, or coerce the minimum number of custodians required to achieve quorum outside of the legitimate ceremony process. Mitigations: increase the required quorum threshold for large transactions to require more simultaneous compromises, add time-delay execution so compromised ceremonies can be detected and cancelled before funds move, and implement out-of-band custodian confirmation so that even a compromised ceremony platform cannot authorize a transaction without each custodian independently confirming via a separate channel.

Follow-up 2: How do you model insider threat for a key custodian?

Model the insider as a fully authenticated, authorized participant who contributes their share willingly but fraudulently. The insider's advantage is that they bypass all authentication controls. Countermeasures must focus on policy controls that require multiple independent custodians who cannot easily collude, separation of duties ensuring no two custodians work in the same organizational unit or report to the same manager, and behavioral monitoring that detects unusual ceremony patterns like off-hours ceremonies or unusual transaction destinations.

Follow-up 3: What controls govern the signing ceremony for large transactions?

For transactions above a defined dollar threshold: enhanced quorum (3-of-5 instead of 2-of-3), mandatory 24-hour time delay before broadcast with any-custodian cancellation right, transaction destination must be on the approved allowlist, out-of-band confirmation required from each quorum participant via separate authenticated channel, and the full ceremony must be logged to an append-only audit system with cryptographic hash chaining before the transaction is broadcast.

Follow-up 4: How do you monitor for unauthorized signing requests?

Three layers: the policy engine rejects requests that violate transaction rules before they reach the ceremony; the ceremony audit log records every signing event with cryptographic integrity; and blockchain transaction monitoring detects any signed transaction on-chain that does not have a matching ceremony log entry — which would indicate a bypass. Custodian authentication anomaly detection flags authentication from unexpected geographies or devices in real time.

Follow-up 5: What residual risk do you document for ECDSA algorithm deprecation?

ECDSA is vulnerable to quantum computing attacks via Shor's algorithm. The residual risk is that "harvest now, decrypt later" adversaries may be recording blockchain transactions today with the intent to compute private keys once sufficiently powerful quantum computers are available. Mitigation requires a planned migration to post-quantum signature algorithms (CRYSTALS-Dilithium, Falcon) before quantum threat materializes — which requires generating new wallet addresses with PQC keys and migrating all assets, an irreversible on-chain operation that must be planned and tested years in advance.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Treating SSS and HSM geographic distribution as complete solutions without addressing the transient reconstruction moment.
  • Not modeling insider custodian collusion — assuming custodians are unconditionally trustworthy.
  • Omitting time-delay execution windows, which are the most important control for catching authorized-but-fraudulent ceremonies.
  • Failing to implement out-of-band confirmation independent of the ceremony platform itself.
  • Treating blockchain irreversibility only as a recovery problem rather than designing time-delay controls to prevent it.
  • Not having a documented PQC migration plan for wallet key material — ECDSA deprecation is a known future risk.
  • Ignoring physical custodian security — technical controls cannot protect against physical coercion.
  • Storing ceremony audit logs on infrastructure that custodians or administrators can modify — logs must be cryptographically chained and replicated off-site.
