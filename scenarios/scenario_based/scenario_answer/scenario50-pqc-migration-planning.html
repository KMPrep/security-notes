Scenario 50 — Quantum-Safe Migration Planning

Scenario: Your organization is beginning a post-quantum cryptography (PQC) migration. You must identify and prioritize systems using RSA/ECC that are vulnerable to "harvest now, decrypt later."

Candidate questions:

  • What threat actor and timeline assumptions drive the urgency of PQC migration?
  • How do you build a cryptographic inventory across 10,000 systems?
  • What controls govern hybrid classical/PQC deployment to maintain backward compatibility?
  • How do you prioritize which data to protect first based on sensitivity and data longevity?
  • What residual risk do you document for systems that cannot be migrated before the quantum threat materializes?

3.4 Highest-risk boundary crossing

The highest-risk boundary crossing is not a network perimeter but a temporal one: the moment an adversary records encrypted traffic or exfiltrates encrypted data today and stores it for future decryption. Unlike all other threats in this set, this crossing is happening right now — adversaries need no quantum computer today, only storage capacity. By the time a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists, the harvest will already have occurred. This makes data with long confidentiality requirements (healthcare, financial records, government secrets, long-lived PKI hierarchies) immediately at risk regardless of how far away quantum threats appear to be.

8. GOOGLE-SCALE CONSIDERATIONS

8.1 Automation and policy-as-code

At Google scale — 10,000+ systems, millions of TLS connections per second, and thousands of certificates — PQC migration cannot be a manual project. Cryptographic algorithm enforcement must be expressed as policy-as-code: TLS configuration policies that reject classical-only cipher suites after a deadline date, certificate issuance policies that block RSA/ECC certificates after the CA migration wave, and code signing pipeline policies that reject binaries signed without PQC algorithms. All policies must be enforced by infrastructure rather than human compliance review.

8.2 Fleet-wide impact analysis

A delayed PQC migration at Google scale means that every TLS session carrying sensitive data is being harvested today by nation-state actors with long-term storage capacity. The blast radius is not a single system failure but the entire historical communication archive of the organization. For data with 10-20 year confidentiality requirements, migration delay directly translates to future mandatory breach notification obligations when quantum computers become available.

8.3 Eliminating human bottlenecks

Manual system-by-system migration approvals, per-certificate algorithm reviews, and ad hoc vendor PQC readiness assessments cannot scale across 10,000 systems and hundreds of vendors. Build an automated PQC migration platform that: continuously discovers classical cryptography, auto-assigns systems to migration waves based on risk score, tracks completion status, escalates deadline misses automatically, and generates executive risk reports without human data aggregation.

8.4 Controls that break at scale

Manual cryptographic inventory maintenance via spreadsheet, ad hoc vendor certification reviews, and per-system migration scheduling fail above a few hundred systems. Replace with automated cryptographic discovery tools integrated in the infrastructure scanning pipeline, a central cryptographic asset management database with API-driven updates from CI/CD, HSM, and TLS telemetry sources, and a migration governance platform that manages wave assignments, exception workflows, and compliance reporting at fleet scale.

ANSWER SUMMARY — 90-second version

Use this to open your answer before going deep on any section.

Post-quantum cryptography migration is unique among security programs because the threat is temporal rather than technical: the harvest is happening now, but the damage occurs years later when quantum computers can run Shor's algorithm against the RSA and ECC ciphertext already collected.

The threat actor driving urgency is the nation-state intelligence service with long-term storage capacity and quantum computing R&D programs. NIST estimates a cryptographically relevant quantum computer could exist in the 2030–2035 window, but timeline uncertainty is high — this means the migration program must be well advanced now.

My prioritization framework is data longevity times sensitivity: long-lived regulated data — healthcare, financial, legal — must be migrated first because its confidentiality requirement extends into the quantum threat window. Next priority is the PKI trust hierarchy and code signing infrastructure because quantum-broken CA and code signing keys create systemic trust failures, not just data exposure.

The approach is hybrid deployment first — X25519 plus ML-KEM for key exchange, dual-signed certificates with RSA plus ML-DSA — to maintain backward compatibility while eliminating the harvest-now risk. Cryptographic agility architecture ensures algorithm swaps are infrastructure operations, not application re-engineering projects.

LIKELY FOLLOW-UP QUESTIONS & IDEAL ANSWERS

Follow-up 1: What threat actor and timeline assumptions drive the urgency?

The primary driver is nation-state actors with both the capability to harvest encrypted traffic at scale today and the likely resources to achieve quantum computing capability within 10-15 years. NIST's 2024 finalization of FIPS 203, 204, and 205 (ML-KEM, ML-DSA, SLH-DSA) reflects the consensus that migration should begin now. The "harvest now, decrypt later" threat means the migration window is not until quantum computers exist — it is now, for any data whose confidentiality requirement extends into that window.

Follow-up 2: How do you build a cryptographic inventory across 10,000 systems?

Automated discovery tooling (Cryptosense Analyzer, IBM Quantum Safe Explorer, open-source tools like certbot-crypto-audit) scans systems for RSA and ECC usage via TLS handshake analysis, certificate scanning, code signing certificate audits, and API inspection. This is supplemented by developer-facing questionnaires for applications that embed cryptography directly. The inventory is continuous, not point-in-time — new systems must be auto-enrolled via CI/CD pipeline hooks that require cryptographic declaration before deployment.

Follow-up 3: What controls govern hybrid classical/PQC deployment for backward compatibility?

Hybrid key exchange (X25519Kyber768 or X25519MLKEM768 in TLS 1.3) combines ECDH and ML-KEM key shares — both components must be broken to compromise the session. Backward compatibility is maintained because the TLS negotiation falls back to classical if the peer does not support PQC. Dual-algorithm certificates (RSA+ML-DSA signed chains) allow verification by both classical and PQC-capable clients. The hybrid period ends when the classical-only tail is below an acceptable threshold, after which pure PQC is enforced.

Follow-up 4: How do you prioritize which data to protect first?

Prioritize using a two-axis framework: data sensitivity (impact if disclosed) multiplied by data longevity (how long it must remain confidential). The highest priority intersection is long-lived regulated data — genomic data, financial records, classified government information, long-term contractual secrets. Next is the trust infrastructure that protects everything else: PKI root CAs and code signing keys, because their compromise has systemic fleet-wide impact. TLS-in-transit for interactive sessions is important but lower priority because the session data has shorter useful lifetime.

Follow-up 5: What residual risk do you document for systems that cannot be migrated in time?

For systems that cannot be migrated before quantum threshold — embedded systems, mainframes, OT equipment — the residual risk is that any data they have protected or transmitted is at retroactive risk. The documentation must include: the system identifier, the classical algorithm in use, the estimated data sensitivity and longevity, the reason migration is not feasible, compensating controls (minimize what sensitive data transits those systems, accelerate sunset planning), and the explicit risk acceptance with CISO sign-off. This creates the accountability trail required if the risk materializes.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

  • Treating PQC migration as a future problem and not recognizing that the harvest-now threat is active today.
  • Conflating quantum-safe with quantum-resistant — hybrid deployment is the transition state, not the final goal.
  • Prioritizing migration by system count rather than data longevity and sensitivity, which leads to migrating low-risk systems first.
  • Not building cryptographic agility into the architecture — hard-coding algorithm names in application code makes future algorithm transitions extremely expensive.
  • Assuming all HSMs support PQC algorithms — many deployed HSMs require hardware replacement, not just firmware updates.
  • Overlooking the certificate size implications of dual-algorithm chains and PQC signature sizes, which can break TLS handshakes in size-limited environments.
  • Treating the NIST timeline estimate as reliable and not planning for timeline compression due to unexpected quantum computing advances.
  • Not including vendor PQC readiness in the migration critical path — a migrated system connecting to a classical-only vendor endpoint achieves little for that communication channel.
