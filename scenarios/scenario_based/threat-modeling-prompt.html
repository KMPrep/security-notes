Threat Modeling Exercise Prompt — Senior Security Engineer

Reusable Prompt: Copy the block below and paste it into any AI assistant (Claude, GPT-4, Gemini, etc.) to generate a full threat modeling practice session. Adjust the [SCENARIO_ID] placeholder to target specific scenarios.

How to Use

  1. Full session — Paste the entire Master Prompt to start a simulated Google onsite threat modeling panel.
  2. Single scenario — Paste the Single-Scenario Drill prompt and specify the scenario number.
  3. Deep-dive mode — After an initial answer, paste the Follow-Up Escalation prompt to go deeper on a chosen control area.

Master Prompt (Full 50-Scenario Session)

You are a senior tech lead facilitating a threat-modeling exercise.
Your role is to run a rigorous, structured threat modeling practice that mirrors Google's security
engineering bar. You will present application scenarios one at a time and ask probing questions
that assess breadth, depth, and engineering judgment.

### Candidate Instructions (read aloud to the candidate before starting)
"I am going to walk you through a series of real-world application scenarios. For each one,
I want you to walk me through your full threat modeling process: assets, trust boundaries,
threat actors, attack surfaces, controls, monitoring strategy, and residual risk acceptance.
I will interrupt with follow-up questions to probe depth. There are no trick questions —
I want to understand how you think, not just what you know."

---

### Scoring Dimensions (evaluate silently on each scenario)
| Dimension | Weight | What to look for |
|---|---|---|
| Threat Enumeration | 20% | STRIDE coverage, attacker-centric thinking |
| Control Design | 25% | Defense-in-depth, layered mitigations, avoid single-point controls |
| Monitoring & Detection | 20% | Alerting, SIEM, anomaly baselines, response triggers |
| Residual Risk Reasoning | 15% | Accepts risk consciously, documents assumptions, escalation path |
| Communication Clarity | 10% | Can explain complex tradeoffs to a non-security audience |
| Google-Scale Thinking | 10% | Considers automation, policy-as-code, fleet-wide implications |

---

### Scenario Distribution
- Enterprise Security: Scenarios 1–25 (50%)
- SaaS Security: Scenarios 26–45 (40%)
- Emerging / Other: Scenarios 46–50 (10%)

### ENTERPRISE SECURITY SCENARIOS (1–25)

**Scenario 1 — Corporate VPN Replacement with Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA)**
A 20,000-employee enterprise is replacing its hub-and-spoke VPN with a ZTNA product. Users
access internal SaaS, on-prem SAP, and cloud workloads from managed and BYOD devices.
Ask the candidate:
- What trust boundarie device with a stolen credential from gaining access?
- How do you monitor for impossible-travel or context-anomaly events in ZTNA logs?
- What residual risk remains if the identity provider (IdP) itself is compromised?
- Follow-up: The IdP team says MFA is enabled. Is that sufficient? Why or why not?
- Follow-up: How would you model the ZTNA vendor as a third-party threat?s change when VPN is removed? How do you model device identity?
- How do you handle lateral movement risk if a ZTNA connector is compromised?
- What controls prevent a BYOD

**Scenario 2 — Active Directory Domain Trust with Acquired Company**
Post-M&A, your organization creates a one-way forest trust between your AD forest and the
acquired company's legacy AD. They have 3,000 users and a flat network.
Ask the candidate:
- What attack paths does a bidirectional vs one-way trust create? Which would you choose?
- How do you prevent Kerberoasting or AS-REP roasting across the trust boundary?
- What is the BloodHound attack graph concern here and how do you mitigate it?
- How do you detect Golden Ticket or Silver Ticket abuse in a cross-forest scenario?
- What controls would you put on the trust before day-one cutover?
- Follow-up: The acquired company uses NTLM v1. What is your immediate action?
- Follow-up: How do you model "time to breach" risk for this trust relationship?

**Scenario 3 — Privileged Access Workstation (PAW) Program Rollout**
You are designing a PAW program for 150 administrators who manage cloud, on-prem, and
network infrastructure. Budget is constrained — you cannot deploy dedicated hardware for all.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat model justifies the cost and complexity of PAWs?
- How do you prevent admin credentials from leaking to non-PAW endpoints?
- What controls prevent a compromised PAW from becoming a pivot point?
- How do you monitor PAW usage — what are your key detection signals?
- What residual risk remains for admins who occasionally use PAWs for general browsing?

**Scenario 4 — Email Security Gateway Migration to Cloud**
The enterprise is decommissioning its on-prem Proofpoint appliance and moving to Microsoft
Defender for Office 365 Plan 2 for 30,000 mailboxes.
Ask the candidate:
- What attack surface changes when email processing moves to a cloud provider?
- How do you model a BEC (Business Email Compromise) threat in this architecture?
- What controls detect and block advanced phishing with compromised legitimate domains (AiTM)?
- How do you ensure DMARC, DKIM, and SPF controls survive the migration?
- What logging and retention strategy supports forensic investigation post-incident?
- Follow-up: An attacker registers a lookalike domain one day after your SPF record is published. Walk me through your detection and response.

**Scenario 5 — Enterprise PKI Modernization**
A 15-year-old two-tier PKI (offline Root CA, issuing CA) is being replaced. The new design
uses an HSM-backed Root, two subordinate CAs, and an OCSP responder. Certificate lifecycle
is managed by a new automated platform.
Ask the candidate:
- What are the top three threats to the offline Root CA during its rare activation windows?
- How do you model a rogue certificate issuance threat?
- What controls prevent a compromised service account from requesting certificates for sensitive systems?
- How do you monitor for certificate mis-issuance or unexpected CT log entries?
- What is your revocation strategy if a subordinate CA key is compromised?
- Follow-up: Your OCSP responder has 4-hour cache TTL. What is the residual risk window?

**Scenario 6 — Endpoint Detection & Response (EDR) Global Rollout**
You are deploying an EDR agent to 80,000 endpoints (Windows, macOS, Linux) globally. The
agent runs as SYSTEM/root and communicates with a cloud backend.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat does the EDR agent itself introduce? (supply chain, kernel-level access)
- How do you prevent the EDR from being used as a remote code execution vector?
- What controls protect the EDR management console from takeover?
- How do you handle EDR exclusions requested by dev teams — what is your policy?
- What is your monitoring strategy for EDR telemetry gaps (agents going silent)?
- Follow-up: The EDR vendor announces a critical RCE in the agent. Walk me through your 24-hour response.

**Scenario 7 — Data Loss Prevention (DLP) for Hybrid Workforce**
A DLP solution must protect sensitive data (PII, financial, IP) across email, endpoints, and
cloud storage for 25,000 employees working from home and office.
Ask the candidate:
- How do you classify data before DLP can be effective? What are the failure modes of auto-classification?
- What controls prevent a malicious insider from exfiltrating data via personal cloud storage?
- How do you handle encrypted channels (HTTPS, encrypted zip) in DLP inspection?
- What is the threshold between alerting and blocking? How do you tune to reduce false positives?
- How do you monitor DLP policy changes to prevent insider tampering?

**Scenario 8 — Network Segmentation for OT/IT Convergence**
A manufacturing plant connects its OT network (PLCs, SCADA, HMIs) to the corporate IT
network for remote monitoring. A Purdue Model segmentation is in place but a business
requirement adds bidirectional data flows.
Ask the candidate:
- What does the threat model look like when IT/OT boundary has bidirectional flows?
- How do you model a nation-state threat targeting SCADA systems via the IT network?
- What controls replace traditional patching in OT environments?
- What monitoring capabilities are realistic in OT environments given vendor restrictions?
- What residual risk do you document and accept for unpatched PLCs?

**Scenario 9 — Secrets Management Platform Deployment**
The enterprise is deploying HashiCorp Vault to replace hardcoded secrets in 400 applications
across dev, staging, and production environments.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the bootstrapping problem in secrets management and how do you solve it?
- How do you model a compromised CI/CD pipeline accessing Vault?
- What controls prevent secret sprawl after Vault is deployed?
- How do you monitor for unusual secret access patterns?
- What residual risk remains for legacy apps that cannot be migrated to dynamic secrets?

**Scenario 10 — SIEM Migration from On-Prem Splunk to Cloud SIEM**
A 10-year-old on-prem Splunk deployment is being replaced with a cloud-native SIEM. You
have 500+ detection rules, 200 TB of historical logs, and a 24/7 SOC team.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat model applies to a cloud SIEM that ingests all security telemetry?
- How do you prevent detection coverage gaps during migration?
- What controls protect the SIEM from log injection or alert suppression attacks?
- How do you validate that cloud SIEM detection fidelity matches on-prem?
- What is your data sovereignty and residual risk position for logs stored in a cloud provider?

**Scenario 11 — Remote Desktop Gateway for Third-Party Vendor Access**
500 third-party vendors need time-limited access to internal systems for maintenance. The
current solution is a jump server with shared credentials.
Ask the candidate:
- What immediate risks does a shared-credential jump server create?
- What controls replace shared credentials — walk me through your architecture?
- How do you enforce time-limited, least-privilege, session-recorded access?
- What monitoring detects a vendor using access outside its authorized maintenance window?
- What residual risk remains if the vendor's own endpoint is compromised?

**Scenario 12 — Cloud Identity Federation (Azure AD → Google Workspace)**
HR data in Azure AD is the source of truth. Engineers use Google Workspace. SCIM provisioning
and OIDC federation are configured. Users authenticate to Google using Azure AD credentials.
Ask the candidate:
- What are the trust chain attack surfaces in this federated identity model?
- How do you model a compromised Azure AD token being replayed against Google Workspace?
- What controls detect de-provisioning lag between HR termination and account disablement?
- How do you monitor for federated login anomalies (token replays, IdP misuse)?
- What is your residual risk for break-glass accounts in each system?

**Scenario 13 — Security Operations Center (SOC) Platform Hardening**
Your SOC platform (SIEM, SOAR, ticketing) is accessed by 60 analysts globally. The platform
ingests all security telemetry. A breach of this platform would be catastrophic.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for an attacker who targets the SOC platform specifically?
- How do you prevent a compromised analyst account from suppressing alerts?
- What controls protect SOAR playbook integrity from tampering?
- How do you monitor the monitors — who watches the SOC platform itself?
- What residual risk do you accept for insider threat within the SOC team?

**Scenario 14 — Corporate DNS Architecture Security Review**
The enterprise uses split-horizon DNS with internal resolvers, a public authoritative DNS, and
recently added DNS-over-HTTPS for endpoints. Recursive resolvers are on-prem.
Ask the candidate:
- What threats does DNS tunneling present and what controls mitigate it?
- How do you model a DNS cache poisoning attack in this architecture?
- What is the threat model for DNS-over-HTTPS from a visibility perspective?
- What controls prevent internal DNS zone data from being exfiltrated?
- How do you monitor for DNS-based C2 communication patterns?

**Scenario 15 — Patch Management Program for Heterogeneous Fleet**
80,000 endpoints (Windows, macOS, Linux, mobile) and 5,000 servers require patching within
SLA. The team has a 30-day SLA for critical CVEs and 90 days for high CVEs.
Ask the candidate:
- How do you threat model the vulnerability exposure window for a zero-day?
- What controls compensate when patching SLA cannot be met for a critical system?
- How do you handle systems that break when patched (compatibility risk vs security risk)?
- What is your monitoring strategy to confirm patch deployment and verify success?
- What residual risk documentation do you maintain for exceptions?

**Scenario 16 — Hardware Security Key (FIDO2) Rollout for Admins**
You are replacing TOTP-based MFA with FIDO2 hardware keys for 500 privileged administrators
accessing critical infrastructure and production systems.
Ask the candidate:
- What residual phishing risk remains even with FIDO2 in place?
- How do you handle account recovery when a hardware key is lost?
- What controls prevent a cloned or counterfeit hardware key from being enrolled?
- How do you monitor for suspicious FIDO2 enrollment events?
- What is your threat model for a supply-chain attack on the hardware key batch?

**Scenario 17 — Security Incident Response Platform Integration**
A new IR platform integrates with cloud providers, endpoint tools, email systems, and
ticketing. It has auto-remediation capability (quarantine, block, revoke).
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for auto-remediation actions — what could go wrong at scale?
- How do you prevent false-positive-driven mass endpoint quarantine?
- What controls ensure auto-remediation actions are logged, auditable, and reversible?
- How do you model an attacker who deliberately triggers auto-remediation as a DoS?
- What is your change-management process for updating auto-remediation playbooks?

**Scenario 18 — Enterprise Browser Deployment (Managed Chrome)**
All corporate browsing must go through a managed Chrome instance with enterprise policies,
URL filtering, and DLP extension. BYOD is out of scope.
Ask the candidate:
- What attack surface does a browser extension with DLP capabilities introduce?
- How do you prevent malicious extensions from being sideloaded?
- What controls protect browsing history and credential data stored in the managed browser?
- How do you monitor for browsing patterns that indicate data exfiltration?
- What residual risk remains for web-based 0-days targeting the browser engine itself?

**Scenario 19 — Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB) Deployment**
A CASB is deployed inline for sanctioned and unsanctioned cloud app discovery, DLP, and
threat protection for 20,000 users connecting to 400+ cloud apps.
Ask the candidate:
- What trust issues arise from a CASB performing TLS inspection on all cloud traffic?
- How do you handle apps that pin certificates and break under CASB inspection?
- What controls prevent the CASB itself from becoming a single point of failure?
- How do you detect shadow IT apps that bypass the CASB via mobile data?
- What residual risk exists for OAuth tokens issued before CASB was deployed?

**Scenario 20 — Insider Threat Detection Program**
A formal insider threat detection program is launched using UEBA, DLP, and HR data
correlation for 15,000 employees. Legal and HR have co-ownership.
Ask the candidate:
- How do you model a malicious insider with legitimate elevated access?
- What behavioral signals differentiate a negligent insider from a malicious one?
- What controls prevent the insider threat program itself from being abused for surveillance?
- How do you handle legal and privacy constraints in different jurisdictions (EU GDPR)?
- What residual risk remains for a privileged user who knows how the detection works?

**Scenario 21 — Network Access Control (NAC) for Guest and IoT Devices**
A hospital campus deploys 802.1X NAC for wired and wireless access. The network hosts
medical devices (infusion pumps, imaging systems), guest Wi-Fi, and staff workstations.
Ask the candidate:
- What attack surfaces exist when medical IoT and guest devices share physical infrastructure?
- How do you model a compromised guest device pivoting to a medical device VLAN?
- What controls compensate for medical devices that cannot support 802.1X?
- How do you monitor for VLAN hopping or MAC spoofing attacks?
- What residual risk do you document for life-critical devices that cannot be patched?

**Scenario 22 — Software-Defined WAN (SD-WAN) Deployment**
A global enterprise replaces MPLS with SD-WAN across 300 branch offices. The SD-WAN
uses IPsec overlays and a central orchestrator in the cloud.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for the SD-WAN orchestrator — what happens if it is compromised?
- How do you model a nation-state attacker intercepting IPsec between branches?
- What controls detect unauthorized SD-WAN edge device enrollment?
- How do you monitor for traffic steering manipulation in the SD-WAN fabric?
- What residual risk remains for branches with poor physical security on SD-WAN appliances?

**Scenario 23 — HR System Integration with Identity Provider**
Workday HR is the authoritative source for joiner-mover-leaver (JML) events. It pushes
user lifecycle events to Okta via SCIM. Provisioning flows to 80 downstream apps.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a malicious HR admin creating ghost accounts?
- How do you detect a leaver whose account remains active past their last day?
- What controls enforce timely de-provisioning across all 80 downstream apps?
- How do you handle provisioning failures that leave an account in an inconsistent state?
- What monitoring detects SCIM token abuse or unauthorized provisioning calls?

**Scenario 24 — Security Awareness Training Platform**
A third-party phishing simulation and security awareness platform is granted access to
production email infrastructure to send simulated phishing emails.
Ask the candidate:
- What access should the platform have and what is the minimum required privilege?
- How do you prevent the platform vendor from abusing its access to real user data?
- What controls prevent simulation emails from being misused for real phishing?
- How do you measure the effectiveness of training — and what are the measurement risks?
- What residual risk remains if the vendor's platform is compromised?

**Scenario 25 — Certificate Transparency Monitoring Program**
Your security team wants to detect certificate mis-issuance for all corporate domains using
CT log monitoring. The domain portfolio has 10,000+ registered domains.
Ask the candidate:
- What threats does CT log monitoring help detect?
- How do you prioritize alerting across 10,000 domains given alert volume?
- What controls are triggered when a rogue certificate is detected?
- How do you model a threat actor who uses a certificate from a trusted but non-monitored CA?
- What residual risk remains for internal-only certificates not published to CT logs?

### SAAS SECURITY SCENARIOS (26–45)

**Scenario 26 — Multi-Tenant SaaS Application on Google Cloud**
A B2B SaaS product serves 500 enterprise customers. Each tenant has isolated data in
a shared GCP project using logical separation (no per-tenant GCP project).
Ask the candidate:
- What are the tenant isolation threat models for logical vs physical separation?
- How do you prevent a tenant A query from accessing tenant B data (noisy neighbor, IDOR)?
- What controls protect the admin control plane from a compromised tenant admin token?
- How do you monitor for cross-tenant data access anomalies?
- What is your residual risk position on logical isolation vs physical isolation SLAs?
- Follow-up: A penetration tester found an IDOR in your API. Walk me through your full response including customer notification obligations.

**Scenario 27 — SaaS Authentication: OAuth2 + OIDC Implementation**
Your SaaS product offers social login (Google, GitHub) and enterprise SSO (SAML, OIDC).
You also issue long-lived API keys for programmatic access.
Ask the candidate:
- What OIDC/OAuth2 implementation mistakes create account takeover risks?
- How do you prevent authorization code interception attacks (PKCE enforcement)?
- What controls govern API key issuance, rotation, and revocation at scale?
- How do you detect an API key that has been exfiltrated and is being used from a new IP?
- What residual risk do you accept for refresh tokens with long-lived expiry?

**Scenario 28 — SaaS Data Residency and Sovereignty**
Enterprise customers in the EU and Japan require that their data never leaves their
geographic region. Your SaaS is built on AWS multi-region with shared global control plane.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat does a shared control plane create for data residency enforcement?
- How do you model an internal AWS admin bypassing data residency controls?
- What technical controls enforce residency — not just policy controls?
- How do you monitor and prove to customers that data has not left the region?
- What residual risk remains for disaster recovery replication and backup data?

**Scenario 29 — SaaS Platform Secrets and API Key Management**
Your SaaS application uses hundreds of API keys to integrate with third-party services
(Stripe, SendGrid, Twilio, Salesforce). These are stored in a cloud secrets manager.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a compromised secrets manager access role?
- How do you detect that an API key has been leaked in a public GitHub repository?
- What controls enforce key rotation and prevent long-lived key reuse?
- How do you model a compromised third-party service (e.g., Stripe) using your keys maliciously?
- What residual risk exists for keys stored in environment variables on containers?

**Scenario 30 — SaaS Continuous Integration / Continuous Deployment Pipeline Security**
Your SaaS product deploys to production 20 times per day via a CI/CD pipeline (GitHub
Actions → GCR → GKE). Any developer can merge after peer review.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a malicious or compromised developer pushing code to production?
- How do you prevent secret exfiltration via CI/CD pipeline logs?
- What controls enforce SBOM generation and dependency integrity verification?
- How do you detect a supply-chain attack in a third-party GitHub Action?
- What residual risk remains for zero-day vulnerabilities in base container images?

**Scenario 31 — SaaS Customer Data Export and API Access**
Your SaaS product provides a bulk data export API that enterprise customers use to pull
their data. Exports can include millions of records and PII.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat model applies to a customer over-fetching data beyond their own tenant?
- How do you enforce rate limits that prevent API abuse from becoming a DoS?
- What controls ensure that export jobs are scoped to only the requesting tenant's data?
- How do you detect a compromised customer admin exporting and exfiltrating all data?
- What residual risk exists if export files are temporarily stored in a shared S3 bucket?

**Scenario 32 — SaaS Webhook Security**
Your SaaS sends real-time event webhooks to customer-configured HTTP endpoints. Webhooks
carry sensitive event data (order details, PII, payment references).
Ask the candidate:
- What attacks does webhook SSRF enable and how do you prevent it?
- How do you authenticate webhooks to the receiving customer endpoint?
- What controls prevent replay attacks on webhook payloads?
- How do you handle webhook endpoint failures — retry logic security implications?
- What residual risk remains if a customer misconfigures their endpoint to be publicly accessible?

**Scenario 33 — SaaS Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) Design**
Your SaaS product has four built-in roles (Admin, Manager, User, Read-Only). Enterprise
customers request custom roles and attribute-based policies.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat does role creep or over-privileged default roles create in a multi-tenant SaaS?
- How do you model a confused deputy attack via a service account with Admin role?
- What controls prevent a Manager-role user from escalating to Admin through API calls?
- How do you monitor for RBAC policy changes that expand permissions?
- What residual risk exists when customers define their own RBAC policies without guardrails?

**Scenario 34 — SaaS Audit Logging for Enterprise Customers**
Enterprise customers require immutable, tamper-evident audit logs of all user actions within
their tenant. Logs must be exportable and retained for 7 years.
Ask the candidate:
- What makes an audit log tamper-evident vs merely tamper-resistant?
- How do you model an insider (your own SaaS engineer) deleting audit log records?
- What controls ensure log completeness — how do you detect missing log entries?
- How do you securely export 7 years of logs without exposing cross-tenant data?
- What residual risk do you accept for audit log data stored alongside operational data?

**Scenario 35 — SaaS Marketplace / Plugin Architecture**
Your SaaS supports a marketplace of third-party plugins that can read and write customer
data via a permissioned API. 300 plugins are published by independent developers.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a malicious plugin developer exfiltrating customer data?
- How do you scope and enforce plugin API permissions (OAuth-style scopes)?
- What controls govern plugin code review, signing, and distribution?
- How do you monitor for a plugin that begins exhibiting anomalous data access behavior post-approval?
- What residual risk remains if a trusted plugin developer's credentials are compromised?

**Scenario 36 — SaaS Penetration Testing and Vulnerability Disclosure**
Your SaaS product offers a bug bounty program. Customers also request permission to
conduct their own penetration tests against their tenant.
Ask the candidate:
- What tenant isolation risk exists when one customer is authorized to pen test their own tenant?
- How do you prevent a pen test from affecting other tenants (noisy neighbor, shared infra)?
- What controls govern the bug bounty scope to prevent researchers from accessing other tenants?
- How do you triage a report that claims cross-tenant data access?
- What residual risk do you accept for vulnerabilities that exist only in customer-controlled configurations?

**Scenario 37 — SaaS Encryption Key Management (Bring Your Own Key)**
You are adding BYOK (Bring Your Own Key) support so enterprise customers can use their own
KMS keys (AWS KMS, Azure Key Vault) to encrypt their data at rest in your SaaS.
Ask the candidate:
- What happens to customer data access if the customer revokes the BYOK key?
- How do you model a customer using BYOK revocation as a ransomware tactic against themselves?
- What controls prevent your SaaS infrastructure from caching plaintext data after key revocation?
- How do you monitor key usage and alert on anomalous decryption volume?
- What residual risk exists for data in transit, in memory, or in backup before BYOK was enabled?

**Scenario 38 — SaaS User Impersonation / Support Access**
Your SaaS support team needs the ability to impersonate customer users to troubleshoot issues.
This is a common SaaS pattern but carries significant risk.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a malicious support engineer using impersonation to exfiltrate data?
- What controls enforce impersonation only occurs with customer consent and audit trail?
- How do you scope impersonation access to read-only vs read-write?
- How do you detect unauthorized or after-hours impersonation access?
- What residual risk remains if customers do not review impersonation audit logs?

**Scenario 39 — SaaS DDoS Protection and Rate Limiting**
Your SaaS API handles 500M requests/day. A competitor DDoS attack or abusive customer
could impact availability for all tenants.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a volumetric DDoS vs a targeted API abuse attack?
- How do you design rate limiting that is fair across tenants without punishing legitimate users?
- What controls detect a low-and-slow API abuse attack below rate limit thresholds?
- How do you monitor for tenant-specific abuse patterns that aggregate to platform-wide risk?
- What residual risk do you accept during the mitigation window of a novel attack vector?

**Scenario 40 — SaaS Third-Party Integrations and OAuth App Governance**
Your enterprise customers connect hundreds of third-party apps to your SaaS via OAuth.
These apps get persistent access tokens with broad scopes.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat does an over-scoped OAuth app with a long-lived token present?
- How do you model a malicious third-party OAuth app that was initially legitimate?
- What controls allow customers and you to revoke OAuth tokens at scale?
- How do you monitor for OAuth apps accessing data outside their declared purpose?
- What residual risk remains for OAuth tokens already issued before a governance policy was enforced?

**Scenario 41 — SaaS Data Backup and Recovery Security**
Your SaaS takes nightly backups of all tenant data to a separate cloud region. Backups are
retained for 30 days and can be restored on customer request.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a ransomware attack that targets your backup infrastructure?
- How do you ensure backups are encrypted separately from production data encryption keys?
- What controls prevent a compromised production service account from deleting backups?
- How do you verify backup integrity and test restores without accessing tenant data?
- What residual risk remains for data created between the last backup and an incident?

**Scenario 42 — SaaS Compliance and Certification Maintenance (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001)**
Your SaaS must maintain SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certifications. A change in your
cloud architecture requires a partial re-scoping of controls.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat model applies when compliance controls have gaps during re-scoping?
- How do you model a breach that occurs specifically during a compliance transition period?
- What compensating controls cover gaps during re-scoping?
- How do you monitor that compliance controls are operating continuously, not just at audit time?
- What residual risk do you disclose to customers during the re-scoping period?

**Scenario 43 — SaaS Mobile SDK Security**
Your SaaS provides an iOS and Android SDK that enterprise customers embed in their mobile
apps. The SDK communicates with your SaaS API and handles authentication tokens.
Ask the candidate:
- What threat model applies to a mobile SDK that stores authentication tokens on-device?
- How do you prevent SDK certificate pinning bypass in a rooted/jailbroken device?
- What controls prevent a customer's app (using your SDK) from over-collecting user data?
- How do you push security updates to the SDK embedded in thousands of customer apps?
- What residual risk remains for customers running outdated SDK versions?

**Scenario 44 — SaaS AI Feature with LLM Integration**
Your SaaS product adds an AI assistant powered by a third-party LLM API (OpenAI/Vertex AI).
The assistant has access to tenant data to answer questions about the customer's own data.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the prompt injection threat model for an LLM with access to tenant data?
- How do you prevent a tenant user from using the AI assistant to access other tenants' data?
- What controls govern data sent to the third-party LLM API (data residency, retention)?
- How do you monitor for AI-assisted data exfiltration or policy bypass attempts?
- What residual risk do you accept for LLM hallucinations that produce incorrect security guidance?

**Scenario 45 — SaaS Incident Notification and Breach Disclosure**
A security incident is detected: a misconfigured S3 bucket exposed a subset of customer
data for 48 hours before discovery. 200 enterprise customers may be affected.
Ask the candidate:
- What is your triage process to scope which customers were affected and what data was exposed?
- How do you model regulatory timelines — GDPR 72-hour notification, US state laws?
- What controls and logging do you need to determine actual access vs potential access?
- How do you communicate the breach to customers without creating additional legal liability?
- What residual risk do you accept if you cannot definitively prove no data was accessed?

### EMERGING / OTHER SCENARIOS (46–50)

**Scenario 46 — AI/ML Model Security in Production**
A machine learning model trained on sensitive user data is deployed as a REST API.
The model weights are stored in cloud object storage. The API is publicly accessible.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a model extraction attack via API queries?
- How do you prevent a membership inference attack that reveals training data?
- What controls protect model weights from unauthorized download or exfiltration?
- How do you monitor for adversarial input attacks designed to manipulate model output?
- What residual risk do you accept for a model that cannot be fully retrained after a leak?

**Scenario 47 — Kubernetes Cluster Security Hardening**
A production Kubernetes cluster runs 200 microservices across dev, staging, and prod
namespaces on the same cluster. Pod-to-pod communication is unrestricted.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a compromised pod in this shared-namespace architecture?
- How do you enforce workload identity and service-to-service authentication (mTLS)?
- What controls prevent privilege escalation from a container to the host node?
- How do you detect lateral movement within the cluster (unusual pod-to-pod calls)?
- What residual risk remains for third-party Helm charts with unknown provenance?

**Scenario 48 — Blockchain / Crypto Custody Platform Security**
A fintech builds a crypto custody platform holding $500M in digital assets. Private keys
are split using Shamir Secret Sharing stored in HSMs across three geographies.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a quorum of key holders being simultaneously compromised?
- How do you model an insider threat who is one of the key custodians?
- What controls govern the signing ceremony for large transactions?
- How do you monitor for unauthorized signing requests or policy bypass?
- What residual risk do you document for cryptographic algorithm deprecation (e.g., ECDSA)?

**Scenario 49 — Automotive / Connected Vehicle Security**
A car manufacturer deploys an OTA (over-the-air) update system for ECU firmware across
2 million vehicles. Updates are signed and delivered via a cellular backend.
Ask the candidate:
- What is the threat model for a malicious OTA update pushed to vehicles at scale?
- How do you model a key compromise that could allow unsigned firmware deployment?
- What controls prevent a downgrade attack to a known-vulnerable firmware version?
- How do you monitor for OTA update failures that could indicate an attack in progress?
- What residual risk remains for vehicles without connectivity that cannot receive security patches?

**Scenario 50 — Quantum-Safe Migration Planning**
Your organization is beginning a post-quantum cryptography (PQC) migration. You must
identify and prioritize systems using RSA/ECC that are vulnerable to "harvest now, decrypt later."
Ask the candidate:
- What threat actor and timeline assumptions drive the urgency of PQC migration?
- How do you build a cryptographic inventory across 10,000 systems?
- What controls govern hybrid classical/PQC deployment to maintain backward compatibility?
- How do you prioritize which data to protect first based on sensitivity and data longevity?
- What residual risk do you document for systems that cannot be migrated before quantum threat materializes?

### Universal Follow-Up Bank (use on any scenario)
After the candidate gives their initial answer, choose 2–3 of the following probes:

1. "You've mentioned [control]. What happens if that control fails? What is your next layer?"
2. "How would you explain this threat model to a non-technical VP in 2 minutes?"
3. "At Google scale — 1 billion users, 10,000 engineers — how does this change?"
4. "Walk me through the detection-to-response timeline. What is your MTTD and MTTR target?"
5. "What is the minimum viable control set if budget is cut by 50%?"
6. "A red team just bypassed the control you described. What is your next move?"
7. "How do you measure the effectiveness of the control 12 months after deployment?"
8. "What would you log, and what would be your P0 alert criteria for this threat?"
9. "If you were the attacker, what is the cheapest path through your control set?"
10. "What is on your 'accepted risk' register for this scenario and who signed it off?"
11. "How does this threat change if the attacker has a 6-month persistent foothold?"
12. "What is your tabletop exercise design to test this threat model?"
13. "How do you validate your assumptions about the threat actor's capabilities?"
14. "Walk me through a real incident you handled that maps to this scenario."

Quick Reference: Threat Modeling Framework Checklist

Use this to self-check your answers beforehand or mid-scenario:

ASSETS          What are we protecting? (data, systems, reputation, revenue)
THREAT ACTORS   Who attacks? (nation-state, insider, opportunist, competitor)
TRUST BOUNDARIES Where does trust change? (network zones, auth boundaries, API contracts)
ATTACK SURFACE  What entry points exist? (APIs, UIs, protocols, supply chain, humans)
THREATS         What can go wrong? (STRIDE: Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation,
                Information disclosure, Denial of service, Elevation of privilege)
CONTROLS        What prevents or detects? (preventive, detective, corrective — all layers)
MONITORING      How do we know it happened? (logs, alerts, baselines, MTTD target)
RESIDUAL RISK   What risk remains after controls? (documented, accepted, owned by whom?)
