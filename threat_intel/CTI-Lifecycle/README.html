The CTI Lifecycle

Category: Cyber Threat Intelligence — Process & Tradecraft
Key standards / frameworks: Traditional Intelligence Cycle, US Intelligence Community Directive 203 (Analytic Standards), Richards Heuer Psychology of Intelligence Analysis, Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH), Admiralty/NATO source reliability grading, MITRE ATT&CK

Overview

The CTI Lifecycle adapts the classic government intelligence cycle to cyber defense. It turns a consumer's question into finished, decision-ready intelligence through a repeatable sequence of phases. Its central discipline is that production is pulled by requirements, not pushed by whatever data happens to be available. Without a requirement, a team drifts into collecting indicators for their own sake and producing reports nobody reads.

The lifecycle is a loop, not a line. Feedback from the consumer reshapes the next round of direction, and analysts frequently backtrack, for example returning to collection when analysis reveals a gap. Treating it as a rigid waterfall is one of the most common ways CTI programs fail.

How It Works

The Six Phases

Direction establishes what the consumer needs to decide and captures it as intelligence requirements. Collection gathers raw data against those requirements from many sources. Processing normalizes, deduplicates, translates, and enriches that data into an analyzable form. Analysis applies structured techniques to turn processed data into assessments with confidence levels. Dissemination delivers the product to the right consumer in the right format and cadence. Feedback captures whether the product actually helped, closing the loop back to direction.

Requirements and PIRs

Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs) are the backbone. A well-formed PIR is specific, decision-linked, and answerable, for example "Which ransomware affiliates are actively exploiting internet-facing VPN appliances in our sector this quarter." PIRs are decomposed into more granular Essential Elements of Information that guide concrete collection tasking.

    DIR[Direction and PIRs] --> COL[Collection]
    COL --> PROC[Processing and Enrichment]
    PROC --> ANA[Analysis]
    ANA --> DIS[Dissemination]
    DIS --> FB[Feedback]
    FB --> DIR
    ANA --> COL

Collection Sources

Collection spans OSINT (open-source reporting, vendor blogs, social media, certificate transparency logs), internal telemetry (EDR, DNS, proxy, and SIEM logs, which are often the highest-value and most underused source), HUMINT (analyst relationships, trust groups, and vetted forums), and dark web monitoring of leak sites, access broker markets, and criminal forums. Sources should be graded for reliability and credibility using a scheme such as the Admiralty code so that a single unverified claim does not silently drive a high-confidence assessment.

From Data to Finished Intel

Finished intelligence carries an explicit assessment, a stated confidence level, the evidence behind it, and a clear recommendation. Structured Analytic Techniques such as Analysis of Competing Hypotheses force analysts to test multiple explanations against the evidence rather than anchoring on the first plausible story. ICD 203 tradecraft standards, describing sourcing, expressing uncertainty consistently, and distinguishing analysis from assumption, keep the product defensible.

Key Takeaways

  • The lifecycle is requirement-driven: no PIR means no purposeful collection and no measurable value.
  • It is a loop with backtracking, not a waterfall; feedback continuously reshapes direction.
  • Internal telemetry is often the richest and most underexploited collection source.
  • Grade sources for reliability and credibility so weak inputs cannot masquerade as strong conclusions.
  • Structured Analytic Techniques such as ACH counter confirmation bias and unfalsifiable claims.
  • Finished intelligence states an assessment, its confidence, its evidence, and a recommendation.
  • Match dissemination format and cadence to the consumer or the product will be ignored.
