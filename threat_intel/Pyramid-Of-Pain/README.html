The Pyramid of Pain

Category: Cyber Threat Intelligence — Detection Prioritization
Key standards / frameworks: Pyramid of Pain (David Bianco, 2013), MITRE ATT&CK, Detection Maturity Model, Sigma and YARA detection standards

Overview

The Pyramid of Pain, introduced by David Bianco, ranks the types of indicators a defender can detect and act on by how much pain denying them causes the adversary. It is a prioritization heuristic: not all indicators are created equal, and the effort to detect a given indicator type should be weighed against how hard it is for the adversary to change it.

From bottom to top the levels are hash values, IP addresses, domain names, network and host artifacts, tools, and finally TTPs. The lower levels are trivial for an adversary to alter and therefore give the defender only fleeting advantage. The higher levels strike at the adversary's actual behavior and tradecraft, forcing costly retooling and relearning.

How It Works

The Six Levels

Hash values uniquely identify a file but change with a single flipped byte. IP addresses are cheaply rotated through hosting providers and proxies. Domain names cost a little more and take time to register and propagate. Network and host artifacts, such as distinctive URI patterns, user-agent strings, registry keys, or named pipes, force the adversary to modify their tooling to evade. Tools represent the adversary's utilities themselves, and denying them forces a search for or development of replacements. TTPs describe how the adversary operates and are the most expensive of all to change.

flowchart TD
    TTP[TTPs Tough] --> TOOLS[Tools Challenging]
    TOOLS --> ARTIFACT[Network and Host Artifacts Annoying]
    ARTIFACT --> DOMAIN[Domain Names Simple]
    DOMAIN --> IP[IP Addresses Easy]
    IP --> HASH[Hash Values Trivial]

Cost and Pain Per Level

The vertical axis is adversary cost. Blocking a hash inconveniences the adversary for the seconds it takes to recompile. Blocking a family of host artifacts, by contrast, may force them to re-engineer their malware. Reliably detecting a TTP such as credential dumping from LSASS memory can invalidate an entire class of the adversary's techniques regardless of which binary, IP, or domain they use next.

Why TTP Detection Is Durable

IOC-based detection is reactive and perishable: indicators are known only after an incident, and adversaries rotate them constantly. TTP-based detection, expressed through behavioral analytics and mapped to ATT&amp;CK, targets the small set of techniques an adversary must use to achieve their goals. These are far harder to change, so a single robust behavioral detection outlives thousands of expired atomic indicators. The trade-off is that higher-level detection is harder to build and tune, demanding richer telemetry and detection engineering maturity.

Key Takeaways

  • Indicators are ranked by the pain that denying them inflicts on the adversary, not by how easy they are to collect.
  • Hashes, IPs, and domains sit at the bottom because adversaries rotate them cheaply and quickly.
  • Artifacts, tools, and TTPs sit at the top because changing them forces costly adversary retooling.
  • TTP-based detection is durable and outlives thousands of perishable atomic indicators.
  • Climbing the pyramid demands richer telemetry and greater detection engineering maturity.
  • Do not abandon the base entirely: cheap high-confidence IOC blocks still add value as a layer.
  • Map higher-level detections to ATT&amp;CK so coverage can be measured and communicated.
